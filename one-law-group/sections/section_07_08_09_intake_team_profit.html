Intake Assessment

One Law Group has a contact form on every page and a 4-person team with a case manager in place — but without a confirmed intake protocol, 24/7 coverage, or a documented follow-up sequence, the firm is almost certainly losing high-value PI cases that are going to competitors who respond faster. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No live chat or after-hours instant contact option is observed on the website — PI prospects who are injured often search at unusual hours and need immediate engagement before they call a competitor
  • No documented intake sequence or defined follow-up protocol is visible externally — without a system, response speed depends on whoever is available when a lead comes in
  • With only a case manager, paralegal, and admin on a 4-person team, intake responsibilities likely overlap with active case management — creating coverage gaps when the team is occupied on existing matters
  • Emanuel is the primary face of the firm and likely the escalation point for complex intake conversations — pulling him away from the litigation and rainmaking that drive the firm's revenue
Blind Spot: Personal injury leads have a very short decision window — accident victims commonly call 3–5 firms in the first 24 hours and sign with whoever responds first and most confidently. Every hour without a response is an opportunity for Omega Law Group or Beverly Hills Injury Firm — located in the same building — to sign a case that contacted One Law Group first.
The Positive
  • Contact forms are present across multiple pages (main content area and footer) — the infrastructure for lead capture is in place; the gap is in follow-up speed and consistency, not in the ability to receive inquiries
The Gap
  • The fastest-responding firm in the Beverly Hills PI market wins the case — without 24/7 coverage and a sub-5-minute response standard, One Law Group is losing cases to competitors who pick up immediately, even when those competitors are less qualified than Emanuel
  • Without intake metrics (form submission rates, call answer rates, time to first response), Emanuel has no visibility into how many leads are being lost before they ever become clients — this is a revenue leak with no visible alarm
  • At One Law Group's average case value, even 2–3 unconverted leads per month due to slow follow-up represents a significant and entirely preventable revenue loss
Fixing intake means Emanuel's reputation generates more signed clients per referral — which compounds his earnings without requiring him to work more hours or spend more on marketing.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact forms appear in multiple locations — main content area and footer — providing consistent access points across the site, which is a solid foundation for lead capture
  • No live chat or instant messaging option is observed on the site — an important gap for PI prospects searching after business hours when no one is available to answer the phone
  • Form fields include Name, Email, Phone, and Case Description with CAPTCHA — functional but not optimized for conversion; no urgency language, no social proof adjacent to the form, and no immediate confirmation response to set expectations for follow-up timing

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

Emanuel Abrishami built One Law Group from nothing to a $2.4M personal injury firm while personally managing payroll, accounting, HR, admin, and client matters — a workload of 50 to 70 hours per week that leaves only 5 hours available for the rainmaking that would actually accelerate growth toward $10M. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Emanuel personally handles payroll, accounting, and HR — core operational functions that have nothing to do with his value as a litigator or rainmaker, and everything to do with why he is working 50–70 hours per week
  • With only 5 hours per week available for business development, One Law Group's growth ceiling is Emanuel's networking capacity — not market demand, not the quality of his work, not the size of the opportunity
  • No operations manager or management layer exists — the case manager, paralegal, and admin report directly to Emanuel without a buffer, meaning every operational decision flows through the attorney
  • The path from $3M to $10M requires organizational infrastructure — dedicated intake management, operations oversight, and financial management — that does not currently exist in a 4-person team managed by the principal attorney
Blind Spot: The most dangerous version of this pattern is that Emanuel is so capable that nothing visibly breaks — the referrals keep coming and the cases keep closing. This creates an illusion of sustainability while the firm is quietly hitting a ceiling that only becomes visible when something slips, someone leaves, or Emanuel gets sick. The ceiling is real; it is just not alarming yet.
The Positive
  • A 4-person operational team (case manager, paralegal, admin) is already in place — One Law Group is not starting from zero; the next hire is a manager, not the first employee
The Gap
  • Every hour Emanuel spends on payroll and accounting is an hour he is not in front of a referring physician, attorney, or potential client — at One Law Group's average case fee, his time has a measurable and significant dollar cost
  • A firm built around one person's personal capacity will plateau at that person's maximum output — growth beyond the current level requires systems and management that exist independently of Emanuel
  • Without a self-managing team, Emanuel cannot take vacation, cannot get sick, and cannot step back — the firm stops when he stops, which means his freedom is permanently deferred regardless of revenue
A self-managing team is not just an operational upgrade — it is the structural prerequisite for Emanuel's $10M goal, and the thing that returns him to the work he is actually exceptional at: winning cases and building the reputation that makes One Law Group worth $10M.

Profit Plan Assessment

One Law Group generated $2.4M in 2025 — a remarkable achievement for a boutique Beverly Hills PI firm — but Emanuel personally manages accounting and payroll, suggesting financial planning happens reactively rather than by design. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Emanuel personally handles accounting and payroll — suggesting no dedicated financial management, CFO-level oversight, or financial operating cadence that would give him clarity on margins, cost per case, and overhead trends
  • $600K of 2025 revenue was collected in 2026 — a cash flow timing complexity that, without deliberate planning, creates unpredictable months and spending decisions made without full financial context
  • No visibility into cost per case or profitability by case type — making it impossible to know whether the most time-consuming cases are also the most profitable, or whether One Law Group is optimizing for volume over margin
  • At $3M+ projected for 2026, without a profit plan in place, rising overhead, payroll, and operational complexity can easily consume revenue growth — more revenue without more planning can mean the same or lower take-home percentage
Blind Spot: PI contingency revenue is inherently lumpy — a single large settlement can dramatically change annual numbers. Without financial planning infrastructure, it is easy to mistake one landmark case for sustainable profitability. Knowing the difference requires a plan, not just a bank balance.
The Positive
  • Revenue trajectory is genuinely exceptional — $1.6M in 2024, $2.4M in 2025, $3M+ projected in 2026 — proving the firm is fundamentally strong and that the underlying economics of One Law Group's PI practice are sound
The Gap
  • Without cost-per-case analysis, One Law Group cannot tell whether its most time-consuming matters are its most profitable — and may be working harder on lower-margin cases while more profitable case types go under-resourced
  • Without deliberate profit planning, more revenue simply creates more complexity — payroll grows, overhead rises, and the percentage Emanuel keeps can stagnate or shrink even as the top line climbs
  • The path to $10M requires knowing exactly what the margin looks like at each stage — otherwise the firm could reach $10M in revenue and still have Emanuel working the same hours for the same or lower take-home pay
More revenue without a profit plan is just more complexity — Emanuel needs to know that $10M in revenue means $10M working toward his freedom, not $10M absorbed by overhead and administrative chaos.
