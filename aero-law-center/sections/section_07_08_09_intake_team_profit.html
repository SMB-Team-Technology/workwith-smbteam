Intake Assessment

The clearest signal on intake comes from the firm's own PPC history: of roughly 140 leads per month, only 25–30 were qualified and just 12–15 were retained — a 9–11% lead-to-retained rate that suggests real leakage between "lead" and "client." Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • The homepage contact form is buried at the bottom of the page inside the "Free Consultation" section — not visible to a prospect who doesn't scroll all the way down
  • No live chat is present, and no after-hours coverage was described on the call — a national remote-team firm's leads can arrive at any hour, from any time zone
  • Historical PPC conversion data shows roughly 75% of leads from one paid channel were judged "junk quality," with no described process for triaging or re-engaging the qualified 25–30% that didn't close immediately
  • No defined follow-up sequence for leads who do not sign immediately was described on the call
Blind Spot: A 9–11% lead-to-retained rate on paid leads was read internally as evidence that PPC "doesn't work" — but the same data shows roughly 45–50% of qualified leads did convert. The gap may be as much about what happens after the lead arrives as it is about the channel that produced it.
The Gap
  • Every lead lost to a buried contact form or a missed after-hours call is a lead the firm already has the reputation and reviews to have won
  • Improving lead-to-retained rate even modestly, without spending another paid dollar, moves the firm closer to its own $5–7M goal
  • Blaming a channel for a conversion problem risks writing off paid lead generation entirely, right when the firm needs more of it — not less — to hit its growth goals
A firm that wants to be the largest aviation law firm in the country can't afford to let leads it already paid for slip through a buried form or an after-hours gap.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The primary contact form sits at the bottom of the homepage, inside the "Free Consultation" section, requiring a full scroll to reach it
  • No live chat is present anywhere on the site

Team Assessment

Aero Law Center has real structure — 13 team members and a dedicated Finance/Revenue Manager — but its highest-value work and its most important calls still route through one person. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • John, the board-certified principal attorney billing $700/hour, was not available for this discovery call — a sign of how much of the firm's calendar still runs through him personally
  • Google Business Profile access is tied to John's personal Gmail account, not a firm-managed account — a small but real single point of dependency
  • No described intake, marketing, or ops leadership layer exists between the Finance/Revenue Manager and the day-to-day work of growing the firm's newest channels
  • A satellite-office/multi-GBP-location strategy is under consideration, which will only compound how much depends on centralized decision-making unless a management layer is built first
Blind Spot: Having a Finance/Revenue Manager already in place is genuine progress most firms this size don't have yet — but it can create a false sense that the operational gap is closed, when the marketing and growth side of the business still has no equivalent dedicated owner.
The Positive
  • 13 team members with individual bio pages already reflect a firm that has invested in building out real depth, not just a solo practice with a website
  • A dedicated Finance/Revenue Manager (Sean Anglim) already exists and served as the call contact — exactly the kind of role most firms at this stage haven't yet created
The Gap
  • Every strategic marketing decision that has to wait for John's calendar is a decision that slows the firm's own stated growth timeline
  • A national platform with a satellite-office strategy on the table needs decision-making that doesn't bottleneck through one attorney
  • Firm-owned access to core marketing assets like GBP protects the business itself, independent of any one person's personal accounts
Building "the largest aviation law firm in the United States" requires a firm that can make its next hundred decisions without John personally weighing in on each one.

Profit Plan Assessment

Aero Law Center knows its top-line numbers clearly — roughly $3M last year, a $5–7M goal this year, $10M long-term — but the prior PPC engagement was judged a disappointment without a full look at blended case value across lead types. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No overall close rate or cost-per-acquisition figure was described on the call outside the PPC-specific historical numbers
  • The $8,600/mo SEO retainer was paid for a year without the promised content volume (6 articles/month) materializing — a real return-on-spend question that was not caught until the relationship ended
  • Case value spans a wide range ($2,000–$5,000 for demand-letter matters up to $18,000–$20,000 average overall), but past ad spend decisions do not appear to have been evaluated against that full range
  • No stated tracking of which practice area or channel is actually the most profitable per dollar spent
Blind Spot: Judging the prior PPC engagement as a failure based on a 9–11% lead-to-retained rate, without weighing it against the ~$17,000 spend and the resulting case values, may have led to walking away from a channel that was closer to profitable than it appeared.
The Positive
  • Revenue and growth goals are both known and specific — $3M actual, $5–7M this year, $10M long term — which is a stronger starting point for a profit plan than most firms have
The Gap
  • Without a per-channel view of cost versus case value, every future ad spend decision risks repeating the same "it didn't work" verdict on channels that may actually be profitable
  • A firm targeting $5–7M this year needs to know not just what it wants to spend on marketing, but what return it can expect — that math has not yet been run
Hitting $10M only builds real wealth if the firm can see, channel by channel, which dollars in produce which dollars out — not just whether revenue went up.
