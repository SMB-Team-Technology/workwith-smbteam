Intake Assessment

With the intake specialist role sitting vacant, paralegals are the ones answering intake calls at MCAT — pulling them away from filing lawsuits and sending demands on cases that are already signed. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Paralegals handle intake calls because the intake specialist role has been vacant, with no dedicated resource to take over
  • No dedicated after-hours coverage in either office — calls after hours risk going unanswered
  • No centralized intake tracking system is referenced anywhere in the research, so leads are likely tracked informally at best
  • High-value work — filing lawsuits, sending demands — is delayed specifically because paralegals are pulled into intake instead
Blind Spot: You may not realize how much revenue-generating work — lawsuits filed, demands sent — is being delayed every week specifically because the people who should be doing that work are stuck answering intake calls instead.
The Gap
  • Every hour a paralegal spends on intake is an hour not spent on a signed case's lawsuit or demand — this directly slows revenue realization on cases already won
  • Without a dedicated intake specialist, there is no way to guarantee every incoming lead — referral or paid — gets the same fast, consistent follow-up
  • As paid marketing ramps up, this same bottleneck will only get worse unless intake is fixed first
Fixing intake first means the marketing investment the firm is about to make will not go to waste — every new lead can actually convert into the extra $2M/year the partners need to prove ROI.

Team Assessment

MCAT's four partners each run their own practice under one shared-overhead structure — a "four chiefs" model that has kept the firm profitable but leaves no one dedicated to building the systems, hiring, and structure a $10M firm needs. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • The firm operates as "four chiefs" — four partners with individual practices and no single person accountable for firm-wide operations
  • No fractional or in-house COO exists to build systems, hire, and manage a dedicated team
  • The vacant intake specialist role has gone unfilled, leaving paralegals to absorb work outside their job
  • 6.5 support staff are spread across two offices, covering roles — like intake — that likely need dedicated ownership that does not currently exist
Blind Spot: The partners may not realize that a firm with four independent practices under shared overhead cannot get partner buy-in for growth investment the same way a firm with one unified operator can — someone has to own the systems, not just the overhead split.
The Positive
  • Five experienced attorneys with individual bios and 25+ years of tenure in the same market bring real, earned stability
The Gap
  • Without a dedicated operator, every new initiative — marketing, intake hires — competes for four partners' already-full attention instead of one person's job
  • The firm cannot prove ROI to partners fast enough to unlock further investment if no one owns tracking and reporting on that ROI
  • Partners explicitly said they are unlikely to attend group coaching — meaning any solution has to work without requiring their day-to-day time
A dedicated operator — not another meeting for four already-stretched partners to attend — is what turns "four chiefs" into one growth engine capable of proving the ROI needed to unlock the path to $10M+.

Profit Plan Assessment

MCAT allocates a fixed 35% of revenue to overhead, with everything else paid directly to the four partners — a structure that provides some clarity but leaves little capital available to fund new growth investment. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • A fixed 35% overhead allocation means growth investment has to come directly out of partner take-home pay — raising the bar for buy-in
  • No profit plan exists yet to fund the targeted extra $2M/year without cutting into what partners currently take home
  • No visibility beyond the overhead split was mentioned into per-practice-area or per-partner profitability
  • Growth investment and partner compensation are currently competing for the same dollars
Blind Spot: You may not realize that until growth investment can pay for itself out of new revenue rather than existing overhead, the partners will always view it as a cost taken from their own pockets instead of a lever pulling in the direction they want to go.
The Positive
  • The firm has a clear, defined overhead allocation model, which most firms this size do not have in writing
The Gap
  • Every dollar spent proving ROI to partners currently comes straight out of what they take home — that is a hard way to get buy-in for bigger investment
  • Without a profit plan tied to new revenue rather than existing overhead, scaling to $10M+ risks scaling stress without scaling wealth
A profit plan that shows growth investment paying for itself out of new revenue — not existing overhead — is exactly what turns partner skepticism into partner buy-in.
