Intake Assessment

Gilley Law launched with Chris as the only person handling every intake inquiry — every call, every form submission, and every follow-up depends entirely on his personal availability, and as LSA volume grows that bottleneck will only get more expensive. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED — Critical Priority
The Problem
  • Chris personally handles every intake call — when he is in court, on a case, or otherwise unavailable, there is no one to answer and no system to respond
  • No after-hours coverage: calls, texts, and form submissions after business hours go unattended until Chris is available the following day
  • No defined follow-up sequence for leads who contact the firm but do not immediately sign — prospects who need time to decide have no reason to choose Gilley Law over a competitor who follows up faster
  • No intake tracking: there is currently no way to measure how many LSA-generated leads are converting into signed clients versus being lost to slow response or no response
Blind Spot: With $6,000/month in LSAs actively generating calls, every missed call or after-hours inquiry is a lead that was already paid for and lost. The ad spend is creating demand that the current intake process cannot fully capture — meaning a meaningful portion of that $6,000/month is effectively wasted before a conversation ever happens.
The Positive
  • The website contact form provides a structured consultation request pathway that captures leads even when no one is available by phone
  • Free 60-minute consultation offer is prominently displayed, lowering the barrier for first contact and increasing the likelihood that leads take action
The Gap
  • Every missed call represents $2,000–$2,500 in potential case revenue that was already earned through paid advertising and then surrendered to a competitor who answered the phone
  • Without intake tracking, there is no visibility into the conversion rate of LSA leads — the firm cannot know how many leads are being won versus lost, or why
  • As paid advertising scales, the intake bottleneck will prevent the marketing investment from reaching its full return — more leads without a better intake process just means more lost leads
A trained intake system converts the leads Gilley Law is already paying for — and removes Chris from being the bottleneck between a prospect's call and a signed client.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The gilley.law website has a contact form anchored via "Request a Consultation" CTAs throughout the page — the form is accessible and clearly positioned, which is a genuine strength for a new firm
  • No live chat, no scheduling widget, and no chatbot are present — the form is the only non-phone contact option, and form submissions route entirely to Chris with no automated acknowledgment
  • After-hours form submissions enter a void: there is no automated response, no follow-up sequence, and no acknowledgment that the inquiry was received — prospects submitting after hours have no reason to wait rather than call the next firm on the list

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

Gilley Law is a fully solo operation — Chris is the only attorney, the only intake coordinator, and the only person managing every aspect of the firm's operations. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED — Critical Priority
The Problem
  • Chris is the only person in the firm — every task, from answering intake calls to managing LSA campaigns to handling legal work, falls on him personally
  • The firm stops generating revenue any time Chris is unavailable — a single day in court, a medical appointment, or a vacation means no intake, no follow-up, and no new cases
  • No accountability structure, no defined roles, and no operational workflows exist — the firm runs entirely on Chris's institutional knowledge and direct effort
  • Every additional case Chris accepts increases personal workload directly, which is the inverse of the autonomous model he is building toward
Blind Spot: The 10-11 PM workdays Chris left his previous firm to escape are already at risk of returning — not because of someone else's demands, but because growth without a team means more work for the only person there. Revenue will grow faster than Chris can personally absorb it, and the breaking point arrives before a team is in place.
The Gap
  • Without a team, scaling revenue means working more hours — the exact outcome Chris walked away from his previous firm to avoid
  • There is no path from where Gilley Law is today to the autonomous firm Chris described without hiring and building a team structure — revenue alone will not create that
  • Every month operating solo is a month the firm's dependence on Chris deepens, making future delegation harder and the transition more disruptive
Building a team is not an operational task — it is the only way to build the firm Chris described: one that earns revenue without requiring him to be the last one working every night.

Profit Plan Assessment

Gilley Law opened in May 2026 with strong early revenue momentum — but as a brand-new firm with no established financial infrastructure, there is currently no visibility into profit margins, overhead ratios, or what the firm is actually keeping versus spending. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER — In Progress
The Problem
  • No established bookkeeping or financial tracking system in place for the new firm — revenue is coming in, but there is no formal mechanism to measure what is being kept
  • The $6,000/month LSA spend is a significant line item, but without a formal ROI tracking system there is no way to confirm how many cases per dollar it is generating
  • No 2026 financial plan broken down by month, marketing channel, or revenue target — the $730,000 annual goal is a number without a structure behind it
  • No visibility into cost per case acquisition, which prevents the firm from knowing which practice area or channel is most profitable to invest in
Blind Spot: The combination of management fees and ad spend could represent 20–30% of monthly revenue depending on actual collections — without financial tracking, the firm cannot confirm whether that percentage is within a healthy range or quietly compressing margins as revenue grows.
The Positive
  • Stated case values ($2,500 for criminal defense, $2,000 for family law) provide a clear starting point for building revenue models and practice area financial benchmarks
  • A defined 2026 revenue goal of $730,000 gives a target to build a financial plan around — most solo practitioners do not have one at all
The Gap
  • Without financial visibility, marketing ROI cannot be measured — there is no way to determine which channels are generating profit and which are generating only activity
  • Revenue growth without a profit plan means cost structure can expand in parallel, leaving Chris no better off financially even as revenue increases
  • Without tracking cost per acquisition, the highest-value cases may not be receiving the most marketing investment — the allocation of that $6,000/month LSA budget may not reflect what actually produces the most profit
Knowing the numbers — cost per case, net margin per practice area, profit per marketing dollar — is what turns Gilley Law's revenue into the financial independence Chris is building toward.
