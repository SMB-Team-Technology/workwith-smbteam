Intake Assessment

With new client intake down to roughly 5 per month, every single lead that reaches CourtLaw now carries outsized weight — and there is no documented process ensuring those leads convert. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
"...yielding 'garbage' calls and no new cases."
The Problem
  • No documented intake process was described on the call — the few leads arriving each month appear to be handled without a defined follow-up sequence
  • Roughly 90% of the client base is Spanish-speaking, yet there is no Spanish-speaking secretary in place — the firm is actively trying to hire one
  • Leads from the current SEO retainer are described as "garbage" calls, which may reflect genuinely unqualified leads, poor intake screening, or both
  • No after-hours or overflow coverage was mentioned, for a firm whose highest-value case type — rideshare accidents — often starts with a call at the moment of the accident, day or night
Blind Spot: A firm converting 15% of 150 monthly leads at peak could absorb an inconsistent intake process. A firm converting from roughly 5 leads a month cannot — losing even one lead to a language gap or a slow callback is a meaningful share of this month's entire pipeline.
The Gap
  • Every lead lost to a language mismatch or slow follow-up is a disproportionately large share of an already-thin monthly pipeline
  • Without a defined process, there is no way to know whether the "garbage" calls are truly unqualified or are simply being mishandled once they arrive
  • Fixing this does not require new marketing spend — it requires catching more of what the firm is already generating
Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact forms appear above the fold, mid-page, and near the footer — a genuine strength, with multiple opportunities to capture visitor intent
  • No live chat was observed — for a firm with intake capacity already stretched thin, live chat could capture prospects who will not pick up the phone or fill out a form
  • No confirmed Spanish-language contact path, despite roughly 90% of the client base being Spanish-speaking

Team Assessment

CourtLaw has real infrastructure — 5 attorneys and roughly 20 staff — but Karim personally handles every financial decision himself, with no dedicated ops or financial leadership layer beneath him. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Karim personally handles all financial decisions and prioritizes payroll over strategic analysis — there is no delegation at this level despite a 20-person staff
  • The firm is actively trying to hire 1-2 more attorneys and a Spanish-speaking secretary, signaling the current team is already stretched even as new client intake has collapsed
  • No manager, director, or leadership layer between Karim and staff was mentioned on the call
  • CFO and bookkeeping support were offered and declined — turning down outside help while carrying every financial decision personally keeps Karim as the single point of failure
Blind Spot: Having 20 people on staff does not, by itself, mean the firm can run without you. Every business-critical decision still runs through you personally — this is a workforce, not yet a self-managing structure.
The Positive
  • A stable core team of 5 attorneys and roughly 20 staff has been retained through a 40-year history — that continuity and institutional knowledge is not something a competitor can replicate overnight
The Gap
  • Every strategic and financial decision that has to pass through Karim personally is time he cannot spend anywhere else
  • Without a leadership layer, any recovery in case volume would strain the same bottleneck that exists today
  • Hiring plans for more attorneys and a bilingual secretary address capacity, but not the deeper issue of every decision still resting on one person
Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

Karim manages all of CourtLaw's finances personally, prioritizing payroll over strategic financial planning — a pattern that leaves the firm reactive rather than in control, even in the middle of a revenue crisis. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
"Probably for a bigger firm."
The Problem
  • No profit margin, cost-per-case, or client acquisition cost figures were shared on the call — none appear to be tracked
  • Revenue peaked at $5M during COVID with no visibility shared into how much of that was actually profit versus overhead for 20 staff
  • CFO and bookkeeping support were offered directly and declined as "probably for a bigger firm," despite $5M peak revenue and a 20-person payroll
  • With new intake down to roughly 5 per month, there is no documented plan for how long the current cost structure can be sustained without a lead flow recovery
Blind Spot: The instinct to see CFO or bookkeeping support as something for "a bigger firm" may be exactly backwards — a firm this size, carrying this much payroll through this inconsistent an intake picture, is often the one that needs financial visibility the most, not the least.
The Positive
  • Karim's focus on making payroll every month, even through a revenue downturn, reflects real commitment to the 20 people who depend on this firm
The Gap
  • Without visibility into which practice areas or case types are actually most profitable, any new growth investment could go to the wrong place
  • A firm carrying 20 staff through a revenue downturn without a financial plan is one slow stretch away from a payroll crisis — repeatedly, not just once
  • Declining financial support because the firm "feels" too small removes exactly the visibility that would confirm whether that is still true
Protecting a 40-year legacy and 20 jobs means knowing the numbers well enough to plan for the next downturn before it arrives — not just surviving this one.
