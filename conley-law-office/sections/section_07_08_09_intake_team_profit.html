Intake Assessment

Conley Law Office's contact form lives on a separate page reached through navigation, with no live chat and no confirmed after-hours coverage — for a solo practice already stretched across three practice areas, that likely means slower follow-up than prospects get from larger competitors. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Contact form is not visible on the homepage — it sits on a dedicated Contact Us page reached via navigation
  • No live chat found anywhere on the site — after-hours visitors have no way to start a conversation
  • No stated intake process, CRM, or tracking system for any of the firm's three practice areas
  • With Ginny as the only named attorney, intake almost certainly competes for her time with active casework and the firm's own bookkeeping cleanup
Blind Spot: You may not realize how many people search for a divorce or criminal defense attorney after hours or on weekends — with no live chat and a buried contact form, those visitors may be calling a competitor before Conley Law ever gets the message.
The Positive
  • Phone number and office address are prominently placed in the footer site-wide, giving visitors an easy way to call directly
The Gap
  • Every after-hours website visitor who can't reach anyone is a lead handed to whichever competitor answers first
  • Without a tracked intake process, there is no way to know how many leads the firm's own website and directory listings are already generating
  • A buried contact form adds friction at the exact moment a prospect is deciding whether to call
Ginny cannot build a sellable firm on an intake process no one can see, measure, or replicate without her.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact form is on a separate Contact Us page, not the homepage — adds a step between interest and action
  • No live chat widget found — after-hours or hesitant visitors have no lower-commitment way to start a conversation
  • Phone number and address are in the footer site-wide, so visitors who prefer to call can find that information easily

Team Assessment

Ginny Conley is the only named attorney at Conley Law Office, and the firm's own financial administration is handled by a part-time bookkeeper whose inconsistent process has already led to a real trust-accounting error — a sign the firm is still built around Ginny personally, not around a team that can run without her. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Ginny Conley is the only named attorney — the website's "our team" language does not identify any other lawyer or manager
  • The firm's own bookkeeping — its most basic internal operational function — depends on a part-time bookkeeper whose inconsistent process created a real compliance incident
  • No evidence of a manager, COO, or staff member with authority to run operations independent of Ginny
  • An active North Carolina expansion is being planned without a demonstrated team structure to support a second location
Blind Spot: Personally driving both the bookkeeping cleanup and the North Carolina expansion at the same time may feel like normal ownership — but it is also exactly the kind of owner-dependency that caps what a buyer will pay for this firm in 5 years.
The Gap
  • Every hour Ginny spends personally overseeing bookkeeping cleanup is an hour not spent on casework, business development, or the North Carolina expansion
  • Without a manager or COO-level team member, the firm cannot open a second location without Ginny's direct, ongoing involvement
  • A firm this dependent on one attorney is inherently harder to value and sell
A firm built to sell in 5 years needs a team that can run it without Ginny in the room — that is not where Conley Law is today.

Profit Plan Assessment

Conley Law's trust account is disorganized enough that client funds have already been misapplied between similarly-named clients, and the firm is currently relying on a manual QuickBooks workaround because its MyCase platform was adopted before its financial features existed. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Client funds for one "Anderson" client were incorrectly posted to another "Anderson" client's account — a direct compliance and financial-visibility risk
  • MyCase, the firm's practice management platform, does not accurately track trust balances because it was adopted before its financial features were built out
  • The firm currently uses a manual QuickBooks workaround for trust accounting, described internally as unsustainable
  • The planned North Carolina expansion carries stricter trust-account regulations, adding compliance risk on top of the existing gaps
Blind Spot: A disorganized trust account is not just a bookkeeping inconvenience — it is the kind of finding that can stall or sink a firm sale during buyer due diligence.
The Positive
  • The firm is already taking action — engaging a dedicated bookkeeping service and planning a MyCase-to-Clio migration to fix the trust accounting issue before it becomes a bigger problem
The Gap
  • Without accurate trust and financial reporting, Ginny cannot know what the firm is actually worth today, let alone track progress toward a stronger valuation
  • Compliance risk in a stricter regulatory state could delay or complicate the very expansion meant to grow the firm's value
  • A buyer's due diligence process will scrutinize trust accounting first — unresolved issues here can lower an offer or kill a deal outright
Clean books and a compliant trust account are not just good practice — they are the foundation of the sale value Ginny is building toward.
