Intake Assessment

Reagan personally fields nearly every prospect that comes through referrals and networking, with no after-hours coverage or CRM system standing behind him yet. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • You personally handle nearly every inbound prospect yourself — intake still runs through you rather than a documented process
  • No embedded contact form or live chat exists on your website — every visitor must click through to a scheduling flow just to reach out
  • No after-hours coverage was confirmed — evening or weekend inquiries have no defined path to a response
  • No CRM or lead-tracking system was mentioned, so there's no way to see how many prospects are contacted, followed up with, or lost along the way
Blind Spot: You may not realize how many of the 0-6 matters a month you're already fielding could be slipping through a first-contact gap you can't see today, simply because nothing is tracking it.
The Positive
  • Every prospect who reaches you gets direct access to the founding attorney himself — a level of personal attention some competitors' larger, multi-attorney teams can't offer
The Gap
  • Every lead lost to a slow first response is a referral wasted — the exact opposite of the reputation you've spent a year building
  • Without a defined process, there's no way to know how much of the "feast or famine" swing is really lost leads rather than lead volume
  • A broken intake process caps how much any new marketing spend can return — the more volume comes in, the more this gap costs
Fixing intake means you stop being the safety net that catches every lead personally — and start building the systems your 3-5 attorney firm will actually need.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • No live chat is present on the site — visitors browsing after hours have no immediate way to engage
  • The "Schedule a consultation" CTA is repeated in the header, hero, and body copy, but requires a click-through to a scheduling flow rather than an embedded above-the-fold form
  • No contact form appears directly on the homepage, adding friction at the exact moment a prospect is ready to reach out

Team Assessment

Reagan is already working 50-60 hours a week and personally manages nearly every client relationship, vendor decision, and hiring call across a team of six. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Reagan personally handles casework, client intake, and every vendor and business-development decision — nearly all of it still funnels through one person
  • Hiring so far has been reactive, driven by workload spikes, rather than a documented plan tied to the firm's three-year, 3-5 attorney target
  • No documented org structure or growth plan exists yet to guide who gets hired next, or when
  • Working 50-60 hours a week already leaves little room to build the systems that would reduce that number
Blind Spot: The fact that you're already at 50-60 hours a week and still building this firm by hand may be the clearest sign that reactive hiring has already reached its limit.
The Positive
  • A real team already exists behind Reagan — 1 W-2 support staff, 2 contract attorneys, and 2 contract paralegals — a meaningful foundation most solo-branded firms this age don't have yet
The Gap
  • Every hour Reagan spends personally managing what a system or manager could handle is an hour not spent on higher-value casework or the growth plan itself
  • Reactive hiring means growth capacity always lags a step behind demand, capping how fast the firm can responsibly take on new matters
  • Without a documented plan, there's no clear answer for who gets hired next when the next busy stretch hits
Until hiring becomes a plan instead of a reaction, Reagan cannot build the close-knit, 3-5 attorney boutique firm he described — he can only keep absorbing the next spike personally.

Profit Plan Assessment

RLB Legal grew to $500K in its first year and is targeting $650K-$750K this year, but the same unpredictable referral flow that built that revenue makes it hard to know what's actually profitable. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No profitability figures or margin discussion took place on the call — revenue growth alone hasn't yet been tied to what the firm actually keeps
  • No average case value has been established for either commercial litigation or oil & gas/transactional work, so there's no benchmark to measure a new lead's worth against
  • No cost-per-lead or cost-per-case tracking exists — a real gap given the firm is actively evaluating a $10K/mo ad agency proposal with no way to measure its return
  • The 0-6 matters/month swing makes monthly revenue and cash flow difficult to forecast, which in turn makes it harder to plan a hire's cost against expected revenue
Blind Spot: You may not realize that committing to a $10K/mo agency proposal without first establishing your own cost-per-case benchmark means you won't actually be able to tell if it worked.
The Positive
  • Hitting $500K in a genuine first year, entirely without paid marketing, shows the underlying practice is profitable on a per-matter basis — the gap is visibility, not performance
The Gap
  • Without a cost-per-case benchmark, evaluating the $10K/mo agency proposal — or any paid channel — is a guess rather than a decision
  • Unpredictable cash flow makes it harder to commit confidently to the next hire, even when workload clearly justifies one
  • The three-year, $2-3M revenue target has no visible profit-per-attorney model behind it yet to confirm it also means more take-home for Reagan, not just more revenue
Predictable profit, not just predictable revenue, is what actually lets Reagan hire from a position of strength instead of necessity.
