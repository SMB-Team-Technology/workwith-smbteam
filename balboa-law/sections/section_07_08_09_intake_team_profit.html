Intake Assessment

Balboa Law is being designed around minimal attorney involvement — which is the right instinct, but it means the entire intake system must be automated and operational before the first lead ever arrives. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No intake process exists — there is no defined pathway for what happens when a PI lead contacts Balboa Law at launch
  • Wife's role as attorney of record is intentionally minimal; without an automated intake layer, every inbound lead will fall back on Gerald to handle personally — defeating the model
  • No CRM, no intake tracking software, and no lead qualification workflow has been configured — leads that arrive at launch will have no structured system to receive them
  • In Houston PI, response speed is the primary conversion driver: the firm that calls a prospect back first wins the case in the majority of situations; there is no system yet to guarantee a sub-5-minute response to new inquiries
Blind Spot: The plan to rely heavily on automation is correct — but automation requires setup, testing, and a defined escalation path before it can be trusted with real leads. If Balboa Law launches ads before the intake infrastructure is ready, the firm will pay for leads it cannot convert. Getting the intake system operational before the marketing engine turns on is not optional; it is a prerequisite.
The Gap
  • Every unresponded or slowly-responded PI lead in Houston is effectively a case fee handed to a competitor — at an average referral fee of $15,000–$40,000 per catastrophic case, even one lost lead per week represents $60,000–$160,000 in potential quarterly income that never materializes
  • Without a defined intake workflow, Gerald cannot step back from the day-to-day even after the firm is generating leads — he becomes the intake system by default
  • The bilingual model requires intake to function in both English and Spanish; a Spanish-speaking prospect who cannot get a Spanish-language response is a lost case
The entire income goal Gerald is building toward depends on intake converting the leads marketing generates — if intake fails, ad spend becomes a cost with no return, and the firm that runs itself never gets built.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • No website contact form exists yet — the intake capture layer for web visitors must be built into the site from the beginning, not added as an afterthought
  • Contact form placement, mobile optimization, and response-time SLAs must be defined before the site goes live to ensure every web inquiry enters a structured follow-up sequence
  • Every incoming web inquiry must have an immediate automated response confirming receipt and setting expectations — prospects who submit a form and hear nothing within minutes will call a competitor

Team Assessment

Balboa Law is currently a two-person operation — Gerald runs everything, and his wife's involvement is intentionally limited — with no defined team structure, no accountability framework, and no automation systems yet built to replace the human roles the firm needs. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
"Focus on marketing and intake for high-value cases — refer to trusted partners for a referral fee. Leverage Gerald's wife's uncle's network of top PI attorneys. Heavily automated to minimize wife's required time investment."
The Problem
  • Gerald is the entire operation: business development, marketing oversight, firm management, and operational decisions all run through one person with no backup
  • No defined roles, no accountability structure, and no org chart — if Gerald is unavailable, the firm stalls
  • The automation infrastructure that the business model depends on does not yet exist; until it is built, Gerald IS the automation
  • Wife is described as "unmotivated and skeptical of PI law" — without a frictionless system that minimizes her required involvement, the firm's attorney of record is a fragile dependency
Blind Spot: The instinct to automate is exactly right, but automation is not a replacement for a team structure — it is what enables a smaller team to operate at scale. Without defined processes, accountability, and escalation paths, automation breaks down when edge cases arise and Gerald ends up handling them manually anyway. Building the process first and automating second is what allows the system to hold.
The Positive
  • Gerald has explicitly designed the firm around automation from the start — this is a significantly better foundation than firms that try to add automation after years of manual processes are already baked in
  • The referral model reduces the attorney's active case management burden, which means the firm can scale intake volume without proportionally scaling the attorney's workload
The Gap
  • Without a self-managing system in place, Gerald cannot step back from the firm even after revenue begins — he trades one form of employment for another, just in his own business
  • A firm that depends on one person's daily attention cannot be sold, cannot scale, and cannot generate the passive income stream that Gerald is building toward
  • Every week Gerald spends personally managing intake, marketing coordination, and operations is a week he is not building the systems that create freedom
The firm that runs itself — and generates $300K–$1M in profit without Gerald or his wife being consumed by it — requires an operational framework to be built deliberately, from day one, as part of the launch plan.

Profit Plan Assessment

Gerald has a clear and ambitious income target for Balboa Law's first year — $300K–$1M in net profit — and has already outlined a marketing investment framework of $100K–$200K annually to support it. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"Target a September 1 launch, with a first-year profit goal of $300K–$1M. Projected expenses: $100K–$200K/year, with marketing as the largest component."
The Problem
  • No P&L structure, no financial tracking system, and no per-case cost visibility — there is currently no way to measure whether the firm is on track toward its profit goal
  • The referral fee model creates a significant cash flow gap: cases are generated monthly but referral fees are paid when cases settle, which can take 12–36 months — this timing gap must be planned for from the start
  • Gerald's marketing budget estimate of $100K–$200K/year needs professional validation against Houston PI market CPCs; without expert guidance, the budget could be absorbed by expensive keywords before generating meaningful case volume
  • No defined profitability metrics for the referral model: target referrals per month, target referral fee per case, or minimum case value thresholds have not been established
Blind Spot: The referral fee business model has a structural profit advantage that Gerald may not be fully accounting for — by referring cases rather than settling them, Balboa Law avoids the case management overhead, expert witness costs, and working capital requirements that traditional PI firms carry. The profit margin on a well-run referral model can significantly exceed what a traditional PI firm earns on the same cases. Building a profit plan that captures this advantage from the start is how Gerald reaches $300K–$1M net faster than a traditional firm could.
The Positive
  • Gerald has established a first-year profit target and a rough expense framework — this level of financial intentionality puts Balboa Law ahead of most pre-launch firms that have no financial model at all
  • The referral model creates inherently high margins once volume is established, since Balboa Law's primary costs are marketing and intake — not case settlement expenses
The Gap
  • Without a profit plan that accounts for the referral fee cash flow timeline, Gerald could generate 20 referrals in month 3 and still show negative cash flow in month 6 while cases are pending settlement
  • No financial visibility means no ability to make intelligent decisions about when to increase ad spend, when to hire, or when to pull back — growth decisions get made on gut rather than data
  • The gap between Gerald's income goal and his marketing budget estimate needs professional validation: SMB's guidance on whether $100K–$200K/year in marketing spend can realistically achieve $300K–$1M in net profit in a Houston PI referral model is the first financial analysis that needs to happen
The $300K–$1M profit goal is achievable in the Houston PI market with the right referral model — but only with a profit plan that accounts for cash flow timing, case mix, and marketing efficiency from the beginning, not discovered after the first year of trial and error.
