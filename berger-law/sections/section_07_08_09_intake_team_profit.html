Intake Assessment

Berger Law is generating 15–20 new cases per month — a real achievement in a rural market — but a 51% consultation-to-hire rate and an average lead response time exceeding 20 minutes mean the firm is converting fewer than it should from the leads it is already paying to generate. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Average lead response time exceeds 20 minutes — the research benchmark is 5 minutes or less; beyond that, contact rates fall sharply and the probability of signing the case drops significantly
  • Consultation-to-hire rate has declined to 51%, down from historical highs — the call identified attorney performance during consultations as the primary cause, meaning the conversion problem is a coaching and scripting gap, not a lead quality problem
  • AI answers all incoming calls, but the human follow-up system that should close the loop is not functioning fast enough — the AI call handler creates a lag between first contact and a real conversation with a qualified person
  • A disconnect exists between the intake process and attorneys: attorneys are unaware of marketing efforts and what prospects have been told before a consultation, which undermines the ability to run a consistent, repeatable sales process
Blind Spot: A 51% consultation-to-hire rate feels like a marketing problem — not enough qualified leads. In reality, it is almost always a sales process problem. The prospects sitting in a consultation with a Berger Law attorney have already decided they need criminal defense help. They are not evaluating whether to hire a lawyer; they are evaluating whether to hire this lawyer. Without a defined consultation framework and coached response to common objections, that evaluation is entirely dependent on how each attorney happens to feel that day.
The Positive
  • The firm is already using an AI call answering system — the infrastructure for 24/7 response exists; what is needed is the human follow-up layer and the intake script that converts the AI-captured contact into a booked consultation
  • With 15–20 cases per month coming in, the firm has enough lead volume that even modest improvements to conversion will produce meaningful revenue gains without any increase in marketing spend
The Gap
  • At a 51% close rate on 15–20 monthly consultations, the firm is losing 7–10 cases per month it paid to generate — at an estimated $4,000 average case value, that is $28,000–$40,000 per month in revenue the firm earned and then gave away
  • Without a multi-channel follow-up cadence (text, email, call) for prospects who do not sign at the initial consultation, a significant portion of lost cases could be recovered with structured follow-up alone
  • The absence of intake-attorney communication means consultations are going in cold — prospects arrive with expectations set during the AI intake that attorneys do not know about, creating disconnects that reduce trust and conversion
Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing. For Alex, that means more revenue from the same effort, which is the exact financial foundation that makes a 10-hour-a-week role possible.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • No live chat widget was found on the website — for criminal defense, the most urgent calls happen after hours and on weekends when most arrests occur; a prospect who cannot reach someone immediately will call the next firm in the search results
  • A contact form exists at lawyerupmichigan.com/contact/ — whether it is visible without navigating away from the homepage is unconfirmed, and a form that requires multiple clicks to find is effectively invisible for most mobile visitors
  • The firm's "free case evaluation" offer appears across multiple pages but without an immediate, frictionless response mechanism — a form submission that goes into a queue and gets called back 20 minutes later loses the urgency advantage that the "free evaluation" offer creates

Team Assessment

Berger Law has four attorneys and two support staff — more than a solo practice, but still entirely dependent on Alex for direction, accountability, and operational decisions. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • KPI tracking is manual and time-consuming — performance bonuses depend on Alex personally compiling numbers, which means he cannot delegate performance management without first building the infrastructure that makes it measurable
  • Holding staff accountable for metrics was identified as a specific challenge — without defined roles, automated KPI dashboards, and a structured accountability process, accountability requires constant owner intervention
  • Finding management-level talent in the rural Upper Peninsula is a documented hiring challenge — without a pipeline for operations-capable staff, the team cannot grow a leadership layer even when the will to do so exists
  • The firm operates with a silo between intake and attorneys — both functions exist but they are not coordinated, which means Alex is the default connector between them and removing him creates a gap in the workflow
Blind Spot: The target of 10 hours a week by 2029 is 3 years away — which can feel like plenty of time. But the infrastructure needed to get there (roles defined, KPIs automated, management layer hired and trained, intake-attorney coordination systematized) typically takes 18–24 months to build properly. Starting in 2026 is not early. It is exactly on schedule. Waiting until 2027 or 2028 means the 2029 goal becomes mathematically impossible.
The Positive
  • Four attorneys with named, dedicated roles (Alex Berger, Glenn Sarka, Jana Mathieu, Tim Wrather) means there is an existing team to build a leadership layer on top of — this is not starting from zero
  • Alex has clearly articulated the goal and timeline — owners who can name the destination and the date are far more likely to build toward it than those who say "someday"
The Gap
  • Without automated KPI tracking, Alex spends time every month doing work that a system should be doing — time that could be redirected to strategic decisions or, eventually, to the 10-hour-a-week life he described
  • The absence of a management layer means attorney performance and intake conversion are only as good as Alex's availability to monitor and coach them — which is not a scalable model for the 2029 goal
  • Without defined roles and clear accountability structures, hiring for the right positions becomes difficult — it is hard to recruit a COO or operations manager without a job description that reflects a real organizational chart
Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows. The team is the mechanism through which the 10-hour-a-week goal becomes real, and building it requires deliberate investment now.

Profit Plan Assessment

Berger Law generates $600k–$1M in combined annual revenue, but the spread between those two numbers tells the real story: revenue is volatile, and Alex described wanting to stabilize it — which means the current financial structure is not producing the predictable income that a strategic step-back requires. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Revenue ranges from $600k to $1M combined — a $400k swing that suggests the firm does not yet have the financial predictability needed to plan ahead or invest with confidence
  • No confirmed financial visibility into cost per signed case, per-attorney productivity or profitability, or which practice areas generate the best margins — without this data, budget decisions are made on intuition rather than numbers
  • The $250k personal W-2 goal is specific and meaningful, but there is no described profit plan or compensation structure that guarantees it is achievable at current revenue without a clear view of margins and overhead
  • Marketing spend of $5k/month represents a material portion of overhead — but without cost-per-signed-case tracking, there is no way to evaluate whether that spend is generating profitable revenue or just activity
Blind Spot: The Wyoming firm's combined revenue makes the Michigan operation's true profitability harder to see. If the combined figure is $600k–$1M, and Wyoming accounts for a meaningful share, the Michigan operation may be generating far less profit than the headline number suggests — which changes the calculus for what is affordable to invest in growth and what Alex actually takes home.
The Gap
  • Without bookkeeping and forecasting infrastructure, the firm cannot proactively manage toward the $250k W-2 goal — it can only react to what the bank account shows at the end of the month
  • Revenue volatility is a profit planning problem: the months where revenue dips are costing Alex sleep and operational certainty that proper cash flow planning and retainer structures can eliminate
  • The decision to expand into Wisconsin (1–2 satellite offices) is a major capital commitment that cannot be made safely without a clear picture of what the Michigan operation is generating and what margins allow for reinvestment
A $250k personal W-2 that is consistent and predictable — not just in good months — is what makes it possible to step back without worrying. That does not happen without a profit plan, and a profit plan does not happen without seeing the numbers clearly first.
