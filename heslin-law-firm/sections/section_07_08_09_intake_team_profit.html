Intake Assessment

The Heslin Law Firm has one of the strongest intake operations seen in a firm of this size — the staff signs nearly every case the firm wants to take, and the bottleneck is not conversion, it is the volume of leads coming in the front door. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: GREEN
The Problem
  • No after-hours coverage — the GBP and website list 8:00 AM – 4:00 PM business hours, meaning any prospect who calls or searches after hours has no path to connect with the firm that evening
  • Personal injury prospects frequently search and call immediately after an accident — evenings, weekends, and late nights are peak demand windows that the firm is not currently capturing
  • No lead tracking in place — even though intake itself is effective, there is no system to measure how many prospects are reaching out, being lost before a consultation, or converting at each stage
Blind Spot: The strength of the firm's intake process may be masking how many leads are being lost before they ever reach intake. A prospect who calls at 7pm, gets voicemail, and calls someone else never appears in the firm's numbers — but that case went somewhere. Implementing a 24/7 answering service does not just improve conversion; it expands the total universe of cases the firm can compete for.
The Positive
  • Staff signs nearly all cases the firm wants — an exceptional close rate that means every additional lead generated by the marketing rebuild translates directly into additional signed cases
  • The intake bottleneck is lead volume, not process — this is the best-case scenario for a marketing investment: the infrastructure to convert leads already exists and works
The Gap
  • Without 24/7 availability, the firm is invisible to prospects during the hours when personal injury leads are most likely to search — the same hours when competitors with 24/7 GBP listings are appearing above the firm in local search results
  • Every case that calls after 4pm and does not reach a live person is a case that likely calls a competitor next — at $8,000 average case value, even one lost after-hours case per month represents significant recoverable revenue
The intake team Gary has built is a genuine asset — and every new case the marketing rebuild generates will be converted by that same team, making the revenue impact of lead generation immediate rather than delayed by process gaps.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact form is located at /contact.cfm — a separate page from the homepage, not visible above the fold, which reduces conversion of website visitors who do not scroll or click through to find it
  • No live chat observed on the current Foster Web Marketing site — website visitors have no immediate way to connect after hours or when they prefer not to call
  • The new WordPress site should place a contact form and click-to-call button above the fold on the homepage, and should include a chat widget for after-hours lead capture

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

The Heslin Law Firm is in the middle of a planned leadership transition — Gary is stepping back and Brian Emhof is stepping in — but the playbook for that transition has not yet been written. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Gary is still the primary attorney and the face of the firm — the firm's brand, referral network, and client relationships are tied to Gary personally, not to the firm as an entity
  • Brian Emhof is joining full-time in November 2026 (pending bar exam results), but there is no formal onboarding plan, caseload transition structure, or leadership development framework in place
  • If Brian is not set up to independently manage cases from day one, Gary will remain operationally essential beyond November 2026 — the step-back Gary has planned will be delayed
  • The firm lacks a documented playbook for how cases are managed, how decisions are made, and how Gary's institutional knowledge gets transferred to Brian before Gary reduces his hours
Blind Spot: A firm that depends entirely on one attorney to function is not a business — it is a job that happens to employ other people. Until the firm can run a case from intake to resolution without Gary being personally involved at every stage, the transition cannot succeed. Building that structure is not a post-November project; it needs to start now.
The Positive
  • Gary has a clear successor in Brian Emhof and a defined target date in November 2026 — most firms never get this far in transition planning, and having the person and the deadline already identified is a significant head start
  • The intake staff is already highly effective and does not require the attorney to be present — the team infrastructure to support Brian already exists
The Gap
  • Without a documented transition plan, Gary cannot step back confidently — he will remain on the hook for every case and every client relationship even after November, because no one will have the authority or knowledge to handle things without him
  • Brian stepping into a firm with 12 cases per month and no playbook is setting him up to struggle — building the structure and caseload simultaneously, alone, is the recipe for the transition to stall
  • The coaching work that needs to happen — building leadership structure, documenting systems, preparing Brian — is not something that can be rushed in the final 60 days before his start date
The transition Gary has worked 30 years toward succeeds or fails based on how well Brian is prepared before November 2026 — and that preparation has to start now, not after the new site is live.

Profit Plan Assessment

The Heslin Law Firm generates consistent revenue of approximately $700,000–$800,000 per year — a solid foundation — but without lead tracking, case-level financial visibility, or a clear plan for growing to the revenue level needed to support Brian's hire, the firm's profitability is being discovered rather than planned. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No lead tracking system — without knowing where cases come from, it is impossible to calculate cost per acquisition, identify the most profitable lead sources, or make informed decisions about where to invest in marketing
  • The firm needs to grow from 8 to 12 cases per month to financially support Brian Emhof's full-time hire — that is a 50% increase in caseload, and there is currently no marketing plan in place to produce it
  • No stated visibility into profit margins, overhead as a percentage of revenue, or revenue per case type — the firm knows total revenue but not which parts of the business are most and least profitable
  • Adding a full-time attorney hire without a corresponding revenue plan creates financial risk — if the caseload does not grow to 12 cases per month by November, the firm takes on payroll before the revenue to support it is in place
Blind Spot: Revenue of $700k–$800k is healthy — but the firm may be leaving money on the table by taking cases of all types without knowing which case types generate the highest fee income relative to time invested. A single data-driven insight about case profitability could shift the firm's intake strategy in ways that improve revenue without adding a single new case.
The Positive
  • Consistent revenue at the $700k–$800k level demonstrates a proven business model and strong client service — the financial foundation is real
  • Adding 4 cases per month at an average value of approximately $8,000 per case produces approximately $384,000 in additional annual revenue — a clear, achievable financial target tied directly to the transition goal
The Gap
  • Without lead tracking, there is no way to know whether the marketing investment being considered is producing the cases that generate it — the firm cannot optimize what it does not measure
  • If the caseload does not reach 12 per month before November 2026, Gary faces a choice: delay the transition or hire Brian before the revenue supports it — neither is the outcome Gary is working toward
  • A clear financial plan that models the revenue impact of going from 8 to 12 cases per month — including marketing investment, Brian's compensation, and target profit margins — does not yet exist
The number Gary needs to hit is clear — 12 cases per month by November 2026 — and building the financial visibility to track progress toward that number is what makes the difference between a transition that happens and one that gets delayed indefinitely.
