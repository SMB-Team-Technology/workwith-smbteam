Intake Assessment

Pilkington Immigration generates strong lead volume through organic search and referrals — but without a dedicated intake team or defined conversion process, those leads are being converted by whoever happens to answer, at whatever rate they personally achieve that day. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No dedicated intake or sales team — attorneys personally handle all consultations, including the 20–25 paid Zoom consultations per month at $300 each, with conversion rates that vary significantly by individual attorney
  • Some attorneys are described as strong closers; others are explicitly not suited to sales conversations — the French-speaking attorney is noted as not well-suited to the sales side of consultations, meaning language-specific leads are likely converting at a lower rate than English-language cases
  • No documented intake process, no defined follow-up sequence, and no tracking of lead-to-case conversion rate — without knowing the current close rate, it is impossible to know how many leads are being lost between inquiry and signed engagement
  • No after-hours coverage mechanism — the firm's geographic network spans Ontario and Florida, meaning time-zone gaps and after-hours inquiries from U.S.-based clients (E-visa, EB-5, family sponsorship) are almost certainly going unaddressed until the next business day
Blind Spot: The 20–25 paid Zoom consultations per month at $300 each generate roughly $6,000–$7,500/month in consultation revenue — but they also create a hidden opportunity cost. Attorneys spending time on $300 consultations that do not convert into cases are not available for billable case work. A trained intake specialist handling pre-qualification would free attorneys to spend their time on the highest-value cases only.
The Positive
  • The paid consultation model ($300 Zoom) is a meaningful filter that pre-qualifies serious prospects before an attorney's time is committed — this is a structural advantage most firms do not have
  • The firm handles 140–160 invoices per month with approximately 75% new clients, suggesting strong market demand — the intake process does not need to create volume, it needs to convert the volume that already exists more reliably
The Gap
  • If the firm is generating 140–160 new client engagements per month at an undocumented close rate, even a 10-percentage-point improvement in consultation conversion would represent dozens of additional cases per month across practice areas averaging $2,000–$7,500 in case value
  • Attorney-handled intake at a $3M firm is a Stage 3 behavior in a Stage 4 body — it is the single most direct constraint on Andrew's ability to step back from daily operations, because the revenue generating process requires him and his attorneys to be personally present for every conversion
  • Without after-hours coverage, every overnight inquiry from a U.S. client (E-visa or family-based sponsorship) that reaches voicemail is a lead that will call the next firm on the search results page by morning
Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The website's primary call-to-action routes visitors to a separate consultation request page rather than presenting an inline form — this extra click is a conversion friction point that reduces form completion rates, particularly on mobile
  • No live chat is present on the website — for immigration clients who may have language barriers or are uncomfortable calling, a text-based contact option significantly increases conversion
  • The toll-free number (1-844-728-1012) is prominently displayed above the fold — strong for phone-comfortable prospects, but the site lacks a 24/7 contact capture option for after-hours visitors across U.S. and Canadian time zones

Team Assessment

Pilkington Immigration has built a 30-person firm with 5 offices, a multi-country practice, and a recently acquired U.S. law firm — but the firm's operational center is still Andrew Maloney, who has never worked with an outside advisor and remains heavily embedded in day-to-day decisions. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Andrew is the operational hub for a firm that has outgrown that structure — marketing strategy, digital advertising decisions, and key business direction all route through him personally, even though the firm employs 30 people across 5 locations
  • There is no management layer between Andrew and the firm's operations that allows the business to run without his direct involvement; Brian Gill handles client relations and day-to-day marketing tasks, but this is not the same as a leadership structure that can operate independently
  • Andrew has never worked with a business coach or outside advisor — the firm has been built entirely through his individual effort, which means there is no external accountability structure, no tested playbook for delegation, and no framework for building the management layer the firm needs
  • Newly acquired Silser Law in Orlando (~$1.5M/yr) adds complexity — integrating a U.S. acquisition while also running the Canadian operation without a strong operational framework in place is a significant risk to both
Blind Spot: The fact that Andrew has successfully grown to $3M by managing everything himself makes delegation feel risky — but it also makes it more urgent. The habits and systems that got the firm to $3M will actively prevent it from getting to $6M. The ceiling is not the market; it is the management structure.
The Positive
  • Andrew has already built a team of 30 and made a strategic acquisition — these are Stage 5 and Stage 6 moves that demonstrate his vision and execution ability; the infrastructure exists to build the self-managing firm, it just needs the right operational framework
  • Brian Gill's role in client relations and marketing provides a foundation for a dedicated marketing leadership function — with the right structure, Brian becomes part of the management layer rather than just a day-to-day executor
The Gap
  • Andrew's direct involvement in day-to-day operations is the primary bottleneck on the firm's growth — every decision that requires him personally is a decision that could not be made while he was on vacation, in a strategy meeting, or scaling the Orlando acquisition
  • Without an accountability structure and documented operational playbooks, the firm cannot scale into new markets (London ON, Kitchener, Edmonton, Calgary, and others Andrew identified) without proportionally increasing Andrew's personal workload
  • A $3M firm acquiring a $1.5M practice needs an operational framework to integrate both; without FCOO-level leadership, the integration risk falls entirely on Andrew, compounding the time and bandwidth constraint that is already limiting growth
Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

Pilkington Immigration generates approximately $3M USD annually with a clear picture of revenue by practice area — but the firm has been flat on that number for nearly two years, is actively burning money on an underperforming ad channel, and does not have the financial visibility to understand whether its shifting practice area mix is improving or eroding margins. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Revenue has flatlined at approximately $3M USD for nearly two years — the firm's prior growth trajectory ($2.2M → $2.4M → $3M) has stalled, and without a clear financial diagnostic it is difficult to identify whether the issue is acquisition volume, conversion rate, case mix, or overhead structure
  • $10,000 per month is being spent on Google Ads at below 0.5:1 ROI — this represents a confirmed monthly loss of roughly $5,000+ in wasted advertising spend that is not generating returns; this money is not reaching the firm's revenue, it is leaving the firm entirely
  • The U.S. Entry Waiver practice area, formerly generating $750,000 per year in revenue, is now on track for approximately $160,000 YTD — a decline of over $590,000 annualized. Without per-practice-area profitability tracking, it is unknown whether the growth in E-Visa ($325K YTD) and other areas is fully compensating for this structural revenue loss
  • No profitability tracking by practice area was mentioned — meaning the firm does not know its cost to acquire a client by case type, its margin contribution by practice area, or whether the current mix of cases is more or less profitable than it was when revenue was last growing
Blind Spot: The shift in practice area mix — from U.S. Entry Waivers (lower case value, $1,500–$2,000) toward E-Visas (higher case value, $7,000–$8,000) — could actually represent a margin improvement even if total volume has declined. Or it could mean that higher-complexity E-Visa cases are consuming more attorney time per dollar earned. Without case-level profitability data, there is no way to know — and no way to make a deliberate decision about where to invest marketing dollars for the best return.
The Positive
  • Andrew has clear visibility into revenue by practice area (E-Visa $325K, Spousal Sponsorship $250K, Citizenship $200K, U.S. Waiver $160K, L1 Visa $150K, Family-Based $115K YTD) — this is a stronger financial foundation than most firms at this stage have, and provides the raw data needed to build a proper profitability model
  • The firm has capacity for approximately 30% more clients without new hires — meaning growth does not require a proportional increase in overhead, and improved marketing performance will flow directly to the bottom line
The Gap
  • Knowing revenue by practice area is the starting point for financial clarity — but without cost allocation by case type, the firm cannot identify its highest-margin work and deliberately shift marketing investment toward it
  • A $120,000/year loss on broken Google Ads (12 months × $10K/month at sub-0.5:1 ROI) represents a profit leak that, once fixed, immediately improves the firm's effective margin without changing any other cost structure
  • Without a CFO-level view of the P&L — tracking both the Canadian operation and the newly acquired Orlando firm as integrated entities — Andrew cannot make strategic decisions about where to grow next, how much to invest in each market, or what the combined operation is actually worth
The path to the CEO role Andrew is building toward is built on financial clarity — knowing exactly what the firm earns, where it earns it, and how to grow the number that matters most.
