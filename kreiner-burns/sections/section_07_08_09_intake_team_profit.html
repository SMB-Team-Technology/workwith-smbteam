Intake Assessment

Kreiner Burns is losing qualified leads at the first point of contact — website form submissions are routing to an unconnected third party, and partners are handling calls without a defined process or follow-up system. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Website contact form leads are routing to a partner's brother who operates independently and does not forward them to the firm — an unknown quantity of qualified commercial and employment prospects have been lost with no awareness this was happening
  • Partners handle all intake personally "on the fly" with no formal process — no intake script, no defined qualification criteria, no structured follow-up timeline for leads who do not sign immediately
  • No dedicated intake coordinator or staff member — all initial client screening and engagement flows directly through Seth, Peter, or Tom, consuming senior attorney time on work that does not require a partner's involvement
  • No scheduling tool (Calendly, Acuity, or equivalent) on the website — prospects who visit after hours or prefer not to call have no self-serve path to booking a consultation
Blind Spot: Every day the contact form routes leads away from the firm, those prospects are potentially engaging with competitors. There is no way to know how many cases have already been lost because of this routing issue — but any one of them could represent $5,000–$25,000+ in commercial litigation fees.
The Positive
  • 75–80% close rate on qualified referral meetings confirms the partners are highly persuasive once in front of a prospect — the intake problem is not conversion skill, it is the number of qualified leads actually reaching them
The Gap
  • Every untracked form submission is a potential commercial or employment client who arrived with zero marketing cost — these are the highest-value leads the firm generates, and they are being lost before any attorney ever speaks with them
  • Without a formal intake protocol, the close rate on marketing-generated leads — which arrive less pre-qualified than referrals — will be materially lower than the current 75–80%, making ad spend less efficient than it could be
  • Leads who do not sign immediately require multiple touchpoints; without a defined follow-up sequence, those prospects are not being re-engaged and are almost certainly signing with competitors who have a process
Until intake is fixed, increasing the volume of marketing-generated leads will not proportionally increase revenue — it will just increase the number of qualified prospects the firm loses before they become clients.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The website contact form is buried in the footer — not visible to visitors who do not scroll the full page; the primary conversion mechanism is effectively hidden from the people most likely to use it
  • No form above the fold on the homepage means visitors who arrive via paid ads or organic search must actively hunt for the contact mechanism — most will not, and the traffic investment is wasted
  • No scheduling widget anywhere on the site — the only path to contact is a phone call or the buried footer form, both of which require active effort at a moment when most prospects are still evaluating options

Team Assessment

Kreiner Burns has built a multi-state, multi-attorney practice with genuine structural depth — 8 attorneys across 4 offices is an asset most growing firms never achieve. But all intake, business development, and firm management decisions still flow through the founding partners, making the firm's capacity to scale entirely dependent on Seth, Peter, and Tom personally. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Partners are the primary point of contact for all intake, client acquisition, and key firm management decisions — no dedicated staff layer exists between the partners and these operational responsibilities
  • No defined accountability structure for the 8-attorney team — who owns intake follow-up, who owns practice area business development, and who owns operational coordination across four states has not been formalized
  • 8 attorneys across 4 states creates real coordination complexity — the founding partners are effectively managing a distributed practice without a defined operations or management structure to support them
  • The path to $2 million requires adding associates; but without defined roles and a management layer, each new hire increases partner supervision burden rather than reducing it
Blind Spot: The firm has the attorney headcount many growing practices dream of — but without defined roles and an intake accountability structure, the partners are doing the work of firm managers on top of their attorney responsibilities every day. The cost of this is invisible because it shows up as time lost, not as a line item on a P&L.
The Positive
  • 8 attorneys across 4 geographic offices is a structural foundation that most firms at this revenue level have not built — the raw material for a genuinely scalable multi-state practice is already in place
The Gap
  • Without a defined operations layer, every new attorney hired to hit $2 million will need partner-level supervision — making growth more expensive and more demanding on the founders rather than less
  • The partners cannot evaluate hiring, marketing allocation, or strategic decisions by practice area without visibility into which attorneys and markets are driving growth versus costs
  • A firm with clear accountabilities, defined roles, and a management layer is the prerequisite for the time and financial freedom that $2 million in revenue is meant to buy
Kreiner Burns has the attorneys to build a $2 million firm — the next step is building the accountability infrastructure that lets those attorneys produce revenue without requiring a partner in every conversation.

Profit Plan Assessment

Kreiner Burns has grown from $700,000 to $900,000 over two years — real and meaningful momentum — but 60–70% of that revenue is concentrated in just one or two client relationships, making the firm's financial stability as fragile as a single referral. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • 60–70% revenue concentration in 1–2 clients is a single-event catastrophe waiting to happen — one client departure or dispute could eliminate $540,000–$630,000 in annual revenue overnight, with no marketing system capable of replacing it
  • No profit margin, overhead breakdown, or cost-per-acquisition data was available on the call — the firm does not yet have the financial visibility to make confident investment decisions about hiring, marketing, or expansion
  • No documented financial roadmap from $900,000 to $2 million — the goal is specific, but the revenue mix, practice area weighting, and cost structure required to achieve it has not been defined
  • Multi-state office overhead across four locations (Hoboken NJ, Yardley PA, Boca Raton FL, Baltimore MD) creates fixed costs without confirmed corresponding revenue attribution by market
Blind Spot: Without a financial model that attributes revenue by practice area and market, the firm cannot identify whether commercial litigation, employment law, or family law is contributing to profit — or consuming it. The most profitable case type may not be the one receiving the most partner time and attention right now.
The Positive
  • Revenue growth from $700,000 to $900,000 confirms a real upward trajectory — and the $2 million goal is a 2.2× increase from a legitimate base, which is achievable with the right systems and timeline
The Gap
  • Without knowing cost-per-acquisition by case type, the firm cannot determine whether the marketing investment it is about to make will generate positive returns in commercial litigation versus employment versus family law — it cannot make a data-driven decision about where to spend first
  • Revenue concentration in 1–2 clients means all budgeting, hiring, and investment decisions are hostage to those relationships — the firm cannot plan with confidence when the foundation of its revenue can shift at any time
  • The gap from $900,000 to $2 million requires a deliberate financial roadmap — not just more leads, but the right mix of case types, disciplined pricing, and a cost structure that preserves margin as the firm scales toward its target
The $2 million goal cannot be planned for without knowing where profit lives today — and building that financial visibility is the first number that needs to come into focus before any growth investment is made.
