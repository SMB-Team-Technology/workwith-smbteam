Intake Assessment

Eric Rhodes personally manages every aspect of intake alongside a full litigation caseload — an arrangement that works when there are no leads coming in, but breaks immediately when a marketing system is added. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
"He needs immediate, skilled help, not long-term strategic coaching. He also views a Fractional COO as premature due to the high cost."
The Problem
  • Eric handles every intake call and client communication personally — there is no delegation, no backup, and no system for managing inquiries when he is in court or preparing briefs
  • Previous paralegal hires required too much hand-holding and did not relieve his intake burden — the lack of a trained intake process means every new hire starts from scratch
  • A compressed schedule creates a direct conflict between answering new inquiries and doing billable work — one always loses
  • No defined follow-up sequence exists for prospects who do not sign immediately — leads that do not convert on first contact are likely lost entirely
Blind Spot: When a marketing system goes live, the call volume Eric receives will increase significantly. Without an intake process in place before that happens, the firm will be spending money to generate leads that do not get answered promptly — and in personal injury, speed to response is one of the strongest predictors of whether a prospect signs.
The Gap
  • Every missed call or slow follow-up is a potential case lost — at an average case value of $5,000–$8,000 per PI matter, even three missed inquiries per month is $15,000–$24,000 in annual revenue that never materialized
  • Without an intake process, scaling marketing spend will produce diminishing returns — more leads, same conversion rate, same bottleneck
  • Personal injury prospects are often shopping multiple firms simultaneously — the first attorney who responds professionally and promptly wins the case
Building an intake system that converts without Eric being present is not a nice-to-have — it is the prerequisite for every hour of freedom he is working toward.

  • No live chat or chatbot was observed on the site — after-hours inquiries from prospective PI clients have no automated response pathway

Team Assessment

Eric Rhodes is running a $500,000 practice as a one-man operation — managing legal work, client communications, intake, and operations entirely on his own. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
"Previous hires were not 'A-players' and required too much hand-holding, which is impossible given Rhodes's compressed schedule."
The Problem
  • Eric is the only attorney and the only staff member — every case, every call, every filing depends on him personally
  • The firm's hiring budget ($60–70K) is far below the market rate for an experienced paralegal with TX/Federal civil procedure expertise ($110–115K) — making it nearly impossible to attract A-players at current revenue levels
  • Previous hires without proper SOPs, training, and accountability structures failed to deliver — the problem was not the people, it was the system they were hired into
  • Without a team that can manage cases, discovery, and client communication independently, Eric cannot take on more cases, cannot step back, and cannot exit on his timeline
Blind Spot: The salary gap is real, but it is a solvable problem — not a permanent ceiling. Doubling revenue to $1M creates the budget to hire at market rate. The sequence is: build the lead system first, grow the revenue, then hire the team the firm actually needs. Trying to hire first without the revenue is the trap Eric is in right now.
The Positive
  • Eric has already identified exactly what he needs — an experienced paralegal proficient in TX/Federal Rules of Civil Procedure and Evidence, with PI and commercial litigation background — that clarity makes the hiring process faster once the budget exists
  • Eric's AI toolstack (Type Less, BriefPoint, Lexi, Fathom's Nexus Protégé) has made him highly efficient as a solo operator — those tools will multiply in value once a team is in place to leverage them
The Gap
  • Every hour Eric spends on discovery, pleadings, and client communications is an hour he is not doing high-value trial work or business development — the ceiling on his output is fixed until he has support
  • Without SOPs and a trained support team, any new hire will require the same hand-holding that previous hires did — creating the same outcome
  • The exit strategy Eric described — stepping back to 2–3 cases at $2M+ — requires a team that runs cases, not an attorney who does everything and has one assistant
The team Eric needs to hire is not affordable at $500K — but it becomes affordable at $1M, and that is exactly what this plan is designed to produce.

Profit Plan Assessment

Eric has clear revenue awareness — $500K current, $1M goal, and a $2M+ long-term exit vision — but no confirmed profit plan for tracking what the firm actually keeps, what each case type costs to deliver, or how to protect margins while scaling. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No profit plan, financial KPIs, or visibility into net income confirmed on the call — revenue is known, but what stays after expenses is unclear
  • The firm's case mix (PI base + whale cases + commercial litigation) has highly variable profitability — without tracking cost per case type, time investment, and overhead allocation, growth could favor the least profitable work
  • Scaling from $500K to $1M will require significant investment in marketing, staffing, and operations — without a profit plan, expenses may grow faster than revenue
  • The exit strategy Eric described ($2M+ in 8–10 years while personally handling only 2–3 cases) requires a firm that is structurally profitable, not just busy — that outcome requires deliberate profit planning starting now
The Positive
  • Eric has a specific, quantified revenue goal ($1M in 12 months) and a clear long-term vision ($2M+ exit) — that clarity is the foundation of a profit plan, not a substitute for one
The Gap
  • Without knowing what the firm keeps per case type, Eric cannot make data-driven decisions about which cases to prioritize, which to decline, and where to invest marketing budget for the highest return
  • The difference between $500K revenue and $500K in actual personal income is everything — knowing the number is not the same as understanding what drives it or how to protect it
  • An exit valuation in 8–10 years is directly tied to demonstrable, predictable profit — a firm that cannot show clean financials is worth far less than one that can
The lifestyle Eric is building toward — fewer hours, more money, and a clean exit — is built on profit, not revenue. The plan starts now.
