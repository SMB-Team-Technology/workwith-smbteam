Intake Assessment

Sarah is doing most of the intake herself between cases, which she named on the call as the bottleneck on both her time and her close rate. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
"I want to not be the one doing intake. I want a team that handles that."
The Problem
  • Sarah personally handles most intake calls — no dedicated intake person on the team of four
  • Office hours are 9–5 only — calls outside that window go to voicemail and the prospect often calls a competitor before morning
  • 40% close rate is the ceiling while an attorney is doing intake between cases, instead of a trained intake function working off a defined script and follow-up sequence
  • No stated CRM or systematic follow-up cadence for leads who do not sign on the first contact
Blind Spot: Family law and estate planning searches spike in evenings and weekends — the exact hours Hartwell & Monroe is closed. Every after-hours caller who lands in voicemail is a paid or referred lead competitors can recapture by morning.
The Gap
  • At ~12 cases/month and a $4,600 blended case value, lifting the close rate from 40% to 55% with a dedicated intake function adds roughly $20,000–$25,000 in monthly revenue from leads the firm is already getting
  • Every intake call Sarah takes is one she pulls away from her associates, her cases, or her kids — the unit economics of attorney-time-as-intake-time are upside down
  • Without after-hours capture, the most expensive leads to acquire (evening/weekend high-intent searchers) are the ones most likely to be lost
Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Team Assessment

Hartwell & Monroe has two associates and one paralegal — a base, but no one is dedicated to intake, operations, or marketing, and Sarah is the de facto operations function. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
"I want the firm to run without me there every day."
The Problem
  • Team of four including Sarah — two associates and one paralegal, no dedicated intake, ops, or marketing role
  • Owner is doing intake, operations, marketing oversight, and casework — four jobs in one person
  • No stated org chart, KPIs, or accountability rhythm for the existing team
  • Sarah named being away from the firm as the thing that does not work — meaning the firm currently depends on her physical presence
Blind Spot: The next hire is almost always assumed to be another associate, but the highest-ROI hire for Hartwell & Monroe right now is intake — a trained intake person frees Sarah's hours immediately and lifts close rate at the same time.
The Positive
  • A team of four is already a foundation most Stage 3 firms do not have
  • Sarah has already named the freedom outcome explicitly — that clarity makes structural changes much faster to land
The Gap
  • Without an intake function and an operations function, every revenue increase pushes more work onto Sarah, not less
  • "Real vacation without my phone blowing up" is structurally impossible while there is no one running the firm in her absence
  • $1.2M with the current team structure looks like Sarah working harder, not differently
Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

Hartwell & Monroe is at $680K in annual revenue with a stated target of $1.2M in 18 months and monthly revenue Sarah described as "some months are great, some are really slow." Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"Some months are great, some are really slow."
The Problem
  • Monthly revenue swings widely — the predictable consequence of a referral-only pipeline with no paid channel smoothing the flow
  • No stated visibility into cost-per-case-acquired, channel ROI, or take-home margin
  • $1.2M target in 18 months — clear ambition, no current plan that converts that target into a monthly revenue, case-count, and ad-spend operating plan
  • Family law and estate planning are not priced or tracked separately by margin — Sarah wants the estate side to grow precisely because it is "steadier," but that decision is being made on feel, not data
Blind Spot: Estate planning at $2,500 per package looks lower-revenue than $4,000–$7,000 family law work, but with the right intake and a flat-fee structure it is almost always higher per-hour profitable — a fact that should shape both marketing budget and team time allocation.
The Gap
  • Without monthly P&L visibility, hitting $1.2M can mean more revenue and less take-home — exactly the trap Sarah's DBM is built to avoid
  • Without channel-level ROI tracking, marketing dollars are scored on gut feel — which is why a $1,500/month Facebook attempt was abandoned without diagnosing whether the channel or the funnel was the problem
  • Without a target case mix, the firm cannot consciously shift toward the "steadier" estate planning work Sarah said she wanted more of
A profit plan is what makes sure the $1.2M target translates into more time with Sarah's kids — not more nights at the office.
