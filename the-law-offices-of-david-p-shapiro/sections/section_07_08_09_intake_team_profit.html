Intake Assessment

David B. Shapiro is personally handling all intake for the firm — including calls that come in at 2 AM — while simultaneously managing 150 active PI cases and running the business. There is no coverage system, no delegation structure, and no after-hours intake pathway for the high-urgency criminal defense and PI cases that arrive outside business hours. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
"I'm getting calls at 2 in the morning."
The Problem
  • The lead attorney personally handles all intake — including 2 AM calls — with no delegation to support staff and no defined process for qualifying and converting inquiries when David is unavailable
  • Weekend PI and criminal defense cases are a confirmed recurring gap — the highest-urgency prospects (arrest calls, accident victims) contact the firm during off-hours, and there is no system to capture or immediately respond to those contacts
  • When the office manager is out sick, intake coverage drops further — the firm has no backup coverage protocol and no redundancy in the system
  • The website has no confirmed contact form, no after-hours digital intake path, and no mechanism for a prospect to submit an inquiry and receive a response — digital leads arriving outside business hours are simply lost
Blind Spot: The cases David is missing on weekends and evenings are not low-value inquiries — they are criminal arrests and car accidents, which are typically the most urgent and most emotionally motivated calls a firm receives. The prospects in those moments call whoever answers. Right now, whoever answers is not this firm.
The Gap
  • Every missed after-hours or weekend call is a case that a competitor answered first — in criminal defense, the family that calls three firms and gets one answer retains that firm before morning
  • The attorney's capacity to handle intake personally is finite — as more PI cases resolve and new leads arrive, this bottleneck will prevent the firm from scaling without David working more hours, not fewer
  • Without a documented intake process, there is no way to measure how many leads are being lost, what the close rate is, or how much revenue is leaking between initial contact and retained status
An intake system that works without David means the 2 AM calls stop — and the cases those calls represent start closing at a predictable rate whether David is in the office, at the Maine house, or at the restaurant.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • No contact form was confirmed accessible on the website due to the active TLS certificate error — the site cannot receive digital inquiries if the browser security warning prevents visitors from loading the page
  • No live chat or chat widget was observed on the website — prospects in urgent situations (post-arrest, post-accident) who reach the site after hours have no immediate way to initiate contact other than calling a number that may go unanswered
  • Fixing the website's HTTPS issue and adding an above-the-fold contact form with a clear after-hours promise ("We respond within 1 hour") would immediately convert more of the referral traffic the firm is already generating

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

David has a support team in place — one good admin, a few part-time staff, and an office manager — but by his own description, it is a "C or D team," and there is no associate attorney to share the legal workload. The firm's 60–70 hour weeks are almost entirely David's hours, and approximately 80% of that work is tasks he identified as delegable to the right people. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
"I have a C or D team. I need an A team."
The Problem
  • The current support staff operates at a level that David cannot fully trust to delegate critical tasks to — his own assessment of "C or D team" means every important task still requires his involvement or oversight
  • No associate attorneys — all legal work flows through David personally, meaning the firm's legal capacity is bounded by one person's hours and the 150-case PI backlog cannot be efficiently resolved without another attorney sharing the load
  • No management layer, no accountability structure, and no defined performance standards — without these, building an A-team is impossible because there is no framework for measuring or improving team performance
  • The office manager is currently out sick, and when that happens, the firm's operational coverage drops with no backup plan — a single point of failure in a role that should have redundancy
Blind Spot: The reason the team has not improved is not a hiring problem alone — it is a systems problem. Without documented processes, defined expectations, and an accountability structure, even an A-player hire will underperform because there is no framework for them to operate within. Building the right system first means the next hire delivers results instead of creating more management work for David.
The Positive
  • David has clearly identified the gap himself — he knows he needs an A-team and has articulated that approximately 80% of his current workload is delegable. This level of self-awareness is the starting point for a real team transformation.
The Gap
  • As long as David is the ceiling of the team's performance, the firm's revenue is capped at what one attorney can personally manage — and the 150-case PI backlog is proof that the current structure has already hit that ceiling
  • Without a team that can run intake, case management, and client communication independently, every hour David spends on delegable work is an hour he is not doing the high-value work that only he can do — and not spending time on the life he described wanting
  • Competitors who have built trained intake and support teams are resolving cases faster, converting more leads, and building the capacity to grow while the owner has more freedom — this is the gap between where David is and where he wants to be
The A-team David described is not just a staffing goal — it is the structural prerequisite for spending summers in Maine and giving attention to the restaurant without the firm falling apart the moment he steps away.

Profit Plan Assessment

Revenue at The Law Offices of David B. Shapiro fluctuates between $15,000 and $70,000 per month depending entirely on case settlement timing — not on decisions David makes, not on systems he has built, and not on a plan he has laid out. The firm has 150 active PI cases in the backlog that represent significant potential revenue, but that revenue cannot be reliably converted until the team capacity problem is solved. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Monthly revenue swings from $15,000 to $70,000 with no predictability — profit arrives when cases settle, not according to any plan, which makes financial planning, team investment, and personal income impossible to budget with confidence
  • No financial visibility was described — no tracking of profit by practice area, no owner compensation structure, and no system for understanding whether PI cases, criminal defense cases, or other matters are most profitable per hour of attorney time
  • The firm's stated revenue goal of $100,000 per month is achievable within the existing caseload — but only once team capacity allows cases to be resolved faster and intake starts converting without the bottleneck of David personally handling everything
  • Without knowing which case types generate the best return on David's time, the firm has no basis for making decisions about which marketing channels to fund, which referral sources to cultivate, or which cases to prioritize
Blind Spot: The 150-case PI backlog is the most significant financial asset this firm currently holds — but it is not being managed as an asset. At a conservative $25,000 average settlement value, the backlog represents $3.75M in potential revenue. Without a system to track case progress, project resolution timelines, and prioritize the highest-value matters, that potential converts to actual profit slowly and unpredictably instead of strategically.
The Positive
  • The firm's revenue potential is not a market problem — David has confirmed a $100K/month goal is achievable with the existing caseload, which means the path to that number runs through operations and team, not through finding new clients
The Gap
  • Without a profit plan, revenue growth from resolving more cases or adding marketing spend may not translate to more owner take-home — it may simply fund higher overhead and more staff without improving David's financial position
  • Without case-level financial tracking, the firm cannot optimize its intake screening, marketing spend, or practice area mix to focus on the most profitable work — every case is treated the same even if some cases generate 10× the profit per hour
  • The current lumpy revenue pattern makes it impossible to plan major investments — in marketing, in hiring, or in David's personal financial goals — with any confidence in the numbers behind those plans
A profit plan converts the $100K/month goal from a number David is working toward into a number he is planning for — and that is the difference between an owner who is always chasing revenue and an owner who knows exactly what it takes to step back.
