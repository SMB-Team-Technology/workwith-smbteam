Intake Assessment

Fletcher Estate Planning has built one of the most effective consultation-to-close processes in the market — a 79% conversion rate that reflects Jim's expertise, warmth, and deep trust with prospects. But with no formal intake infrastructure and no after-hours coverage, the firm is almost certainly losing leads that never reach Jim at all. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
"I'm considering charging a fee — maybe $99 — to get people to commit so they don't no-show."
The Problem
  • Jim personally handles every consultation — there is no delegated intake function, no intake coordinator, and no process that runs without him
  • No after-hours coverage identified — prospects who call evenings or weekends reach voicemail with no defined follow-up protocol to recapture them
  • No-shows are a confirmed friction point — time blocked for consultations that do not materialize represents direct revenue loss at $3,000 per client
  • No documented lead follow-up sequence — prospects who do not immediately book or sign have no structured re-engagement path
Blind Spot: Jim's 79% close rate is calculated against prospects who actually make it to a scheduled consultation. The more important unknown is how many high-intent leads call after hours, reach voicemail, and call a competitor before morning — those losses are invisible in any close-rate metric and may represent as much revenue as the no-show problem Jim is already trying to solve.
The Positive
  • A 79% consult-to-client conversion rate is exceptional — roughly three times the industry average for estate planning — and a genuine competitive asset that paid advertising will immediately amplify
  • Jim's instinct to introduce a consultation fee shows sound business thinking: filtering prospects by commitment level is a proven tactic to reduce no-shows without reducing quality
The Gap
  • Every missed after-hours call that goes unrecaptured represents a potential $3,000 trust plan client — at current close rates, just five missed leads per month is $15,000 in lost monthly revenue
  • When paid advertising drives higher lead volume, the intake bottleneck will surface immediately — Jim cannot simultaneously run consultations and respond to new inquiries, and without a system in place, ad spend will generate leads the firm cannot convert
  • Without a follow-up sequence for unconverted leads, prospects who do not book on first contact are effectively lost — a structured email or text re-engagement sequence recovers a measurable percentage of these at zero additional cost
Jim's close rate is already a superpower — the goal now is ensuring that superpower gets the chance to work on every qualified prospect, not just the ones who happen to call during business hours.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Website returned access errors during automated research — contact form configuration and live chat presence could not be directly verified; findings are sourced from search metadata and directory data
  • No live chat widget observed in search metadata or directory profiles — prospects who visit after hours have no immediate engagement option and must either call (voicemail) or submit a form with no guaranteed response time
  • Free webinar registration represents a positive low-friction entry point that captures prospect contact information before a consultation is requested — this is an asset that could be better integrated into a follow-up automation sequence

Team Assessment

Fletcher Estate Planning is a three-person family practice: Jim handles all client-facing work and strategy, daughter Sarah provides paralegal support, and a second daughter joins in July. This is the right foundation — but Jim remains the hub of every key decision and every client interaction. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Jim is the sole revenue-generating function — all client consultations, all high-touch decisions, and all relationship management flow through him personally
  • No intake coordinator or client services role exists — both paralegal team members handle document support, not lead qualification or appointment management
  • The firm has no operations function that runs independently of Jim — if Jim is in a consultation, there is no one qualified to handle a new prospect inquiry simultaneously
  • Both support staff are family members, which creates natural boundaries around delegation, accountability, and the ability to assign management responsibility as the firm grows
Blind Spot: Jim has already demonstrated the instinct to delegate by outsourcing plan drafting — yet the same logic has not been applied to intake and follow-up. The most direct path to a self-managing firm is not hiring more attorneys; it is building the intake and client coordination layer that allows Jim to focus exclusively on consultations, where his close rate makes every hour worth $3,000.
The Positive
  • Outsourcing plan drafting to a third-party service at $300–$400 per plan shows Jim already thinks like a business operator — protecting his time by delegating production work is exactly the mindset that scales
  • Proactively bringing on a second paralegal before the growth push begins shows forward planning — the team is being built ahead of demand rather than in reaction to it
The Gap
  • A solo-attorney bottleneck means Jim's income is directly capped by his available hours — at $3,000 per client and a finite number of consultation slots, there is a hard ceiling on what he can produce alone
  • When paid advertising scales lead volume, the absence of an intake coordinator means Jim will spend time triaging new inquiries instead of running the $3,000 consultations he has already mastered
  • The path from $420,000 to $840,000 requires Jim to work smarter, not just harder — and that means building a coordination layer around him so every hour he works is a high-value revenue hour
The goal is a firm where Jim shows up for consultations and the rest runs without him — that is the version of Fletcher Estate Planning that hits $840k and keeps growing without Jim trading more hours for more income.

Profit Plan Assessment

Jim has clear visibility into his revenue — $420,000 projected this year, $840,000 as the goal — and a disciplined approach to pricing his services at $2,000–$3,000 per client. What is less clear is whether the path to that goal includes a tracked profit strategy and a cost structure built to handle paid advertising. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No financial dashboard or profit tracking system was mentioned on the call — revenue is known but margin by service type and take-home as a percentage of revenue are not tracked at a granular level
  • Adding paid advertising introduces a new cost category (management fees + ad spend) that must be managed against margin targets — without a profit plan in place, scaling ad investment can compress take-home rather than grow it
  • Drafting costs at scale ($300–$400 per plan) represent a significant variable expense that grows with volume — at 100+ plans per year, this exceeds $35,000 annually before overhead, and without tracking it, profitability can erode invisibly
  • No mention of a cost-per-acquisition target or margin floor — without these benchmarks, it is impossible to evaluate whether a given marketing channel is actually profitable at the case level
Blind Spot: At Jim's current revenue of $420,000 and outsourced drafting costs of $300–$400 per plan, a significant portion of gross revenue flows out before a single overhead expense is paid. If plan volume doubles to hit $840,000, so does that cost — potentially adding $35,000–$40,000 in drafting expenses alone. Without a profit plan that models this, doubling revenue does not guarantee doubling take-home.
The Positive
  • Revenue metrics are clearly tracked — $144,000 YTD at the time of the call and a clear annual projection of $420,000 shows Jim monitors his top-line performance closely
  • Single-service focus on estate planning creates a clean, predictable revenue model — one practice area, one primary package price, one client type — which is the simplest foundation for building a profit plan
The Gap
  • Doubling revenue to $840,000 will not automatically double take-home — without a profit plan that accounts for rising variable costs (drafting, advertising, additional team), net income growth may significantly lag revenue growth
  • Marketing ROI cannot be optimized without cost-per-lead and cost-per-acquisition data — knowing that an ad campaign brought in 10 clients means little without knowing what those 10 clients cost to acquire versus what they returned
  • Without financial visibility at the case level, it is impossible to know which service offerings, client sources, or geographic markets are the most profitable — and therefore which to scale
Jim's goal is not just $840,000 in revenue — it is $840,000 that translates to real financial freedom, and that requires a clear view of what actually stays in the firm after every cost is paid.
