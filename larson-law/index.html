Here Is Where Larson Law Stands Today

Larson Law is a South Jordan, Utah personal injury firm with nearly four decades of history, now under the sole leadership of Adam Larson following his father's retirement in March 2026. Built almost entirely on referrals, the firm generates approximately $1.1 million per year with no predictable marketing system, no Google Ads presence, and a near-zero Google review footprint — while competitors with 300 to 1,500+ Google reviews actively capture every PI case that begins with an online search.

Stage 6 is where the firm runs without you at every client signing, your income is no longer tied to your personal availability, and the financial security you have been working toward finally becomes real — and that is exactly where this is taking you.

Where You Are Today

Stage 3: Solo Practitioner
  • Revenue flat at ~$1.1M/year for five consecutive years — driven entirely by unpredictable referral flow
  • Adam personally signs every new client — a structural bottleneck that caps the firm's case volume
  • 95–98% of cases come from referrals with no controlled marketing system and no measurable online presence
  • Current marketing spend of ~$2–3K/month on FindLaw and social posting produces no tracked results

Where You Need To Be

Stage 6: Law Firm Owner
  • The firm runs without you
  • Your income is no longer tied to your personal hours
  • Your time is yours to spend however you choose
  • This is where true freedom and true wealth become possible
The gap between where you are and where you want to be is a systems gap, not a talent gap — you have already proven you can run a great personal injury firm; now it is time to build one that grows without requiring you at every step.

The Law Firm Growth Acceleration Model

We Help Law Firm Owners Get More Profit, More Personal Freedom, And Outstanding Results For Their Clients.

Most law firms try to grow by chasing more leads. But leads alone are not the problem — and leads alone are not the answer. Here is what actually works.

Lead Generation

Leads are to a law firm what gasoline is to a car. Without them, you go nowhere. But a steady, predictable flow of leads changes everything — you get to choose your cases instead of taking whatever comes in.

Intake

But all the leads in the world are worthless without a system to turn them into paying clients. Most firms are losing cases they already paid for simply because their follow-up is slow or inconsistent.

Self-Managing Team

Once clients are coming in, you need a team — otherwise you end up working 90-hour weeks and results suffer. A self-managing team is what gives you the freedom to step back.

Profit Plan

And once the team is in place, most owners look at their bank account and ask: where did all the money go? That is what a profit plan solves — clarity on what you earn, what you keep, and how to grow both.

All four have to work together. If one is missing, you cannot have more profit, more freedom, and outstanding results for your clients at the same time.

Intake Assessment

Larson Law's intake is entirely dependent on Adam — every new client must be personally signed by him as required by the Utah State Bar, with no after-hours coverage and no defined follow-up system for leads who don't sign immediately. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Adam must personally sign every new client — a Utah State Bar requirement that creates a hard ceiling on how many cases the firm can take in any given month, regardless of how many leads come in
  • No after-hours intake coverage — the office closes at 5pm Monday through Thursday and at 3pm on Fridays, meaning any accident victim who calls outside those hours gets no response and is likely to call a competitor before morning
  • No defined intake system or structured follow-up sequence for leads who do not sign on the first call — prospects who need more time to decide have no process keeping them engaged
  • No intake tracking or lead management system — there is no way to know how many leads contacted the firm this month, how many signed, and how many were lost and why
Blind Spot: The personal signature requirement feels like a compliance constraint, but it is also an operational design choice — many PI firms solve this with a hybrid model where trained intake staff qualify and close leads, with the attorney signing same-day via a streamlined review process rather than being the first point of contact on every call. There may be more flexibility in how Adam fulfills this requirement than he currently believes.
The Positive
  • Adam's personal involvement in every client engagement means new clients feel heard and cared for from the first interaction — a trust signal that referral-based clients especially notice
  • The 6–8 month case cycle and consistent referral base mean the firm has a predictable rhythm even without a formal intake process — this rhythm becomes a foundation to build on rather than something to replace
The Gap
  • Every hour Adam spends as the first point of contact on intake calls is an hour he cannot spend in the CEO role — as marketing drives more inbound leads, the intake bottleneck becomes the firm's limiting constraint, not the lead volume
  • After-hours leads are the highest-intent prospects in personal injury — accident victims call immediately after an accident, not the next business day; without coverage, these calls go directly to Kramer Law Group and Siegfried & Jensen
  • Without a follow-up sequence, leads who do not sign on day one are effectively abandoned — in a practice area where the decision cycle can span days, this is a systematic revenue leak with no current way to measure or stop it
Adam described wanting to step out of the day-to-day and operate as a CEO — but as long as every signed case requires his personal involvement at intake, that transition cannot happen. Intake is not just a revenue problem; it is the structural barrier between where he is now and the freedom he is building toward.

Profit Plan Assessment

Larson Law generates approximately $1.1 million per year, but revenue has been flat for five consecutive years — and with average case fees declining from $13,000 to $11,000 this year, the firm is earning less per case while costs continue to rise. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Revenue has been flat at approximately $1.1 million for five consecutive years — the firm has not grown, and inflation and rising costs mean the real value of that revenue is declining each year
  • Average case fee has dropped from approximately $13,000 to approximately $11,000 this year — a trend that erodes margin case by case without any visible warning signal unless someone is tracking it
  • Marketing spend of $2–3K/month on FindLaw directory listings and social posting produces no tracked results — the firm is spending money with no measurement, no attribution, and no way to know what, if anything, it is returning
  • The need to consider a line of credit to fund marketing investment suggests limited financial reserves — a firm generating $1.1M per year that needs external financing to invest in growth likely has a profit margin and reserve problem that growth alone will not solve
Blind Spot: Declining average case value is a margin problem that marketing cannot fix. If the mix of cases is shifting toward lower-value matters — or if settlement outcomes are declining — doubling revenue while the average case fee continues to fall means running harder just to stay even. Understanding why the average dropped by $2,000 per case this year is one of the most important financial questions the firm needs to answer before scaling.
The Positive
  • Adam knows his key numbers — revenue, average case value, case cycle time, and active case count — which gives him a baseline that many solo practitioners do not have and a foundation to build financial planning from
  • The 6–8 month case cycle is relatively short for personal injury, which creates a faster cash flow cycle than firms handling multi-year litigation — this is a structural profit advantage worth protecting and optimizing
The Gap
  • Without a profit plan that accounts for the declining average case fee, growth in case volume may not produce proportional growth in take-home income — more cases at $11K is not the same as more cases at $13K, and the gap compounds at scale
  • Without marketing attribution, the firm cannot distinguish between spend that produces cases and spend that produces nothing — the $2–3K/month going to FindLaw may be dead weight, but there is currently no data to confirm or redirect it
  • Without financial reserves, the firm has limited ability to invest aggressively in marketing during the months where it would make the most difference — a line of credit can bridge the gap, but only if the underlying profit model can service the investment
Growing revenue without growing profit is just growing the grind — Adam has described wanting financial security, the ability to retire comfortably, and the freedom that comes from a firm that runs itself. None of those outcomes are possible without a clear profit plan running alongside the growth plan.
