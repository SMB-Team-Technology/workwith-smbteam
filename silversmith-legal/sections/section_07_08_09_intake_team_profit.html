Intake Assessment

Silversmith Legal has no digital intake infrastructure — no contact form, no after-hours coverage, and no observed intake process beyond a phone number and email address on a static contact page. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • The website contact page lists only an email address and phone number — no embedded contact form, no online scheduling link, and no way to submit an inquiry at any hour without making a direct call or sending an email
  • No after-hours contact coverage is observable — prospects who visit the site outside of business hours have no way to initiate contact digitally, and calls that go to voicemail in a moment of need rarely result in a callback
  • No intake process, intake scripts, or lead tracking system has been documented for handling prospects who do not book a consultation on first contact
  • The above-fold CTA on the website links to the attorney biography page rather than to a contact or intake step — high-intent visitors have no clear path to becoming a client from the homepage
Blind Spot: Estate planning and probate clients frequently research attorneys late at night or on weekends — after a parent's health crisis, a spouse's unexpected death, or a sudden realization that their own affairs are not in order. If the website cannot capture an inquiry in that exact moment, that prospect will call whoever they can reach at 9am the next morning — which is almost always someone who runs ads and has a contact form that worked the night before.
The Gap
  • Every prospect who visits the website outside business hours and cannot submit an inquiry is a lead that converts to a competitor — for a probate practice focused on urgency cases, these after-hours moments are often the highest-intent touchpoints in the entire funnel
  • Without a defined follow-up sequence, leads who call and do not immediately book an appointment have no second chance — they go cold, and the cost of every ad click that generated them is wasted
  • With probate cases that often start in crisis — a family losing a loved one and needing to act quickly — fast and accessible intake is not a nice-to-have; it is the difference between getting the case and losing it to the first attorney who answers
Fixing intake is what turns Amy's available capacity into actual revenue — and it is the single highest-ROI improvement the firm can make without spending another dollar on marketing.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • No contact form is present anywhere on the Silversmith Legal website — the contact page provides only an email address (admin@silversmithlegal.com) and a phone number (970-306-6449)
  • No live chat widget was observed on any page of the website
  • The absence of a contact form makes it impossible to track, respond to, or follow up on web-generated inquiries at scale — and prevents any lead capture during evenings, weekends, and holidays when estate planning and probate research frequently occurs

Team Assessment

Silversmith Legal has a three-person team in place — Chris, Amy, and Stephanie — which gives the firm more capacity than a solo practitioner, but Chris remains the essential driver of all business development and the only attorney currently capable of handling probate cases independently. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Amy handles estate planning volume but is still on a probate learning curve — Chris must remain personally involved in probate case delivery, which limits how much he can step back from day-to-day practice
  • No intake or business development role exists on the team — Chris and Amy are both focused on service delivery, and Stephanie handles administrative support; no one is focused on converting new prospects into clients
  • No documented intake scripts, case processes, or operational playbooks that transfer knowledge to the team independently of any individual — if Amy left tomorrow, the estate planning practice would lose its entire service capacity
  • The firm's website is still being updated to feature Amy — basic team visibility infrastructure is still being built out while the firm simultaneously plans a market expansion
Blind Spot: The way the team is currently structured, if Amy leaves, the estate planning practice loses its entire service capacity — and if Chris is unavailable, probate stops. A self-managing firm needs documented processes that transfer knowledge regardless of who is on the team, so that the firm's capabilities are not dependent on any single individual being present and healthy every day.
The Positive
  • Amy handling estate planning volume already creates meaningful capacity separation from Chris — this is the most important structural step a growing firm can take, and it is already in motion
  • Two-office presence across two Colorado valley markets signals growth intent and gives the firm a geographic footprint that most competitors in this market do not have
The Gap
  • Chris cannot step away from probate cases today without those matters going unserviced — until Amy can handle probate independently or a third attorney is added, Chris remains the firm's only probate resource and cannot take true time off
  • Without documented intake and case delivery processes, scaling the team means building from scratch at each new hire rather than onboarding into a system that already works
  • The freedom Chris described — working on the firm rather than in it — requires a team that can run client delivery end-to-end without his presence; right now, that capability does not exist in either practice area
The team Chris has built is the right start — but Amy running estate planning while Chris focuses on selective oversight is only fully possible when there are documented systems underneath them both, not just good people relying on good intentions.

Profit Plan Assessment

Silversmith Legal generates approximately $750,000–$850,000 annually from its legal practice, with additional revenue from RIA-related activity — but with no stated revenue goal, no consolidated profit view by practice area, and a probate fee structure still being refined, the firm is growing by feel rather than by plan. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No revenue goal was stated on the call — the firm is growing reactively rather than toward a defined target, which makes it impossible to size marketing investment correctly or know whether it is producing the right outcome
  • Probate billing model is still evolving — the flat fee to open ($4,500) plus a retainer (~$5,000) model is being tested, and whether Colorado allows percentage-based probate fees is still unconfirmed; inconsistent fee structures make margin planning difficult
  • Revenue spans both legal practice and RIA-related activity without a clear breakdown of what the law firm itself generates in net profit — making it difficult to measure the true return on any marketing or operational investment
  • No cost-per-case or cost-per-lead baseline has been established — when digital marketing launches, there will be no historical data to measure ROI against, making early campaign decisions reactive rather than informed
Blind Spot: Adding significant marketing investment without first establishing a baseline financial model — revenue per case type, cost per case type, margin per practice area — means Chris will not be able to determine whether the marketing is profitable until months of data have accumulated. Setting a profit-per-case target for both probate and estate planning now transforms every marketing decision from a guess into a measurable bet.
The Positive
  • Revenue of $750K–$850K from a three-person practice represents strong per-attorney productivity — the firm generates real money relative to its size
  • Multiple revenue streams (legal practice + RIA-related) provide some income diversification, which is a structural advantage many law firms do not have
The Gap
  • Without a defined revenue goal, there is no frame of reference for whether the marketing investment is appropriately sized — Chris could over-invest and dilute margins, or under-invest and miss the growth window entirely
  • Without knowing the profit margin per case type, it is impossible to determine with certainty whether probate or estate planning deserves the larger share of the marketing budget — or whether both are equally worth scaling
  • The financial freedom Chris is building this firm to deliver requires predictable, planned profit — not profit that is discovered at year-end after expenses are tallied; right now, profit is what is left over, not what is designed for
Knowing exactly what the firm earns, what it keeps, and what each case costs to acquire is what transforms revenue growth into the personal freedom Chris is building this firm to achieve — and that clarity starts with setting a target and tracking it weekly.
