Website Audit

The Problem
  • PageSpeed scores could not be captured live in this research pass (tooling quota limit) — a live mobile and desktop PageSpeed run is recommended before finalizing scope, since load speed on mobile directly affects conversion for a phone-first practice like this
  • Only 5 of the 9 practice areas marketed on the homepage (wrongful death, traumatic brain injury, pedestrian injury, and drowning are missing) have their own dedicated page — a real content and keyword-targeting gap
  • No geo-targeted landing pages exist for any of the surrounding St. Louis cities, despite the firm positioning itself as serving the broader metro
  • Minimal attorney bio — no detailed credentials, background, or headshot for Jamil M. Naji surfaced on the site
Blind Spot: Four of the case types Naji Injury Law actively markets on its own homepage — wrongful death, TBI, pedestrian injury, and drowning — have no dedicated page for Google to rank. Every one of those is a keyword competitors are capturing by default.
The Positive
  • Site is HTTPS-secured with a clean "Free Consultation" CTA and clickable phone number above the fold
  • Title tags are reasonably keyword-optimized ("Injury Attorney in Clayton, MO," "Personal Injury Law Firm in Missouri") and NAP is consistent across every directory checked
The Gap
  • Thin site architecture (11 total URLs) means most of the marketed practice areas cannot rank on their own merits
  • No geo pages means every neighboring St. Louis suburb is being ceded to firms that do have them
  • An unverified page-speed profile going into a proposal call is a real risk if mobile load times are slow — most prospects searching for an injury attorney are on their phones
Naji Injury Law cannot out-build Brown & Crouppen's 30-year web footprint overnight — but every missing page today is one more year before this site can carry the $3M firm Jamil is building toward.

Local Service Ads

LSA Presence
  • No live top-of-page placement data could be captured this session — Naji Injury Law's current LSA/Google Screened status should be confirmed live before the proposal call
  • The firm's prior 30-day PPC/LSA test was shut down due to lead quality concerns, which points to a setup and follow-up issue worth revisiting rather than a reason to avoid the channel
Competitor LSA Landscape
  • Brown & Crouppen — high-volume incumbents in this market typically hold consistent LSA placement given their scale and review volume
  • Schultz & Myers — a 4.9-star, high-review-count profile is exactly the profile Google favors for LSA placement and trust signals
The Opportunity
  • LSAs are pay-per-lead, not pay-per-click — a better fit for a firm whose intake already converts 88% of qualified leads, since every lead that comes through is more likely to become a client
  • LSAs appear above everything else on Google — above paid ads, above the map pack, above all organic results — and you only pay when a real prospect calls you directly.
  • Google Screened status would help close the credibility gap left by an unclaimed, unrated Avvo profile
Blind Spot: A pay-per-lead model pairs especially well with a firm whose bottleneck is lead volume, not intake performance — every LSA lead is more likely to convert here than it would at a firm with weaker intake.
