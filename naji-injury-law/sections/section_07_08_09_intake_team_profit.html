Intake Assessment

Intake is the one pillar at Naji Injury Law that is already working — the firm converts nearly nine out of every ten qualified leads it receives. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: GREEN
"High conversion rate (~88%) on qualified leads."
The Problem
  • Your strongest pillar is being starved by your weakest one — an 88% conversion rate has nothing to work with when the lead source in front of it has produced zero conversions since July 3
  • Your intake team's real capacity is unknown, because it has never been tested against a full, owned pipeline of qualified leads
  • You have no confirmed after-hours or overflow coverage in place to protect that 88% conversion rate as lead volume grows
  • You have no visibility yet into how this conversion rate would hold up once lead volume triples or quadruples
Blind Spot: An 88% conversion rate on today's trickle of leads is not the same as an 88% conversion rate on the volume a real, owned lead-gen system would send — you may not know yet whether your team can hold that rate at scale.
The Positive
  • A near-90% conversion rate on qualified leads is a genuinely strong result, and it is a direct product of recruiting an experienced, six-person team out of larger firms
  • This means the bottleneck holding the firm back is lead volume and quality — not the team's ability to close the leads it gets
The Gap
  • Every month the lead pipeline underperforms, an 88%-converting intake team sits underutilized — that is lost revenue this firm has already paid its team to be ready for
  • A conversion engine this strong is the exact reason a real lead-gen investment pays back faster here than at most firms
  • Without a documented after-hours process, growth in lead volume could outpace the team's current bandwidth to protect that conversion rate
Your intake team has already proven it can convert — what it has never had is enough leads to prove what it could really do for a $3M firm.

Team Assessment

Naji Injury Law has already recruited a six-person team out of larger firms — a genuine head start — but Jamil is still personally in the room for decisions as fundamental as who to hire next in marketing. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
"The goal is more return on hours, not fewer hours."
The Problem
  • Roles across the six-person team are not yet clearly broken out beyond Jamil (owner/attorney) and Laura (joint decision-maker on the marketing hire)
  • Jamil and Laura are personally holding a pending hiring decision until they see the growth plan and costs — a sign that key operational decisions still route through ownership
  • No dedicated marketing, ops, or intake lead has been confirmed — meaning growth initiatives currently depend on the owner's direct involvement to move forward
  • No leadership or management structure beneath ownership has been described yet
Blind Spot: Wanting more return on your hours rather than fewer hours is exactly what makes this urgent, not less — every hour you spend deciding who to hire in marketing is an hour not spent on the casework and client relationships only you can do.
The Positive
  • Recruiting a six-person team from larger firms in year two is a real accomplishment — most firms this age are still solo or working with one or two support staff
The Gap
  • Owner involvement in hiring and growth decisions costs Jamil the exact "return on hours" he says he wants
  • Without a dedicated marketing or ops lead, every new system this firm adds still funnels back through Jamil to get approved and run
  • Growth to $3M and $7M will only compound this bottleneck unless decision-making authority starts moving off of Jamil's desk
You said you want more return on your hours, not fewer of them — that only happens once your team can make the marketing and growth calls without waiting on you.

Profit Plan Assessment

Naji Injury Law is on track for $1M in fees this year with stated ambitions of $3M and eventually $7M — a clear revenue trajectory, but no margin or profitability visibility was discussed on the call. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"On track for $1M in fees."
The Problem
  • Revenue targets ($3M, then $7M) were discussed, but no margin, expense, or cost-per-lead figures were shared on the call
  • Roughly three-quarters of current lead flow is paid to a third-party vendor with no visibility yet into the true return on that spend versus what it costs the firm in lost brand equity
  • No stated plan yet for how added revenue from growth converts into owner take-home versus reinvestment
  • Growth to date has been funded without a documented profit plan behind it
Blind Spot: Chasing $3M and then $7M in revenue without a profit plan in place first means the firm could get bigger without Jamil actually taking home more — bigger is not automatically better.
The Positive
  • Revenue is growing on a clear trajectory with specific, stated near- and long-term targets — a firm with no direction would not have $3M and $7M as concrete goals
The Gap
  • Without margin visibility, it is impossible to know today whether the third-party lead vendor is actually profitable once its true cost is measured against brand-owned alternatives
  • Scaling revenue without a profit plan risks scaling expenses at the same pace, leaving take-home flat
  • A profit plan built now, while the firm is still at $1M, is far easier to course-correct than one built after reaching $3M
Getting to $3M and $7M only matters if what is left over at the end grows with it — a profit plan is what turns bigger revenue into the wealth and freedom you are actually building toward.
