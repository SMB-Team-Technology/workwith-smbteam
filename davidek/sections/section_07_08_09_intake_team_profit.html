Intake Assessment

Davidek's blended engagement/close rate has slipped from the high 80s to roughly 80% across Amber (~75%) and Robert (~90%), while a growing share of intake volume is now low-value "tire kicker" and AI-generated-document-review inquiries. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Overall engagement/close rate has fallen from the high 80s to about 80% blended, with individual rates ranging from ~75% to ~90% and no clear process to close that gap
  • Intake is absorbing a growing volume of "tire kicker" and AI-generated-document-review requests, with no defined process to screen or fast-track them
  • No contact form is visible above the fold on the website — prospects must call or email, with no other channel offered
  • No documented after-hours coverage exists, despite estate planning inquiries often coming from prospects researching outside business hours
Blind Spot: You may not realize how much of Amber and Robert's time each week goes to "tire kicker" and AI-document-review inquiries that were never going to become paying clients — time that could go toward the leads that will.
The Positive
  • Robert's ~90% engagement/close rate is a strong benchmark the rest of the intake process could be built around
  • A blended engagement rate around 80% is still well above what many firms in this practice area achieve
The Gap
  • Every point the blended rate slips below the "high 80s" it used to run at is leads already paid for walking away
  • Unfiltered "tire kicker" volume dilutes the time Amber and Robert have for the higher-net-worth prospects the firm says it wants to attract
  • No after-hours coverage means inquiries outside business hours risk going to whichever competitor answers first
A disciplined, filtered intake process protects the close rate that already funds this firm — and that reliability is exactly what makes a firm attractive to a future buyer.

Team Assessment

Davidek is staffed at 3 of 5 attorney seats after more than a year spent trying to hire two more attorneys, and the owner remains closely involved in diagnosing payroll and margin issues alongside a newly-engaged fractional CFO. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Attorney capacity is staffed at only 3 of 5 seats, a gap that has persisted for over a year despite active hiring efforts
  • Significant attorney time each month goes to auditing and redoing legacy estate plans drafted by a former attorney with performance issues — capacity pulled away from new client work
  • A time-tracking study is currently underway, which typically signals the firm does not yet have full visibility into how team time is actually being spent
  • No documented leadership or management layer exists between the owner and the attorney team beyond the newly-engaged fractional CFO on the finance side
Blind Spot: The two open attorney seats are not just a revenue problem — every month they stay open, the owner and the current team absorb more of the workload personally, which is the opposite of the freedom a sale-ready firm requires.
The Positive
  • Robert's ~90% engagement rate shows the current team, when staffed, performs at a high level
  • Engaging a fractional CFO and running a formal time-tracking study shows real intent to build financial and operational visibility rather than guess
The Gap
  • Every month the two attorney seats stay open is a month of case capacity — and revenue toward the $4M goal — the firm cannot capture
  • Owner and CFO time spent on diagnostics has not yet converted into an operating plan that reduces payroll pressure
  • Without a defined leadership structure beyond finance, day-to-day team management still likely runs through the owner
Closing the attorney staffing gap and building real management structure around the team you have is what turns Davidek from a firm the owner runs into an asset the owner could eventually sell.

Profit Plan Assessment

Payroll currently consumes more than 50% of Davidek's revenue against a stated 25% target, pushing profit margins into the low single digits even as the firm targets $4M in annual revenue by the end of 2027. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"Payroll costs exceed 50% of revenue (vs. a 25% goal), driving profit margins into the low single digits."
The Problem
  • Payroll costs exceed 50% of revenue against a 25-35% target range, and profit margins currently sit in the low single digits
  • Total revenue for the current year is projected to fall below last year's $2.7M due to the attorney staffing shortfall
  • The firm has stated a specific 20% profit margin target but has not yet implemented a plan to close the gap from where margins sit today
  • Growth to $4M by 2027 without first addressing the payroll-to-revenue ratio risks scaling the same margin problem to a larger number
Blind Spot: Hitting $4M in revenue without first fixing the payroll ratio would only make the profit problem bigger in dollar terms, not solve it.
The Positive
  • Engaging a fractional CFO and running a time-tracking study shows the firm already has real financial visibility — this is a firm that knows its numbers, not one flying blind
  • A specific, stated 20% profit margin target gives this plan a clear finish line to build toward
The Gap
  • Every point payroll sits above the 25-35% target is profit that never reaches the owner or funds the $4M growth plan
  • Without a documented plan connecting revenue growth to margin improvement, the $4M target could be reached without meaningfully increasing what the firm actually keeps
  • The value of a firm built to sell is driven by profit margin, not top-line revenue — this gap directly limits the enterprise value being built toward
Turning a stated 20% margin target into an implemented plan is what converts $4M in revenue into real enterprise value — the kind of firm that is actually worth building toward a sale.
