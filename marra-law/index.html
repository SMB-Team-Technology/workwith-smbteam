The Law Firm Growth Acceleration Model

We Help Law Firm Owners Get More Profit, More Personal Freedom, And Outstanding Results For Their Clients.

Most law firms try to grow by chasing more leads. But leads alone are not the problem — and leads alone are not the answer. Here is what actually works.

Lead Generation

Leads are to a law firm what gasoline is to a car. Without them, you go nowhere. But a steady, predictable flow of leads changes everything — you get to choose your cases instead of taking whatever comes in.

Intake

But all the leads in the world are worthless without a system to turn them into paying clients. Most firms are losing cases they already paid for simply because their follow-up is slow or inconsistent.

Self-Managing Team

Once clients are coming in, you need a team — otherwise you end up working 90-hour weeks and results suffer. A self-managing team is what gives you the freedom to step back.

Profit Plan

And once the team is in place, most owners look at their bank account and ask: where did all the money go? That is what a profit plan solves — clarity on what you earn, what you keep, and how to grow both.

All four have to work together. If one is missing, you cannot have more profit, more freedom, and outstanding results for your clients at the same time.

Intake Assessment

Marra Law is handling roughly 10 estate plan signings per month — with demand strong enough that signings are booked out to August — but the internal process for managing those clients from intake through completion is breaking down every time a staff member leaves, falls ill, or takes leave. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
"Clients stall for months with no firm follow-up; other clients want work faster but internal delays occur."
The Problem
  • Client follow-up breaks down completely when staff depart or take leave — there is no system that continues to move matters forward independent of which specific person is in the office that week
  • Documents arrive by email and print, are placed on desks, and are never uploaded to Clio — meaning matter records are incomplete and attorneys have no reliable way to check case status without asking someone directly
  • Clio client portal adoption is low — most clients are communicating by email and phone rather than through the system, creating scattered records and eliminating the possibility of automated status updates
  • There is no standardized intake-to-completion workflow — what happens next in a matter depends on who is handling it and whether that person is available, not on a defined process
Blind Spot: Clients who go months without hearing from the firm do not just leave negative reviews — they stop referring. For a firm built almost entirely on referrals and reputation, a single client who felt abandoned by the process is a permanent hole in the referral network that may never be visible on a spreadsheet but costs far more over time than any individual matter fee.
The Positive
  • Clio and Clio Draft are already in place — the firm is not starting from zero on practice management technology, and the infrastructure to build a proper workflow already exists
  • Estate planning fees are collected upfront before drafting begins — which means cash flow does not depend on case completion, and intake systems can focus on client experience rather than collections
The Gap
  • A client who goes months without contact is a client who calls your office upset — and Joe, already managing judicial duties, is the one who ends up on that call
  • Without a defined follow-up system, every staff departure creates an identical crisis: cases stall, clients go dark, and the owner has to personally intervene to assess where things stand
  • The firm is currently handling 10 estate plan signings per month with signings booked to August — meaning demand already exceeds throughput capacity, and without a system, scaling that volume will only create more operational breakdown, not less
A firm where Joe personally rescues intake every time a staff member leaves is not a firm that can run without him — and that is exactly the freedom he is looking for.

Profit Plan Assessment

Marra Law handles a volume of estate plans, probate matters, family law cases, and personal injury files that suggests healthy gross revenue — but without a clear profit plan, that revenue is not being intentionally converted into personal income and firm growth. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Revenue was not discussed on the discovery call — there is no clear number on the table for what the firm earns, what Joe takes home, or what the gap between them looks like
  • Aged probate and estate administration matters that have extended beyond their one-year target are tying up both staff capacity and billed-but-not-collected revenue — the longer these stay open, the more they cost in overhead without contributing to profit
  • A hybrid billing model across flat-fee estate planning, hourly family law, and contingency personal injury makes it genuinely difficult to know which practice area is most profitable and deserves the most focused investment
  • Without defined profitability targets by practice area, the firm has no basis for deciding which cases to take, which to turn away, or where to direct marketing investment for the highest return
Blind Spot: Personal injury contingency cases take 12–36 months to resolve and consume significant attorney time. Without tracking time-invested versus eventual settlement recovery, the firm may be cross-subsidizing PI development with profit from estate planning — the very practice area generating the firmest, most predictable revenue. Understanding this dynamic is the first step to intentional profit management.
The Positive
  • Estate planning fees are collected upfront before drafting — a strong cash flow model that most practices cannot match, and one that can fund firm investment without waiting on case resolution
  • With 10 estate plans signed per month at an average of $6,000 each, the estate planning practice alone generates approximately $60,000 per month in identified, predictable revenue
The Gap
  • Firms that grow without a profit plan typically hit a ceiling where revenue is strong but the owner is not taking home what they expected — more work, more staff, more overhead, without a proportional increase in personal income
  • Without resolving the aged probate backlog, the firm is carrying revenue that has already been earned on paper but is not available to reinvest or distribute — that is a profitability drag that compounds every month
  • The decision of which practice areas to grow, which to maintain, and which to wind down should be driven by margin analysis — without it, growth decisions are based on preference or volume rather than profitability
More revenue without a profit plan is just more responsibility — what Joe actually wants is more of what the firm earns ending up in his hands, on his terms.
