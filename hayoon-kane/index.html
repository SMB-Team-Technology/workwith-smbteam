Here Is Where Hayoon Kane Law Firm Stands Today

Hayoon Kane Law Firm is a 5-person immigration practice based in Las Vegas, Nevada, specializing in T-Visa cases for human trafficking victims and serving the Korean-speaking immigrant community — generating approximately $700K annually and operating as a Stage 4 Small Business Manager. The firm has built a genuine competitive moat in the Korean-language immigration niche, with 159 five-star Google reviews and a dominant organic presence for Korean immigration searches in Las Vegas. The single most critical finding is that 100% of the firm's paid lead generation depends on one channel — Korean-language Meta ads at $9,000 per month — while at least four named competitors are actively running Google Ads for immigration searches, and an Arizona-based firm is advertising directly into Las Vegas for the firm's highest-value case type.

Stage 6 is the stage where Hayoon can step back from every consultation, trust her team to run the practice, and have the income to purchase the home in San Francisco she has been working toward — and the capacity to serve the Korean immigrant community at the scale she knows is possible.

The Law Firm Growth Acceleration Model

We Help Law Firm Owners Get More Profit, More Personal Freedom, And Outstanding Results For Their Clients.

Most law firms try to grow by chasing more leads. But leads alone are not the problem — and leads alone are not the answer. Here is what actually works.

Lead Generation

Leads are to a law firm what gasoline is to a car. Without them, you go nowhere. But a steady, predictable flow of leads changes everything — you get to choose your cases instead of taking whatever comes in.

Intake

But all the leads in the world are worthless without a system to turn them into paying clients. Most firms are losing cases they already paid for simply because their follow-up is slow or inconsistent.

Self-Managing Team

Once clients are coming in, you need a team — otherwise you end up working 90-hour weeks and results suffer. A self-managing team is what gives you the freedom to step back.

Profit Plan

And once the team is in place, most owners look at their bank account and ask: where did all the money go? That is what a profit plan solves — clarity on what you earn, what you keep, and how to grow both.

All four have to work together. If one is missing, you cannot have more profit, more freedom, and outstanding results for your clients at the same time.

Status: AMBER
The Problem

  • The firm is spending $9,000 per month on Meta ads generating leads that convert at 20% booked-to-hired — without a cost-per-acquired-case calculation, it is impossible to know whether that $9k is producing a positive return or consuming margin
  • Revenue goal of $1.2–$1.5M is clear, but without a documented profit plan, hitting $1.4M in revenue does not guarantee hitting the personal financial milestone — buying a home in San Francisco requires a specific amount of take-home income, not just gross revenue

Blind Spot: T-Visa cases at $11,000 each are the firm's highest-value work — but the actual profit on those cases depends on how much time Hayoon and the team invest per case. Without tracking time and cost per case type, the firm may be prioritizing T-Visa cases for revenue reasons without knowing whether they are also the highest-margin cases. A profit plan answers this question and lets the firm choose cases strategically.
The Positive
  • Revenue at $700K with a clear $1.2–$1.5M growth target means the firm is in a growth orientation — the owner is not satisfied with the status quo and is actively looking to build toward a specific personal financial milestone
  • A defined average case value ($11,000 per T-Visa) gives the firm a clear unit economics foundation — most firms at this stage do not know their case value; knowing it makes ROI calculation and profit planning much more straightforward
The Gap
  • Without knowing the profit per case, the firm cannot make strategic decisions about which cases to prioritize, how many cases are needed to hit the $1.5M goal, or how much of that revenue will actually be available as personal income for Hayoon
  • The San Francisco home purchase is a specific financial target — without a profit plan that connects revenue goals to take-home income to personal savings, the firm can hit $1.4M in revenue and still find that the purchase is out of reach
  • As the firm adds staff to scale, without a profit model, every new hire is a guess about whether the revenue they generate will exceed their cost — a simple profit framework turns those guesses into data-driven decisions
A profit plan is what ensures that reaching $1.5M in revenue actually means reaching the personal milestone Hayoon is working toward — the San Francisco home is funded by take-home income, not gross revenue.
