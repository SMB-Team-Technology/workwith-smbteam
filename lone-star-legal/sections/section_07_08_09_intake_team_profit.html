Intake Assessment

Lone Star Legal has a structural advantage most firms at this stage lack — a dedicated intake specialist is already on staff — but the intake system around that specialist has gaps that are costing the firm signed cases every week. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Office hours are Monday–Friday, 9 AM–5 PM — personal injury accidents do not happen on a business schedule, and the firm has no confirmed after-hours intake coverage for the calls, texts, and form submissions that come in outside those hours
  • Contact form is buried at the bottom of the homepage — even a prospect who wants to reach the firm online has to scroll past the entire page to find the form, and most do not
  • No chatbot or after-hours form-capture system observed — when a prospect lands on the site at 10 PM after an accident, there is no mechanism to capture that lead before they call a competitor
  • No defined follow-up process is observable from the outside — without a documented intake sequence with specific response time standards, leads that do not sign on first contact may be lost entirely
Blind Spot: PI accident victims are in a high-emotion, high-urgency state when they first reach out — the firm that responds first almost always wins the case. Industry data shows that response time within 5 minutes of a lead's first contact produces dramatically higher close rates than a response even 30 minutes later. Every hour of after-hours silence is a call that a competitor answers first.
The Positive
  • A dedicated intake specialist (Jewel Maghanoy) is on staff — this means intake is not falling on attorneys or paralegals who have competing case demands, which is the single most common intake failure in solo and small PI firms
The Gap
  • In a market where competitors likely run 24/7 intake through answering services or AI, every after-hours call that reaches Lone Star Legal's voicemail is a PI case potentially worth tens of thousands of dollars that goes to a competitor who answers
  • A contact form buried at page bottom means the firm's website is not working as a lead capture tool — for every 100 paid ad visitors who land on the page, a significant percentage will not scroll far enough to find a way to contact the firm
  • Without a structured follow-up sequence, prospects who call and do not immediately sign — because they are still in the hospital, talking to insurance, or comparing firms — may never hear from Lone Star Legal again, even though many of them would sign with consistent follow-up
A firm that runs itself starts with an intake system that converts leads around the clock, without Robbye or Todd personally handling every first call — and that system is what turns a marketing investment into predictable, compounding revenue.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact form is observed near the bottom of the homepage under a "Contact Us" section heading — not visible above the fold on desktop or mobile without scrolling, which significantly reduces form submission rates compared to a form placed prominently in the hero section
  • No live chat widget or chatbot is present on the site — the firm's Instagram and Facebook accounts are active, but there is no automated on-site capture mechanism for after-hours visitors
  • Phone numbers are clearly displayed (737-356-7827 primary, 737-234-8831 secondary) and the hero section includes a "Schedule a Free Consultation" CTA — the click-to-call experience is functional, but it only works when someone is available to answer

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

Lone Star Legal has a more developed team structure than most firms at a comparable revenue level — with a CEO, senior paralegal, finance manager, and intake specialist already in place — but without a predictable lead flow driving that team to full capacity, the structure cannot yet operate independently of the partners. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Without a consistent, predictable case pipeline, staff capacity cannot be fully deployed — team members are in position but the volume of cases needed to utilize that capacity is not flowing in through any systematic channel
  • Key-person dependency remains a risk with two senior partners (Robbye and Todd Kelly) handling both business development and litigation — if either is unavailable for any period, case work and firm operations slow down without a clear backup structure
  • No publicly observable accountability system (documented KPIs, weekly standups, performance metrics) — a firm with nine staff and two practice groups needs accountability infrastructure to scale without the partners being involved in every decision
  • CEO role (Selby Jewel) is listed but the scope of that role in day-to-day operations is not confirmed — if the CEO is operational rather than strategic, it may not yet free up the partners from management decisions
Blind Spot: A firm with nine people and no consistent lead flow is actually more exposed than a solo practitioner — there are more salaries to cover, more overhead to carry, and more pressure on each case to generate revenue. Without a marketing system generating cases at scale, the team structure that should be a strength becomes a fixed cost that creates financial pressure on the partners.
The Positive
  • Nine-person team with a CEO, two attorneys, one associate, two paralegals, a finance manager, and a dedicated intake specialist — this is a functional organizational structure that most competitors at this market level cannot match
  • The presence of a Finance & Accounting Manager and a CEO suggests the partners have already begun delegating non-legal functions — that foundation is valuable and can be built upon quickly
The Gap
  • Without a case pipeline that consistently fills the team's capacity, every partner vacation, trial preparation period, or personal obligation creates a bottleneck — the firm is not yet operationally independent of Robbye and Todd being present and available
  • Without documented role clarity, performance metrics, and an accountability structure, the team cannot grow — the next hire, the next case type expansion, or the next revenue milestone will each require the partners to be more involved rather than less
  • The gap between what the team structure suggests is possible and what the digital presence suggests is happening indicates the firm is at a tipping point — the right marketing investment could allow this team to run at full capacity and begin generating true leverage for the partners
A self-managing team that generates leverage for Robbye — where the firm handles more cases, more efficiently, without her in every decision — is the foundation of the firm she deserves to run after two decades of building it.

Profit Plan Assessment

Lone Star Legal has a Finance & Accounting Manager on staff, which puts it ahead of most PI firms at a comparable stage — but having financial staff is not the same as having a profit plan that drives decision-making. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No revenue or profitability figures were confirmed by the firm — without a clear financial picture, it is not possible to assess whether case volume is translating into owner take-home or being consumed by overhead and staff costs
  • PI practices carry high case values ($20K–$500K+) but irregular settlement timelines — a firm can have a strong month on paper and still face cash flow problems if expenses run ahead of settlements
  • With nine staff and two office locations in directory listings, fixed costs are meaningful — without a profit plan that sets targets for cases closed, revenue collected, and owner distribution, growing revenue may simply mean growing expenses at the same rate
  • No visible marketing spend today means the firm is not currently running the kind of ROI tracking that a profit plan requires — adding $10,000–$25,000/month in ad spend without financial clarity makes it harder to measure what is working and optimize accordingly
The Positive
  • A dedicated Finance & Accounting Manager (Vickie Kelly) is on staff — meaning financial record-keeping is not falling entirely on the attorneys, and the firm has the infrastructure to implement a profit plan quickly once the framework is in place
The Gap
  • Without knowing the firm's current cost per case, cost of acquisition, or owner take-home percentage, every growth decision is made on instinct rather than data — that is the definition of profit discovered rather than planned
  • A firm investing in full-service marketing for the first time needs a baseline financial picture before launch — understanding current margins makes it possible to set ROI targets and identify the case volume needed to justify and grow the investment
  • PI practice areas have dramatically different profitability profiles (car accident vs. catastrophic injury vs. offshore) — without tracking profitability by practice area, the firm could be working its hardest on the cases that generate the least margin
When Robbye has a profit plan that tells her exactly how much the firm needs to earn each month to hit her personal and business goals — and a marketing system that consistently delivers those cases — that is when running the firm stops feeling like financial uncertainty and starts feeling like freedom.
