Intake Assessment

Non-attorney salespeople handle the first client meeting and Kim joins the second, but no after-hours coverage was confirmed and CRM hygiene is currently a manual task that falls on Scott. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No after-hours coverage was confirmed — leads arriving evenings or weekends have no defined path to a response
  • CRM data hygiene is currently an ad hoc task for Scott rather than a maintained system, so lead status and follow-up can go untracked
  • The two-meeting structure has no visible feedback loop back into marketing — Scott is left reconciling lead quality from paid channels against what actually closes, by hand
  • No documented follow-up sequence was identified for prospects who don't book on first contact
Blind Spot: Every lead the firm's $6K–$8K/month Google Ads and active LSA investment generates is only as good as what happens after the click — and right now Scott, not a system, is what stands between an ad click and a signed client.
The Positive
  • A defined two-meeting sales structure already exists — non-attorney salespeople handle the first conversation, and Kim joins to close — a real process most solo-attorney-facing firms never build
  • The firm's 4.9-star, 273-review profile suggests that whatever intake happens today does convert into satisfied, referring clients
The Gap
  • Manual CRM upkeep means leads can go stale silently between the first and second meeting — lost revenue that never shows up as a clear number
  • Missing after-hours coverage likely costs leads directly to competitors who are already answering immediately via 24/7 LSA message capture
  • Every hour Scott spends patching intake and CRM by hand is an hour not spent on higher-leverage marketing work
Fixing intake means Scott stops being the manual safety net between a lead and a signed client — and starts running the firm instead of rescuing it one gap at a time.

Team Assessment

With Kim as the firm's principal attorney and public face, and Scott running marketing and operations day to day alongside a receptionist, office manager, and non-attorney sales staff, McAdoo has more built-out team structure than most firms this size — but nearly all of the operational and marketing workload still funnels through one person. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
"...forced to DIY landing pages causing inconsistency and fatigue..."
The Problem
  • Scott — the firm's de facto operations and marketing lead — is personally building and maintaining landing pages, a task better handled by a dedicated marketing system
  • No dedicated ops layer exists above Scott; when a vendor gap, listing inconsistency, or CRM issue comes up, it becomes his problem by default
  • The firm currently coordinates an outside ad vendor (Grow My Ads) and previously managed a web vendor relationship (Non-Stuffy Attorney, now terminated) — meaning vendor management itself is another manual responsibility on Scott's desk
  • The Garden City office is not yet live or optimized on Maps or the website — another task still waiting on available bandwidth
Blind Spot: The fact that the two-meeting sales structure works reasonably well may be masking how fragile the rest of the operation is — one person, Scott, is the single point of failure for marketing, vendor coordination, and CRM hygiene all at once.
The Positive
  • The firm already has more team depth than most solo-attorney-facing estate planning practices — a receptionist, an office manager, dedicated non-attorney sales staff, and a dedicated ops/marketing lead in Scott
  • Kim is inserted only at the second client meeting, a real structural choice that protects her time compared to firms where the attorney handles every conversation personally
The Gap
  • Every hour Scott spends DIY-ing landing pages or reconciling vendor gaps is an hour not spent managing the team or growing the practice
  • Without a manager or system layer above Scott, Kim has no real path to delegation — the firm still depends on one person holding marketing and ops together
Until Scott is supported by systems instead of being the system himself, neither he nor Kim can step back — no matter how much the firm grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

Neither annual revenue nor profitability came up on the call, and no cost-per-case or cost-per-acquisition tracking was mentioned for either the firm's Google Ads spend or its Local Service Ads presence. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No revenue figure was shared and no profitability discussion took place on the call
  • Ad spend has increased from roughly $6K to $8K a month through Grow My Ads with no confirmed tracking of what that increase is returning in signed clients
  • No mention of cost-per-lead or cost-per-case tracking for either paid channel — Google Ads or LSA
  • No defined average case value was identified for estate planning, wills/trusts, or Medicaid planning work
Blind Spot: Without knowing what each new client is worth or what it costs to acquire one, it's impossible to know whether the jump from $6K to $8K a month in ad spend was a good decision or an expensive guess.
The Gap
  • Without cost-per-case tracking, the firm cannot tell which practice area — estate planning, probate, or Medicaid planning — is actually the most profitable, even with a stated goal of shifting the mix toward estate planning
  • Rising ad spend without a profit lens means growth could mean more revenue without more take-home for Kim
Until Kim and Scott can see the numbers clearly, growth is a guess — and neither of them can plan the freedom they're both working toward.
