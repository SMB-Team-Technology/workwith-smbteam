Intake Assessment

Whaley Law has no intake process — Stewart personally answers every inquiry when he is available, with no coverage when he is in court, with a client, or simply off for the evening. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Stewart personally handles every intake inquiry — there is no staff member, virtual assistant, or answering service to receive calls when he is unavailable
  • No after-hours call coverage exists — a client who gets a traffic ticket at 7pm and calls to hire an attorney will reach voicemail and likely call the next firm that answers
  • The firm's website is currently offline, eliminating web-based contact forms as a lead capture channel entirely
  • No defined follow-up sequence exists for prospects who make an initial inquiry but do not sign immediately — there is no system to re-engage warm leads
Blind Spot: Traffic ticket clients are time-sensitive — they have a court date approaching and need an attorney quickly. A missed call or a delayed response from Stewart often means the client hires the first attorney who answered, not the best one. The intake gap costs more in this practice area than almost any other because the buying window is short.
The Gap
  • Every unanswered call during court appearances, client meetings, or after hours is a lost case — at an average of $1,100 per traffic ticket, even a handful of missed calls per week represents thousands of dollars in monthly revenue walking out the door
  • Without a follow-up system, prospects who inquire and do not immediately retain are simply forgotten — no nurture, no callback sequence, no second chance to convert
  • As paid advertising begins generating more inbound volume, an unstructured intake process will become the bottleneck that prevents ad spend from translating into retained clients
A structured intake process is what turns marketing spend into actual revenue — and it is what makes it possible for Stewart to eventually step away from the phone without the firm stopping.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The firm's website is currently inaccessible due to an expired SSL certificate — no contact form, inquiry field, or any digital intake mechanism is reachable by prospective clients
  • No live chat was observed on the site in any cached or indexed content — live web chat is one of the highest-converting intake tools for high-intent legal searches and represents a gap that competitors may be exploiting
  • Once the website is restored, a prominent above-the-fold contact form with a compelling CTA ("Get a Free Consultation") is essential for capturing leads who prefer not to call immediately

Team Assessment

Whaley Law is a true solo practice — Stewart is the only person in the firm, responsible for legal work, intake, admin, billing, and marketing without a single support staff member. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • There are zero staff members — Stewart handles every function in the firm, from client calls to legal filings to billing, without any delegation
  • A 40-hour work week and actual vacations — the specific goals Stewart stated on the call — are structurally impossible in a one-person firm regardless of how much revenue grows
  • Every hour spent on admin, intake, and scheduling is an hour not spent on billable work — and there is no one to absorb those tasks as case volume increases
  • A health emergency, family obligation, or extended absence would halt the firm's revenue entirely, with no backup structure in place
Blind Spot: The belief that "I'll hire someone once I have more money" is the trap that keeps solo practitioners solo. Revenue does not create the capacity to hire — hiring creates the capacity to grow revenue. Stewart's trajectory suggests the demand is already there; the constraint is not money, it is infrastructure.
The Gap
  • Without at least a part-time intake or admin role, any increase in lead volume will create overwhelm rather than growth — Stewart will be the bottleneck at every stage
  • The freedom Stewart is building toward — the 40-hour week, the vacations, the relief from financial pressure — has a prerequisite: a firm that functions when he is not in it
  • Competitors like Lemley Law Partners, with trained intake staff and defined processes, are converting leads at hours when Stewart is unavailable, in court, or off-duty
Building a team is not an expense — it is the only way to turn a job that Stewart owns into a business that works for him.

Profit Plan Assessment

Stewart's revenue is growing — doubling year-over-year is a real achievement for a solo practitioner — but the firm is leaving significant money on the table through pricing that is far below market rate for its highest-value cases. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Stewart charges $7,000 flat fee for felony criminal defense cases where peers in the same market charge $30,000 — this is not a competitive pricing strategy, it is a cash-flow-driven underpricing that limits income without adding clients
  • With multiple practice areas (traffic tickets at $1,100, license defense at $2,500–$4,000, and felony defense at $7,000), there is no visibility into which case type generates the best return per hour invested
  • The firm operates without a formal financial plan — no defined profit targets, no expense benchmarks, and no system for projecting revenue based on case mix and volume
  • Household financial pressure (supporting a spouse's medical practice launch) means the firm's cash flow decisions are being driven by immediate need rather than long-term strategy
Blind Spot: At $7,000 for felony defense, Stewart may be attracting clients who can only afford $7,000 — rather than clients who value the work at its actual worth. Pricing communicates positioning. Moving felony fees toward even $12,000–$15,000 (still well below market) would double felony revenue without adding a single new case, and would attract clients who expect to pay for quality rather than just the lowest price.
The Positive
  • Revenue doubled year-over-year from $15,000/month in June 2025 to $30,000/month in June 2026 — this trajectory, if sustained, reaches $60,000/month within another year without any additional marketing investment
The Gap
  • Felony case underpricing at $7,000 versus a market rate of $30,000 means every felony case Stewart takes is generating 23 cents on the dollar of its potential value — closing that gap even partially would dramatically accelerate the path to $1M annual revenue
  • Without knowing the profit per case type, it is impossible to prioritize the right practice areas, set the right marketing budget, or make intelligent decisions about which clients to take and which to refer out
  • The $1M annual revenue goal requires a plan — not just more clients, but a clear picture of average case value, case volume targets, and a pricing structure that reflects the quality of work being done
Getting to $1M is not just about more cases — it is about knowing what the work is worth, charging accordingly, and building a financial structure that turns revenue into the real stability and freedom Stewart and his family are working toward.
