Intake Assessment

Larry personally evaluates every case that comes through the door, with no dedicated intake staff and no defined process to catch what falls through the cracks. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
"Automate workflows to stabilize revenue and enable expansion, starting with the bottlenecked pre-intake evaluation process."
The Problem
  • Larry personally handles every case evaluation himself — no delegation, no dedicated intake staff
  • No CRM or intake tracking system in place to catch leads that go unanswered
  • No after-hours coverage — with lead flow already described as "inconsistent," any after-hours inquiry risks going to a competitor before Larry can respond
  • No defined follow-up sequence exists for prospects who do not retain the firm on the first call
Blind Spot: The pre-intake evaluation process you named as your biggest bottleneck on the call is not a marketing problem — it's the reason marketing dollars, once spent, will not convert as well as they could.
The Positive
  • Every case that reaches evaluation benefits from Larry's own 40+ years of legal judgment — a genuine differentiator for cases this complex
The Gap
  • Every hour Larry spends manually evaluating leads is an hour not spent on the active, high-value cases already in the pipeline
  • With lead flow already inconsistent and no tracking system, there is no way to know how many leads are lost to delayed response rather than case merit
  • As soon as new marketing dollars are spent to fix Lead Generation, an unfixed intake bottleneck will cap the return on that spend before it ever reaches revenue
Automating this bottleneck is the exact outcome Larry described wanting — the first domino that has to fall before workflows anywhere else in the firm can stabilize.

Team Assessment

Larry is the entire firm today — no additional attorneys, paralegals, or staff were discussed on the call, and every function from case evaluation to client management runs through him personally. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No additional attorneys, paralegals, or intake staff were discussed on the call
  • Every case evaluation and workflow decision currently depends on Larry personally
  • The firm's own stated plan — fix workflows and technology before hiring — means today's operational gaps have to close before any new hire could be fully supported
  • No leadership or management layer exists to absorb responsibility as the firm grows
Blind Spot: Larry's own exploration of tools like Supio, Westlaw Co-Counsel, and Open Evidence shows real openness to changing how the firm operates — the missing piece is a workflow and staffing structure to put that technology adoption to full use.
The Positive
  • Larry's instinct to fix workflows and technology before adding headcount is the right sequence — it means new hires, when they come, will be set up to succeed instead of inheriting today's bottlenecks
The Gap
  • Every day Larry personally evaluates cases and manages the firm is a day not spent growing it or stepping back from it
  • Cash-flow volatility becomes harder to manage when no one besides Larry can make operational decisions
  • Scaling revenue without a team structure in place means scaling Larry's own workload, not the firm's capacity
The stable, expandable firm Larry described on the call cannot exist as long as every workflow depends on him personally — building a self-managing structure is what turns that vision into something real.

Profit Plan Assessment

Revenue is $1M+ annually, but with resolution timelines running long on complex, high-value litigation, Larry has described real cash-flow volatility rather than predictable profit. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"High-value ($400k+), complex cases (med mal, nursing home abuse) create significant cash-flow volatility."
The Problem
  • Revenue is $1M+ annually, but no profitability figures by practice area were discussed on the call
  • Cash flow is described as significantly volatile, driven by the long resolution timelines on complex cases
  • No revenue goal was stated on the call — this audit uses a standard 2x-current-revenue default (~$2M+) in its place
  • Average case value is known only as a range ($400k+), not tracked as a specific, predictable number
Blind Spot: Because your highest-value cases are also your slowest to resolve, revenue and cash flow can tell two very different stories in the same month — without separating the two, it is easy to feel stretched even in a strong year.
The Positive
  • A $1M+ revenue base built almost entirely on complex, high-value litigation shows real selectivity in the cases the firm takes on — a discipline a profit plan can build directly on
The Gap
  • Without profitability visibility by case type, there is no way to know which practice areas are actually the most profitable, not just the largest
  • Cash-flow volatility makes it harder to commit confidently to new marketing or staffing investment, even when the underlying revenue trend is healthy
  • A stated growth goal without a matching profit plan risks the firm working harder for the same — or lower — take-home
Stabilizing revenue was the outcome Larry named directly on the call — and that starts with a profit plan that turns high-value cases into predictable income, not just impressive case results.
