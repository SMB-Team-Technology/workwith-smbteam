Intake Assessment

JJ Law has an in-house daytime intake team and recently switched to AI-powered after-hours coverage — but the firm is still signing only 5–7 cases per month against a stated target of 10, which means leads are being lost somewhere between first contact and signed retainer. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • The firm recently replaced its human after-hours intake agents with an AI service (Alliance) due to "poor performance" — the transition addressed coverage gaps but the lead conversion shortfall persists, suggesting the problem is in the overall intake process, not just after-hours coverage
  • No confirmed CRM tracking, intake KPIs, or multi-step follow-up sequence — without measurement, there is no way to identify how many qualified leads are lost between initial contact and signed retainer, or at which step the drop-off is occurring
  • At 260 active PI cases, the in-house intake team is handling significant volume — new inbound leads may not be receiving the immediate attention they need in the critical first five minutes of contact, which is when the probability of signing drops fastest
  • No confirmed structured follow-up sequence for prospects who do not sign on first contact — leads who do not commit immediately are likely being abandoned rather than nurtured through the decision process
Blind Spot: PI prospects calling after a car accident are often in distress and making emotional decisions in the hours immediately after the incident. The gap between an AI intake interaction and a trained human intake specialist who can build rapport and guide a distressed caller to a signed retainer could be the difference between a case signed and a prospect who calls the next firm on the list. This is worth measuring with direct call auditing.
The Positive
  • The firm recognized the after-hours intake problem and made a structural change — proactively switching to AI-powered coverage shows ownership of the issue rather than accepting the status quo
  • An in-house daytime intake team is already in place, which means the infrastructure for a high-performing intake function exists and can be optimized without a rebuild from scratch
The Gap
  • At 5–7 cases per month versus a target of 10, the firm is leaving an estimated 3–5 cases per month unsigned — at $8,500 average retained fee, that is $25,500–$42,500/month in potential revenue not being captured from leads already being generated, before any new marketing spend is added
  • Without a tracked follow-up sequence, leads who do not sign on first contact are almost certainly being abandoned; in PI, most clients consult multiple firms before choosing — structured multi-touch follow-up is the difference between winning and losing those cases
  • No intake conversion rate data means the firm cannot identify whether the problem is response speed, after-hours coverage, follow-up cadence, or intake quality — making it impossible to fix the right thing without measurement in place
Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • A contact form is present at /contact-us/ but its visibility above the fold on mobile — the primary device for PI searches — has not been confirmed; PI cases that start with a form submission rather than a phone call require a response process that is just as fast as a live call to prevent the prospect from moving on
  • Live chat presence was not observed during research — unconfirmed whether this represents a gap in website conversion; after-hours web visitors who cannot reach anyone by phone are unlikely to wait until morning to take action
  • The 24/7 phone coverage is confirmed and prominently displayed on the site — (888) 495-3339 with "24/7 availability" messaging — which is a positive intake signal, but availability alone does not guarantee fast response or a trained intake process for every call

Team Assessment

JJ Law has team members in place across three locations — in-house intake staff, AI after-hours coverage, and case management for 260 active PI cases — but Jonathan Jackson remains the primary engine of new business in Houston, where every case comes through his personal referral network. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • The firm's Houston market is entirely referral-dependent through Jonathan's personal relationships — when he is not actively maintaining those relationships, that revenue stream does not generate new cases, making the firm's largest market completely dependent on his presence and energy
  • The revenue decline from approximately $3M to $1.5M in retained fees signals that the firm experienced a disruption that a more institutionalized team structure would have been able to weather — structural dependency on the owner is a vulnerability, not a business model
  • No business development or marketing function exists within the team — new case generation is not assigned to any team member or process; it lives in the owner's personal calendar and network
  • Attorney count, case management staff ratios, and org structure were not discussed in detail — at 260 active PI cases across three locations, the operational demands are significant, and without a clear accountability structure, owner oversight is likely extensive
Blind Spot: Jonathan's value in this firm should be as a brand and strategist, not as the person manually generating every Houston case. The fact that he has built a recognizable personal brand — "The Sneaker Lawyer" with an NFL backstory — means the brand can generate cases at scale through digital channels without requiring his personal presence in every relationship. That pivot has not happened yet.
The Positive
  • Operating across three locations — Houston, Oklahoma City, and Tulsa — with 260 active cases requires genuine organizational capability; this is not a solo practice, and the infrastructure to scale a team already exists
  • The proactive decision to switch from human to AI after-hours intake demonstrates a willingness to systematize operations rather than rely on people for every function — this is the right instinct applied to one area of the business
The Gap
  • Every case sourced through Jonathan's personal referral network is a case that only exists while Jonathan is actively working — building a firm with real enterprise value requires institutionalizing lead generation so that cases keep coming whether or not Jonathan is in the office
  • Without a team member or function responsible for business development or marketing performance, the firm cannot grow its Houston presence without increasing demands on the owner — the exact opposite of the goal
  • Reaching $2.5M in retained fees while also stepping back from day-to-day operations requires delegation not just of tasks but of the revenue generation function itself — and that delegation has not yet begun in the firm's most important market
Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

JJ Law has more financial clarity than most firms at this stage — Jonathan knows his average case values, active case count, and retained fee totals — but a 50% revenue decline from peak to 2025 without an early-warning financial system shows that clarity about current numbers is not the same as a plan that protects them. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Revenue dropped from approximately $3M to $1.5M in retained fees — without a clear financial model identifying which clients, practice areas, or channels drove the original growth, the path to $2.5M is being rebuilt without a blueprint for what made the firm successful before
  • Current marketing spend of $15.5k/month ($10.5k digital + $5k radio in Oklahoma) represents $186,000/year in investment — without clear ROI attribution by channel, it is not possible to determine whether this spend is producing proportional returns or where it should be reallocated
  • The firm handles personal injury, wrongful repossession, estate planning, probate, and is adding family law — each with different case economics, time-to-settlement windows, and cost structures — without confirmed practice area profitability tracking to inform where resources should be concentrated
  • The gap between $4M in gross settlements and $1.5M in retained fees represents a 37.5% effective fee ratio — understanding what drives the gap between gross and net, and how to improve it, is worth significant money at scale
Blind Spot: The firm knows its case volume and average case value, but the more actionable number is cost per signed case by channel. If Oklahoma digital spend is producing cases at $2,000+ cost per acquisition while Houston referral cases cost effectively nothing, the firm may be spending $186,000/year on marketing that could be deployed more efficiently with proper attribution tracking in place.
The Positive
  • Jonathan knows his average case values ($8,500 TX, $8,250 OK), his active caseload (260 PI cases), and his retained fee totals — this level of financial awareness is ahead of most firms at this stage and provides a solid foundation for building a proper profit plan
  • A concrete 2026 revenue goal of $2.5M exists — having a specific, time-bound financial target is the prerequisite for building any meaningful profit plan, and it is in place
The Gap
  • Without ROI attribution on the current $15.5k/month marketing spend, there is no data-driven basis for deciding whether to increase, reallocate, or consolidate that investment — the firm is spending blind in Oklahoma while spending nothing in Houston
  • The path from $1.5M to $2.5M requires approximately 118 additional cases per year at $8,500 average — that is roughly 10 more cases per month — and the financial model should specify exactly which channels, markets, and practice areas will generate them and at what cost
  • Without practice area profitability tracking, the firm may be growing revenue in areas that cost more to service while its highest-margin work gets under-resourced — a single dashboard showing retained fee margin by practice area would immediately clarify where to focus
The goal is not $2.5M in revenue — it is $2.5M in retained fees with margins that make every case worth taking and a financial model that shows exactly how to get there.
