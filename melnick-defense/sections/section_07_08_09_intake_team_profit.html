Intake Assessment

Dan Melnick personally handles every incoming lead inquiry — answering phones, screening potential clients, evaluating cases, and deciding whether to take them on, all while managing an active trial schedule including a current 4-week murder case. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Dan personally handles every intake call — there is no intake coordinator, no staff member, and no defined process; every lead must reach Dan directly or it is not captured
  • No CRM or intake tracking software in place — there is no system to record inquiries, track where they came from, follow up with prospects who did not retain immediately, or measure conversion rate
  • No after-hours coverage — criminal defense and DUI prospects often call in the evening or on weekends, immediately after an arrest; if no one answers, they call the next firm on the list
  • No defined follow-up sequence — a prospect who calls, has a brief conversation, and does not retain on the spot is likely never contacted again; the marketing dollars spent to generate that lead are wasted
Blind Spot: People charged with DUI or arrested for misdemeanors often call from the police station or within hours of being released — the urgency window is real and short. Every missed call after hours is a case that will be retained by whichever firm answers first. Dan may not realize how many calls go unanswered while he is in court, in a deposition, or focused on trial prep.
The Positive
  • Dan's 44 years of criminal defense experience means that when he does speak with a prospect, the conversion quality is high — he can quickly evaluate a case and communicate credibility in a way that builds trust
The Gap
  • Every lead that falls through the cracks is revenue already paid for through Google Ads or LSA spend — without an intake process, the firm is essentially funding lead generation and then releasing a significant portion of those leads to competitors
  • As long as Dan is the intake function, adding more leads does not help — it just adds more pressure on the one person who is already at capacity
  • Without intake data in a CRM, there is no way to measure close rate, identify which lead sources produce the best cases, or improve the system over time
Every lead that falls through a broken intake process is one fewer case in the misdemeanor and DUI volume Dan needs to build the firm he can eventually hand off — and one more hour Dan spends chasing business instead of running it.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • A contact form on the website was not confirmed — direct site access was blocked during research; the firm's intake capture capability via the website should be verified and optimized as part of any website rebuild
  • No live chat widget was observed in search snippets or indexed content — criminal defense prospects searching at night are not being offered any immediate way to connect or leave their information
  • The Gmail address (melnickdefense@gmail.com) is used as the public contact — inquiries sent by email go to a personal Gmail inbox with no tracking, no automated response, and no CRM integration

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

Dan Melnick is a one-man law firm — every client intake, every case strategy, every court appearance, and every business decision runs through him personally, with no full-time associate, no admin staff, and no delegation structure in place. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Dan is the entire law firm — no full-time associate attorney, no intake coordinator, no admin or operations support; every function of the business depends on his personal participation
  • Outside appearance counsel is used for some court dates, but this is a workaround, not a delegation structure — Dan still manages, briefs, and oversees every case; the administrative and strategic load does not decrease
  • No accountability structure, no defined roles or job descriptions, and no reporting system — there is no framework for bringing on staff even when the time comes to hire
  • The current backlog (fire disruption, double calendar, active 4-week trial) demonstrates that the firm has no capacity buffer — when unexpected events occur, Dan absorbs all of it personally
Blind Spot: Dan is thinking about hiring a full-time associate as a future step — but if the intake process, case management system, and role definition are not in place before that hire happens, the associate becomes a new dependency rather than true delegation. Dan will spend time managing the associate rather than stepping back. The systems have to be built before the person can run them independently.
The Positive
  • Dan has a clear vision for the team he wants to build — a full-time associate attorney who takes full case responsibility — and he has previously built and sold a practice, which means he understands conceptually what a transferable business looks like
The Gap
  • As long as Dan is the firm, every vacation, illness, or capacity limit is a direct hit to revenue — the firm cannot earn without him present
  • A firm that requires the owner's personal involvement in every case has no transferable value — the "hand off to a younger attorney and collect passive income" exit Dan wants is impossible to execute without a team structure
  • Dan's stated goal of a 4-day work week within 12 months cannot be achieved without delegation infrastructure — adding more cases without a team just means more hours, not less
A firm that depends entirely on Dan Melnick cannot be handed off to a younger attorney and cannot generate passive retirement income — building the team structure is what transforms a job into a business worth owning and selling.

Profit Plan Assessment

Dan's firm has generated meaningful revenue throughout a 44-year career — evidenced by years of Google Ads investment and the ability to service a substantial ad debt — but there is no evidence of a structured profit plan, financial visibility by case type, or a model for what the firm needs to earn to fund his retirement goals. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Revenue was not stated on the discovery call and no financial targets were articulated — there is no confirmed understanding of what the firm currently earns or what it needs to earn to reach Dan's retirement goals
  • The $300K Google Ads debt accumulated because campaigns ran without oversight — spend was not tracked against revenue outcomes, and the financial impact was not caught until it became a crisis; this is the defining symptom of absent financial controls
  • No confirmed profit tracking by case type — Dan is planning to shift from high-value serious felonies to high-volume misdemeanors and DUIs, but there is no financial model showing whether that shift increases or decreases take-home profit
  • Financial strain from investment losses and the Ads debt means Dan cannot comfortably retire today — the gap between his current financial position and what he needs is unquantified
Blind Spot: The shift away from serious felony cases toward high-volume misdemeanors and DUIs may feel like a quality-of-life improvement — and it likely is — but if current serious felony cases represent a disproportionate share of total revenue at higher case values, the volume of misdemeanor/DUI cases needed to replace that revenue may be larger than Dan currently anticipates. This must be modeled before the case mix shift is fully executed.
The Positive
  • Dan has real-world experience building and selling a practice (2000 sale with ongoing monthly payout) — he is not starting from zero conceptually; he knows what a transferable business looks like and has done it before
  • The $300K Google Ads debt is nearly fully repaid — this financial overhang is resolving, which means cash flow will soon be freed up and available for reinvestment in systems and team
The Gap
  • Without knowing current revenue, cost per case, and overhead by case type, there is no baseline from which to build a growth plan — the goal of "autopilot and profit" requires quantifying exactly what "profit" means and building a path to it
  • A firm being sold or handed off is valued on recurring, predictable revenue — not on the owner's personal reputation; without financial documentation and profitability by system rather than by person, the exit Dan wants commands a fraction of what it could
  • Every month without a profit plan is a month Dan's financial runway for retirement remains undefined — and in a market with investment losses already on the books, the firm's profitability is the primary lever he controls
The "autopilot and profit" model requires profit to actually be engineered — modeling the case mix economics, setting revenue targets by practice area, and building toward a firm with documented, transferable profitability is what makes the retirement handoff Dan wants financially real.
