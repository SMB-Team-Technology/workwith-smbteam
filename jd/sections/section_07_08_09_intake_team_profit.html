Intake Assessment

JD Law Group has demonstrated historically strong conversion capability — a 65% close rate far exceeds industry norms — but that rate is now declining significantly as lead quality has dropped and the intake process has not been formalized for the new reality of digital-generated prospects. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No dedicated intake specialist — the paralegal supports case work for two attorneys, leaving intake as an ad hoc responsibility rather than a defined role with ownership
  • Historical 65% close rate was built on referral-based prospects who came pre-sold; digital leads require a faster, more structured follow-up sequence to achieve comparable conversion
  • Contact form on roswelllegal.com is not above the fold and is not the primary CTA — phone-first design means after-hours prospects have no low-friction way to submit an inquiry
  • No observed after-hours coverage mechanism — a prospect who searches for a car accident lawyer at 9pm and cannot reach someone will call the next firm on the list
Blind Spot: The current close rate decline is being attributed to lower lead quality — but the intake system itself may be amplifying that problem. Digital leads who do not get a same-day response have typically contacted 2–3 other firms by the time you call back. A fast, structured follow-up sequence can recover a significant portion of leads that currently appear unresponsive.
The Positive
  • A historical 65% close rate is exceptional and reflects genuine attorney-to-client trust and consultative skill — that ability does not disappear, it just needs to be applied to a higher volume of properly followed-up leads
The Gap
  • Every uncontacted digital lead is lost revenue — at a $5,000–$6,500 average case value, losing even 3 additional leads per month from slow follow-up represents $15,000–$20,000 in monthly revenue leakage
  • The gap between a 65% close rate and a 30–40% digital close rate is not skill — it is speed and structure; a defined intake process closes that gap without changing who Doug is or how he practices law
  • After-hours inquiry capture is a competitive differentiator that larger firms like Hammers and Van Sant Law have already built — prospects who cannot reach JD Law Group after 5pm are converted by those firms overnight
Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • roswelllegal.com's primary contact method is a phone number above the fold — no contact form above the fold and no after-hours capture mechanism for PI prospects who arrive outside business hours
  • jdgroup.law has a contact page but the submission path and form visibility have not been confirmed as optimized for conversion — form location and friction have direct impact on lead capture rate

Team Assessment

JD Law Group operates as a three-person firm — two attorneys and one paralegal — where both principals share responsibility for casework, business development, intake, and administration simultaneously. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Both attorneys are generalists by necessity — Doug and Joshua each handle their own cases while also managing intake, operations, and business development with no dedicated support for those functions
  • No dedicated intake, marketing, or operations role exists — the paralegal supports case work, which means every non-legal operational task competes for attorney time
  • Doug has explicitly stated he prefers "working in" the business (solving legal puzzles) over "working on" it — but the current structure offers no separation between those two roles
  • At current case volume, the team is manageable; but scaling to $3M+ will require adding operational infrastructure before revenue arrives, not after
Blind Spot: The preference to keep the team small — which is a reasonable and understandable choice — is currently the structural reason why Doug cannot have both case volume and focus time simultaneously. Adding one well-defined role (intake/operations coordinator) would not make the firm "bigger" in a way that adds management burden — it would remove the management burden Doug is currently absorbing himself.
The Positive
  • Two-attorney partnership with distinct practice area strengths (Doug in PI, Joshua in criminal defense and domestic violence) creates a natural division of legal responsibility — the framework for a self-managing structure already exists at the attorney level
The Gap
  • Without an operational support role, every new lead that marketing generates will land back on Doug's desk — growth in case volume without team support means more work for Doug, not more time for the legal puzzles he loves
  • The inability to delegate intake and administrative functions is the single structural reason Doug cannot step back from the business even when revenue is strong
  • Competitors like The Ford Law Firm — with 25+ years in market and 10,000+ cases — have built the support infrastructure that allows their attorneys to focus on legal work; JD Law Group is competing for the same cases without that structural advantage
Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

JD Law Group generated $2.5M in 2025 but is currently on pace for roughly 25% of that volume in 2026 — and without a clear profit plan, the path back to prior revenue levels will not necessarily restore prior income levels. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No profit margin, overhead ratio, or cost per acquisition data was shared on the call — the firm is operating without real-time financial visibility into what each case type actually returns
  • Revenue has declined sharply from $2.5M in 2025 — at the current 2026 case pace (35 criminal, 10 PI year-to-date), the firm is projecting roughly 90–100 total cases for the year versus 355 in 2025
  • The firm's revenue goal is to recover to $2.5M and then grow at 20% annually — but there is no stated plan for managing costs proportionally as revenue recovers
  • Without knowing the cost per lead, cost per case, and overhead structure, it is impossible to determine whether growth investment will translate into higher take-home or simply higher overhead
Blind Spot: Criminal defense and PI cases have very different cost structures — criminal cases are typically flat-fee and predictable, while PI cases are contingency-based with variable resolution timelines. Without tracking profitability by practice area, the firm cannot see whether its current 50/50 revenue split is the most profitable allocation of attorney time and marketing spend.
The Gap
  • Without financial visibility, the firm cannot determine the maximum safe investment in marketing and advertising — which means either under-investing and limiting recovery, or over-investing and creating cash flow risk
  • As revenue recovers, overhead and marketing costs will increase simultaneously — without a profit plan, more revenue can mean thinner margins rather than more take-home income for Doug
  • A clear cost-per-case-type analysis could reveal whether criminal defense or PI is the higher-margin practice — that information should drive marketing spend allocation, not a default 50/50 assumption
Profit clarity is what ensures Doug gets paid for every case he wins — not just the firm's revenue line, and not just the accounts he can see from day to day.
