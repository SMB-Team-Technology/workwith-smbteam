Intake Assessment

David Farias is personally handling every intake call, client contact, and after-hours inquiry — in a practice area where leads call immediately after accidents, at any hour of the day or night. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
"60–70 hours/week including after-hours client calls"
The Problem
  • David personally handles every intake call and after-hours inquiry with no delegation, no answering service, and no backup — each contact interrupts casework and trial preparation
  • No formal intake process, call script, or case qualification criteria in place — every inquiry is handled ad hoc with no consistent approach to conversion
  • No CRM or lead tracking system — the source, status, and outcome of every inquiry goes unrecorded, making it impossible to measure or improve conversion rate
  • No after-hours coverage structure — PI leads who call after accidents at night or on weekends reach either David directly or no one, with no defined follow-up protocol for missed calls
Blind Spot: The fact that David is personally available for after-hours calls sounds like a service advantage — but it means the firm's intake capacity is limited to one person's availability. As marketing-driven lead volume grows, this will become an immediate bottleneck, and cases will be lost not because the marketing failed but because the intake infrastructure was not ready for the volume.
The Gap
  • Without a defined intake process, David cannot distinguish between a marketing problem and an intake problem — he has no baseline conversion rate to measure against, and no data on how many inquiries are being lost before a case is signed
  • At a 15% conversion rate, a single missed after-hours PI inquiry represents a lost case worth an estimated $8,000 or more — cases that go to whichever competitor answers the phone first
  • Every hour David spends on intake calls, case qualification, and administrative follow-up is an hour not spent on the trial preparation and litigation work that is his competitive advantage and the reason referral partners send him cases
If David's goal is to be known as the premier trial litigator in Texas, the path runs through a firm that converts leads reliably — not one that depends on David personally answering the phone at midnight.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The website is in pre-launch (Coming Soon) mode with no contact form, no inquiry capture field, and no digital intake pathway — all inquiries must come through a direct phone call
  • No chat tool has been implemented — prospects who prefer to inquire digitally rather than call immediately have no option to do so
  • When the website launches, a visible contact form above the fold and a 24/7 inquiry capture option will be critical for converting after-hours and non-phone visitors who are ready to inquire but not yet ready to call

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

Farias Trial Law is entirely dependent on David Farias for every function — casework, intake, administration, records, and client management — with no support staff and no delegation possible at any level. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
"Taking cases from pre-litigation-focused attorneys who lack the capacity or expertise for trial"
The Problem
  • Zero staff on payroll — David handles all intake, casework, medical record collection, client communication, and administrative functions without any support
  • Local contract paralegals at $60–$110/hr are cost-prohibitive for the routine administrative tasks consuming David's time — tasks that a legal assistant could handle at a fraction of that rate
  • No role definition, no accountability structure, and no separation between what only David can do (litigation) and what someone else could do (admin, records, client follow-up, intake scheduling)
  • At 60–70 hours per week, David is already at capacity before any marketing investment adds a single new lead to the pipeline — more cases without team infrastructure means the quality of work on existing cases will suffer
Blind Spot: The barrier to hiring is not the cost of a paralegal — it is the perceived cost of bringing on any staff member. A part-time legal assistant at $18–$25/hr handling medical records, intake scheduling, and client follow-up would free 15–20 hours per week for David at a cost far below the value of a single additional PI case signed.
The Gap
  • Without at least one support person, the ceiling on firm growth is David's personal hours — every additional case taken is a direct trade of more of his time, not leverage from a system
  • The tasks David is currently absorbing (record gathering, intake calls, administrative work) carry high opportunity cost: every hour spent on them is an hour not spent on the trial preparation and board certification coursework that builds his long-term reputation
  • The path to board certification David wants requires focused study, continuing legal education, and engagement with the Texas legal community — none of which are accessible at 60–70 hours per week with no operational support
Building a team — even starting with one person — is what lets David stop being the bottleneck and start being the litigator he is building the firm to become.

Profit Plan Assessment

Farias Trial Law is self-funding all litigation costs with cash flow entirely dependent on future settlements — making income unpredictable and financial planning effectively impossible in the firm's current structure. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
"Self-funding all litigation costs, with cash flow dependent on future settlements"
The Problem
  • No revenue target has been established — Michael Kopp noted on the call that setting revenue goals is a prerequisite for achieving David's stated professional goals, but those targets remain undefined
  • Cash flow is entirely tied to settlement timing — months with no settlements mean months with no income, regardless of how many active cases are in progress at any given time
  • Litigation cost exposure is unmanaged — self-funding deposition costs, expert fees, and filing costs across multiple active cases simultaneously creates cumulative financial risk with no tracking or cap in place
  • No financial visibility system, no cost-per-case tracking, and no profit margin data — the true cost of each case in time and disbursements has not been established
Blind Spot: The current model — referrals plus settlement-based income — can feel financially comfortable in busy periods, which masks the underlying risk. If three or four active cases experience simultaneous delays, the resulting cash flow gap can become a practice-threatening event without a cash reserve strategy in place.
The Gap
  • Without financial visibility, David cannot know whether he is building a profitable practice or an expensive job — the difference only becomes clear when settlement timing goes against him and there is no reserve to absorb the gap
  • No financial plan means no ability to budget for marketing investment, staff hires, or the continuing legal education required for the board certification David is working toward
  • A firm with no defined revenue targets has no benchmark to evaluate whether any investment — including SMB Team — is generating a measurable return, making accountability and course correction impossible
The freedom David wants — to focus on trying cases and building his reputation as a litigator — requires a financial foundation that does not crack every time settlement timing is unpredictable.
