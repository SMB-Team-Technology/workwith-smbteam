Intake Assessment

Durak Law advertises 24/7 availability and a free 60-minute consultation — strong signals of accessibility — but without a defined intake system, that availability promise is difficult to deliver consistently as caseload grows. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Solo practitioner model almost certainly means the attorney handles or is involved in initial intake conversations — this creates a direct ceiling on how many new clients the firm can sign in any given week
  • No after-hours intake system or answering service observed — calls received after business hours likely go to voicemail, and in legal, the prospect calling at 9pm has already Googled three competitors by morning
  • No observed CRM or structured lead tracking system to ensure every inquiry receives a defined follow-up sequence and no prospects fall through the cracks
  • No visible lead form-to-call conversion protocol — web leads that do not receive a response within five minutes convert at a fraction of the rate of those who do, and this is likely being lost on every form submission received
Blind Spot: Attorney-handled intake feels personal and high-quality — and it is, for the clients who get through. But it also means every hour Michal spends on intake calls is an hour not spent on casework, strategy, or building the firm. The cost is not visible in any single conversation; it shows up as a permanent ceiling on how much the practice can grow.
The Gap
  • At even 10% improved conversion on current organic and referral inquiries, the firm signs meaningfully more clients without any additional marketing spend — intake is the highest-ROI investment the firm is not making
  • After-hours leads that go unanswered before the next business morning are almost certainly calling a competitor who does have after-hours coverage — and first contact wins in legal intake consistently
  • Without a defined follow-up sequence, leads who do not sign immediately are being left to make their own decision — which often means signing with the next firm that followed up proactively
A structured intake process is the fastest way to grow revenue without growing the marketing budget — and it is the first step toward building a firm that does not require Michal to be present for every new client conversation.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact forms are present on the homepage and contact page — a positive structural element — but no response-time guarantee or expectation-setting is visible to prospects after submission
  • No chat or instant-response mechanism is observed on the website; visitors with urgent questions outside business hours have no immediate option to engage and are likely to move on to the next result
  • The free consultation form should be the primary conversion point on the site — but without a confirmed rapid follow-up process, form submissions may not be converting at industry benchmarks for legal intake

Team Assessment

Durak Law appears to operate with Michal Durakiewicz as the primary and likely sole attorney, with no team structure visible on the website beyond the lead attorney — every case, every intake call, and every client delivery flows through one person. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No team members are identified on the website beyond the lead attorney — the firm is entirely dependent on one person for every function: intake, case management, client communication, and delivery
  • Without support staff in intake, administration, or case management, every new client adds directly to Michal's personal workload — there is no parallel capacity being built
  • No accountability structure or defined roles are observable, meaning there is no foundation to hire into — the firm would be starting team-building from scratch
  • The advertised 24/7 availability claim is likely being absorbed personally by the attorney, which is sustainable for now but creates burnout risk and a hard ceiling on growth as volume increases
Blind Spot: Running a solo practice for 10 years successfully can make the status quo feel like the natural state — but it also means the ceiling has been in place for the entire decade. A firm that runs itself is not built in one step; it starts with the first defined hire and the first delegation that actually holds. The firms Michal is competing against — Rogers, Shea & Spanos with multiple attorneys and staff — did not get there by staying solo.
The Gap
  • Every week without a delegated intake process is a week the attorney spends billable hours on administrative and intake work — time that cannot be recovered and represents a direct cost to the firm's profitability
  • Without a team structure, Michal cannot be in court, on vacation, or unavailable for any reason without the firm going dark for new inquiries — there is no redundancy
  • Competitors with multi-attorney offices and defined staff roles can serve more clients, respond faster, and grow faster — all without the principal attorney doing more personal hours
The path from solo practitioner to a firm that generates income without requiring Michal's personal presence at every step begins with the right team structure — and that structure needs to be intentionally and systematically built.

Profit Plan Assessment

With no financial data available from the discovery process, Durak Law's current profitability picture is unknown — but a solo practitioner managing six practice areas without documented financial tracking is almost certainly not optimizing for margin. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No revenue, profitability, or cost data was available for this audit — the firm is operating without a shared or documented financial picture, which is common for solo practitioners but creates significant blind spots as the firm grows
  • Six active practice areas with widely varying case values and time requirements — from Business Law to Criminal Defense to Education Law — create margin complexity that is nearly impossible to manage without financial tracking by practice area
  • No observed billing, collections, or profit planning process visible from external research; financial management in solo practices is typically handled personally by the attorney, often without formal systems
  • Without knowing cost per case acquisition by practice area, marketing dollars cannot be efficiently allocated to the work that produces the best return per hour and dollar invested
Blind Spot: The most time-consuming cases at Durak Law are very likely not the most profitable ones. Education Law and Professional License Defense cases may carry premium fee structures relative to time spent, while high-conflict family law cases may consume disproportionate hours per dollar earned. Without tracking profitability by case type, it is impossible to know which practice areas the firm should be marketing most aggressively — and which are keeping Michal busy at a cost.
The Gap
  • Without a profit plan, additional revenue from marketing investment may not translate to proportionally more take-home income — the gap between gross revenue and what Michal actually keeps is invisible without tracking
  • Six practice areas without profitability data means the firm cannot target marketing spend at the highest-return cases — every dollar spent may be generating the right number of leads but the wrong mix of cases
  • Solo practitioners consistently report that their busiest periods were not their most financially rewarding — because overhead, unbilled time, and case mix were not being tracked against actual take-home
Michal did not build this firm to work harder for less — but without a profit plan, that is the risk that unmanaged growth creates. The goal is more income with more freedom, not just more cases on the calendar.
