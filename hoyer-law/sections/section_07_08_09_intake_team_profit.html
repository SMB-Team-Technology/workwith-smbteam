Intake Assessment

Hoyer Law has the staff structure to support a strong intake process — a receptionist, a case manager, and a GM overseeing operations — but no confirmed intake system, follow-up protocol, or after-hours coverage has been identified. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No confirmed intake process or written follow-up sequence — without a defined protocol, intake quality depends on individual staff behavior rather than a system that works every time
  • No after-hours coverage confirmed — family law prospects often reach out evenings and weekends when their situation feels most urgent, and without someone responding, those calls go to competitors
  • A recent Google review cited an attorney being "frequently late to calls and even missed a few completely" — a visible signal that scheduling and follow-through gaps exist that can cost retention before the relationship even starts
  • No confirmed CRM or intake tracking software — without a centralized system, leads that do not convert immediately risk falling through the cracks before follow-up happens
Blind Spot: When a family facing a divorce or custody battle reaches out after hours and gets voicemail, they do not wait — they call the next firm on the list. With CoilLaw and Arnold Wadsworth likely having after-hours coverage, Hoyer Law could be converting far fewer of the organic leads it already attracts than its reputation deserves.
The Positive
  • Website features a prominent "Book Consultation" CTA above the fold and a "Request a Consult" form — incoming leads have a clear path to express interest, which is a conversion-ready starting point
  • Receptionist and case manager roles on staff suggest that intake is at least partially delegated — the foundation for a structured system is already in place
The Gap
  • A family law firm generating leads without a documented follow-up system typically converts 20–40% fewer consultations into retained clients than one with a defined intake process — at Hoyer Law's scale, that gap represents significant annual revenue left uncaptured
  • After-hours gaps mean that the organic traffic and reputation Hoyer Law has spent 15 years building is generating leads that go unanswered at the moments when families are most ready to hire
  • Without intake metrics — response time, consultation-to-retention rate, lead source tracking — it is impossible to know which marketing channels are producing actual clients vs. just inquiries
A structured intake system is what converts Casey's marketing investment into clients automatically — and that is what creates the predictable income that gives him the option to step back.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact form and "Book Consultation" CTA are prominently placed above the fold — the website is structured to capture intent effectively when visitors are ready to act
  • No live chat or chat widget observed — an opportunity to capture the visitors who are not yet ready to call but would respond to a low-commitment chat interaction, especially during off-hours
  • Without a chat option, evening and weekend website traffic — which often represents emotionally driven, high-urgency family law prospects — has no immediate response mechanism other than a form submission that may not be seen until the next business day

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

Hoyer Law has more team structure than most firms at this stage — a GM, three attorneys, a lead paralegal, a financial specialist, a case manager, a law clerk, and a receptionist across two locations — but the accountability systems, performance tracking, and delegation framework needed to make this team truly self-managing have not been confirmed. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No confirmed org chart, KPI system, or performance reporting structure — without defined metrics for each role, the team may be performing tasks without being accountable to outcomes
  • Analia Hoyer serves as both GM and paralegal — a dual role that suggests the firm is still in the phase where operational leadership and legal work are mixed rather than separated into dedicated lanes
  • Two-location operation creates coordination complexity that multiplies without a clear management layer — the Lehi and St. George offices need consistent standards, communication protocols, and reporting without Casey being in both places
  • The recent review referencing late calls and missed appointments suggests team accountability around client-facing commitments is not yet systematized
Blind Spot: Having a GM in place is a significant advantage — but a GM without formal authority, defined KPIs, and a documented delegation framework is still a coordinator, not a true operator. The difference between those two things is the difference between Casey being occasionally available and Casey being genuinely free.
The Positive
  • Having Analia Hoyer as GM is a meaningful head start — most firms at this stage have the owner playing the GM role themselves, so the structural foundation for delegation already exists
  • Specialized roles across 10 staff members (financial specialist, lead paralegal, case manager) indicate the firm has moved beyond the generalist phase where everyone does everything
The Gap
  • A 10-person team without formalized accountability systems is a team that scales Casey's involvement rather than replacing it — every new client adds complexity that eventually flows back to him
  • Two-location firms that lack defined management protocols often experience inconsistency in client experience between offices — a reputation risk for a firm that has earned 28 awards and 139 reviews based on service quality
  • Without a clear transition of authority from Casey to Analia in documented form, the firm's dependence on Casey's decisions does not change even as the team grows
The team Casey has assembled is genuinely rare — but until Analia's GM role is backed by a formal accountability structure, Casey is still the operating system running everything, and that is what stands between him and the freedom he has earned.

Profit Plan Assessment

Hoyer Law has the revenue base and team infrastructure of a substantial firm — but without a clear profit plan in place, two locations and 10 employees can generate strong top-line revenue while leaving the owner with less personal income than the numbers suggest. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No confirmed visibility into profitability by case type, cost per acquired client, or monthly owner distributions — without these numbers, it is impossible to know which work is making the firm money and which is consuming it
  • A Financial Specialist (Randy Seager) is on staff, suggesting financial work is happening — but a staff member processing transactions is not the same as a strategic profit plan that guides decisions about which cases to take, what to charge, and where to allocate resources
  • Two-location overhead with 10 employees creates a significant fixed cost base — without knowing the firm's margin structure, adding more clients could increase costs faster than revenue
  • No stated revenue goal or profit target was available — without a defined destination, financial decisions are reactive rather than strategic
Blind Spot: Many family law firms discover that high-conflict, high-hourly-rate cases that feel profitable are actually less profitable than simpler matters when staff time, attorney hours, and client management costs are fully accounted for. Without tracking profitability by case type, Casey could be steering the firm toward its most time-consuming work rather than its most profitable.
The Positive
  • Randy Seager's dedicated Financial Specialist role indicates the firm has separated financial administration from legal work — a structural prerequisite for building a real profit plan
The Gap
  • Without a profit plan, revenue growth at a two-location firm with 10 employees typically produces more complexity before it produces more owner income — the margin per employee often stays flat or declines without active management
  • No confirmed cost-per-acquisition metric means marketing dollars are being spent without knowing how efficiently they convert to retained revenue — the highest-performing firms track this monthly
  • Casey's path to personal financial freedom — taking home more without working more — requires knowing exactly where the firm's profit is generated and protecting it as the firm scales
The profit plan is what ensures that every client Hoyer Law adds is building Casey's wealth rather than just adding to his workload — and that distinction is the difference between growing a business and growing a job.
