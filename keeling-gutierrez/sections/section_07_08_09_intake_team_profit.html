Intake Assessment

Keeling Gutierrez books 70 consultations per month and converts 30 — which means 40 warm, pre-qualified prospects walk away every single month with no follow-up attempt, no outreach, and no system to bring them back. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Intake calls are handled by paralegals with no defined script, no qualification criteria, and no consistency — conversion rates vary based on who happens to answer the phone
  • 40 prospects per month complete consultations but do not sign — and no follow-up attempt is made; once they leave, they are gone
  • After-hours calls route directly to the partners' personal cell phones — an unsustainable arrangement that places the partners personally on call for intake at all hours and loses leads when calls go unanswered
  • No intake tracking in place — there is no visibility into where prospects come from, how long it takes to book a consultation, or why the 40 monthly non-converters decided not to sign
Blind Spot: The 40 prospects who consult but do not sign are the warmest leads the firm has — they already chose to reach out, already had a conversation, and may only need a follow-up call or two to convert. Research shows that most firms never follow up after a non-signing consultation. A three-touch follow-up sequence within 72 hours could realistically recover 10–15 of those 40 leads every month without any additional ad spend at all.
The Positive
  • 70 consultations per month is a meaningful volume — the firm's marketing is generating real interest, and the gap is in converting that interest to signed cases, not in generating the interest itself
  • A 43% initial close rate (30 of 70 consultations signed) is a workable foundation — with a defined script, structured follow-up, and intake accountability, that rate can be improved systematically
The Gap
  • 40 warm consultations per month with no follow-up represents approximately $120,000 in potential monthly revenue that is walking out the door — that is $1.44 million per year in cases the firm is generating interest for but not capturing
  • After-hours lead loss is unmeasured but real — bankruptcy is a stress-driven decision that prospects often make late at night or on weekends; every call that goes to a partner's cell phone and isn't answered is a lead who calls the next firm on their list
  • Without intake tracking, there is no baseline to improve against — the firm cannot know if it gets better or worse, or whether any process change actually moved the conversion rate
Every consultation that leaves without signing is not a missed sale — it is a future referral, a future repeat client, and a future Google review that never happened. Fixing intake is the fastest path to more from the same effort.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • A dedicated contact page and a /free-bankruptcy-consultation-houston-tx/ landing page are confirmed present — prospects who find the firm online have a path to submit their information, but the position and prominence of these forms relative to the page fold could not be verified due to site access limitations
  • No live chat was observed on the website — in a practice area where prospects are researching debt relief options across multiple sites simultaneously, the absence of an instant-response option means the firm may be losing prospects who want an answer before they are ready to call
  • With no intake tracking system in place, form submissions and phone leads from the website are not being attributed to their source — the firm cannot know whether its Google Ads, LSA spend, or organic rankings are producing the web leads it receives

Team Assessment

Keeling Gutierrez has seven attorneys and dedicated support staff — the people are in place, but without defined roles, intake-specific accountability, or an operations function, the partners remain the backstop for nearly every critical function in the firm. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No dedicated intake function exists — paralegals handle calls as part of a broader paralegal role, without a script, without performance targets, and without any accountability metrics tied to consultation-to-case conversion
  • After-hours coverage routes to the partners' personal cell phones — meaning Kenneth Keeling and Yolanda Gutierrez are personally handling intake overflow at all hours, which is neither scalable nor sustainable
  • No KPIs or accountability structure is in place for the team — there is no defined standard for call handling quality, no monthly case targets by attorney, and no visibility into individual performance that would allow the partners to coach or course-correct
  • No operations or marketing function exists within the firm — every gap in those areas, from citation cleanup to ad oversight, defaults back to the partners' personal attention
Blind Spot: A seven-attorney firm can look operationally mature from the outside while still functioning like a solo practice on the inside. Having headcount is not the same as having a team that can run without the partners. The difference is systems, scripts, and accountability structures — and right now, none of those exist in a formal way at Keeling Gutierrez.
The Positive
  • Seven attorneys is the right scale to support significant growth — the headcount exists; the gap is in defining roles clearly enough that each person can perform at the level the firm needs without constant partner involvement
  • Two Spanish-fluent attorneys is a differentiator that no agency can replicate — it positions the firm to serve Houston's 45% Hispanic population in a way that most competitors cannot, but it requires a structured campaign and intake process to leverage it at scale
The Gap
  • Without a dedicated intake coordinator, every improvement in lead generation will still funnel through an unscripted, untracked process — capping the return on every marketing dollar the firm spends regardless of how well the ads perform
  • As long as the partners absorb after-hours calls and operational gaps personally, their time is not their own — and the firm's growth ceiling is set by the partners' available bandwidth, not by the market
  • Without KPIs, the team has no way to improve systematically — there is no data on what good intake performance looks like, no targets to hit, and no mechanism to identify underperformance before it costs the firm cases
The firm Yolanda and Ken built has the people to become untouchable — what it does not yet have is the structure that allows those people to win without the partners in the middle of everything.

Profit Plan Assessment

The firm generates approximately $3 million per year and has been flat long enough that Yolanda described it as stagnant — and the combination of untracked ad spend, a case mix weighted toward the least staff-efficient case type, and a competitor tripling its monthly filings makes the profit trajectory more concerning than the number alone. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Revenue has been stagnant at approximately $3M per year despite ongoing ad spend — the firm is spending to stand still, and any disruption to its referral base (which represents over 50% of revenue) could tip the trajectory downward
  • No attribution on the $6–7k/month Google budget — without knowing cost per consultation and cost per signed case by channel, there is no factual basis for deciding where to invest more or where to cut back
  • Chapter 7 cases — the firm's most profitable per staff-hour — represent only 10–15 of the 30 monthly cases currently being signed; Chapter 13 cases requiring 3–5 years of ongoing staff management represent the majority of the current case mix
  • A competitor (Allmand Law) tripled its monthly filings in 90 days — from 30 to 90 cases per month — while the firm's volume held flat, which strongly suggests Allmand is capturing a material share of the same market the firm operates in
Blind Spot: Chapter 13 cases generate $9–14k in nominal fee revenue — but that revenue arrives over 3–5 years of ongoing case management, while staff time and overhead costs continue throughout. Chapter 7 cases generate $3k upfront and close in weeks. At scale, a firm running significantly more Chapter 7 cases is often more profitable per staff-hour than one with the same nominal revenue in Chapter 13s. The firm's planned Glade AI integration is specifically designed to accelerate Chapter 7 processing — which makes the shift to a higher Chapter 7 case mix not just strategic but operationally achievable faster than it would be without the tool.
The Positive
  • $3M in annual revenue from a single-location firm with a 40-year reputation is a meaningful foundation — the market trust is there, and it does not need to be built from zero
  • Yolanda's clarity about Chapter 7 profitability — and the firm's plan to use Glade AI to scale it — shows the strategic awareness is present; the gap is in execution, not understanding
The Gap
  • Without tracking cost per case by channel, every budget decision is a guess — the firm cannot know whether its current ad spend is generating cases at $200 each or $2,000 each, and it cannot make a rational decision about how much to increase spend based on results it cannot see
  • Revenue flat at $3M while a competitor triples its case volume in 90 days is not a coincidence — it is a signal that market share is being actively taken before the firm has the systems to defend or reclaim it
  • Without a deliberate profit plan, reaching the $6M revenue goal will require doubling effort and headcount — unless the case mix shifts toward higher-margin Chapter 7 cases and the intake conversion rate improves simultaneously
The 40-year firm Yolanda and Ken built deserves a profit trajectory that matches its reputation — predictable, measurable, and not dependent on whether referrals keep coming in the way they always have.
