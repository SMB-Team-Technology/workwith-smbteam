Intake Assessment

Shingleton Law has a remarkably high conversion rate on initial consultations — but that conversion rate lives entirely in Adam's hands, and it cannot survive the volume or geographic scale the firm is building toward. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
"Adam Shingleton will handle initial client consultations to maintain a high ~90% conversion rate. Assistants will manage post-consult admin."
The Problem
  • Every initial consultation currently runs through Adam — his ~90% close rate is exceptional but it is a single point of failure, not a scalable system; when volume increases, Adam becomes the bottleneck
  • No defined follow-up sequence exists for leads who inquire but do not immediately book a consultation — these prospects are leaving without any structured attempt to re-engage them
  • The contact form is located at /contact-5, a sub-page URL that suggests the primary intake touchpoint may not be prominently placed on the homepage — visitors with intent may be leaving before finding it
  • No confirmed after-hours intake coverage — calls and form submissions received outside business hours have no documented response protocol, and in probate, timing often matters to the executor
Blind Spot: A 90% close rate on consultations Adam personally runs is the firm's most valuable current asset — but it is also the clearest signal that intake has not been documented or taught. When volume grows through paid advertising, Adam cannot physically handle all consults, and without a written consultation framework, any delegate will close at a fraction of that rate.
The Positive
  • A ~90% consultation-to-client conversion rate is exceptional by any benchmark — the fundamentals of Adam's intake approach are working and worth documenting as the basis for a scalable process
  • Post-consultation admin has already been delegated to assistants — the firm is thinking in terms of process delegation, even if the highest-leverage step (the initial consult) has not yet been addressed
The Gap
  • Every inquiry that does not receive a structured follow-up sequence is a case the firm has already paid to generate — without a 3-step email and text follow-up, an estimated 20–30% of non-converting inquiries are being abandoned rather than nurtured to a booking
  • Statewide expansion requires intake to work without Adam — Wilmington, Raleigh, and Charlotte markets cannot be served at scale if Adam must personally handle every initial consultation from Hampstead
  • The inability to delegate consultations is the single biggest constraint on how fast the firm can grow — not marketing, not the tech platform, not the team — intake is the ceiling
The firm Adam wants to build — one that runs without him — cannot exist if he is personally required to convert every single lead that comes in the door.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The contact form is confirmed present at /contact-5 — but the deeper URL path suggests it is not the first element a visitor encounters on the homepage; a booking CTA above the fold would reduce the number of steps between intent and action
  • No live chat or chatbot is present on the website — after-hours visitors who want to ask a quick question or schedule a consultation have no option other than submitting a form and waiting
  • Virtual consultations are prominently marketed as an option (in-office, remote, or at your location) — this is a genuine strength that reduces friction for out-of-county prospects and should be prominently featured near the booking CTA

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

Shingleton Law has more team structure than a typical solo practice — a tech co-founder who has already built the efficiency platform, and assistants who handle post-consult admin — but every decision that drives revenue still runs through Adam. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
"Adam Shingleton will handle initial client consultations to maintain a high ~90% conversion rate."
The Problem
  • There is no intake coordinator — the role that would allow Adam to step back from initial consultations and trust that conversion rates are maintained does not exist yet
  • No documented SOPs for intake, case onboarding, or client communication — without written processes, every new hire requires Adam's personal time to train, and every departure takes institutional knowledge with it
  • Geographic expansion into Wilmington, Raleigh, and other NC markets is part of the stated 12-month plan — but expansion into new markets without a defined team structure and documented playbook will require Adam's direct involvement in each new location
  • The tech platform dramatically reduces per-case admin time — but the efficiency gains are currently only available to people Adam has personally trained, not to a scalable team that can be built and replicated across markets
Blind Spot: The proprietary tech platform Zach has built is not just an efficiency tool — it is the key differentiator that makes high-volume, low-value probate cases profitable at scale. But the platform's value is only realized if the team using it is trained, documented, and scalable. Right now, the tool exists but the operating system around it does not.
The Positive
  • Having a dedicated tech co-founder (Zach Milne) who has built proprietary workflow automation is a structural advantage that most solo practitioners do not have — this is the infrastructure foundation for a truly self-managing firm
  • Post-consult admin is already being delegated to assistants — the mindset of building support roles is present, even if the org chart is still thin
The Gap
  • Every week Adam spends handling consultations he could have delegated is time he could have spent building the systems and oversight structure that allow statewide expansion — the cost is not just time, it is delayed growth
  • Statewide expansion as planned within 12 months requires a functioning intake coordinator, documented SOPs, and a training playbook before the first new market launches — without those, each new market adds work rather than reducing it
  • The $10M revenue goal Adam has stated requires a team that generates cases and serves clients without his direct involvement — that team does not exist yet, and it will not build itself
The time freedom Adam wants requires a team that runs without him — and that team is built from documented processes, trained people, and clear accountability, not from adding more volume to a system still built around one person.

Profit Plan Assessment

Shingleton Law is in the early stage of a deliberate revenue transition — phasing out estate planning and pivoting to probate-only — and that transition creates real short-term financial uncertainty that makes having a clear profit model more important, not less. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Annual revenue is approximately $125,000 — a level that supports a solo attorney but does not provide the financial cushion to absorb the cost of marketing investment, team expansion, and multi-market launches simultaneously without a clear cash flow model
  • The practice area transition from estate planning to probate-only means that the revenue mix Adam has built over the past year will shift materially — but no model has been described for how that transition is phased, what the revenue floor is during the transition, and when probate volume is sufficient to retire estate planning completely
  • While the technology platform reduces time per case, no financial tracking model has been described for case acquisition cost, profit per case, or return on marketing investment — growth without this data means decisions about ad spend, team hires, and expansion timing are made on intuition rather than numbers
  • The path from $125K to $10M requires clear milestone triggers — what revenue level requires the first hire, what level unlocks a second market, what profit margin is required before scaling — and none of those milestones have been mapped against the firm's current cost structure
Blind Spot: The technology platform means Shingleton Law can handle probate cases that other firms turn away (small estates under $10K in assets) at a profit. But without tracking the actual time cost and case profit by type, Adam cannot prove this to himself or to future investors, lenders, or team members — and he cannot make confident decisions about which cases to prioritize at scale.
The Positive
  • Adam has a clear average case value in mind — $3,000 to $7,000 per probate case — and a stated close rate of ~90%, which means the inputs for a case volume model already exist and the math to a $250K milestone is straightforward
  • The firm's tech-enabled efficiency (5-hour workflow reduced to 30–45 minutes) creates genuinely higher margins per hour than traditional probate practices — the profit advantage is real, it just has not been quantified yet
The Gap
  • Without a probate-only revenue model showing exactly how many cases at $5,000 average are needed to reach $250K, $500K, and $1M, Adam is making growth decisions without a dashboard — which makes it harder to know when to hire, when to expand, and when the current marketing investment is producing the expected return
  • Investing in marketing at $2,397/month requires confidence that the case volume and revenue projections will materialize — a profit plan with clear milestones and early warning indicators is what turns that investment from a leap of faith into a calculated decision
  • The statewide expansion goal within 12 months carries significant cost: physical offices, additional GBP setups, local ad spend, and new hires in each market — without a cash flow model, the risk is investing in expansion before the current market is generating the revenue needed to fund it profitably
The self-managing, statewide firm Adam wants to build is built on numbers — knowing what each case costs to acquire, what it earns, and what milestone triggers the next phase of growth — and that starts with building the profit model now, before scaling amplifies both the gains and the gaps.
