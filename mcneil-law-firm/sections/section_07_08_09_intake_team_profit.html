Intake Assessment

McNeil Law Firm is generating 75–125 leads per month and converting 26% of those who qualify — but there is no dedicated intake staff, no defined follow-up sequence, and no after-hours coverage to capture the prospects who cannot reach anyone during business hours. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No dedicated intake staff — leads arriving by phone or contact form are handled by operations and bookkeeping staff who are not structured or trained for lead conversion
  • No after-hours coverage — the website has no mechanism to capture evening and weekend inquiries; voicemail without a defined follow-up protocol means after-hours leads often call a competitor before morning
  • No attribution at intake — because the firm cannot identify which leads came from paid ads versus organic, there is no way to optimize or even measure intake conversion by lead source
  • Contact form buried below the fold on the homepage — reducing form submission volume before prospects even reach the intake step
Blind Spot: At a 26% close rate, 74% of qualified leads are choosing another firm or not signing — and research shows that responding to an inquiry within 5 minutes versus 1 hour increases close rate by over 21×. Small improvements to speed-to-lead alone, without any additional marketing spend, could add multiple cases per month.
The Positive
  • 26% close rate on qualified leads indicates that when the firm does engage prospects in a consultation, it converts at a reasonable rate — the intake process itself is functional, even if it is not systematized
  • A team of four provides enough staffing capacity to delegate intake responsibilities without adding headcount
The Gap
  • At 38–63 qualified leads per month and a 26% close rate, 28–46 qualified prospects are choosing another firm each month — at a blended case value of approximately $4,100, that represents $115,000–$189,000 in monthly uncaptured revenue the firm already paid to generate
  • Without a defined follow-up sequence for prospects who do not sign on the first contact, cases that require multiple touchpoints are being abandoned prematurely — especially common in contested divorce and custody matters where the decision timeline is longer
  • No process for lead capture outside business hours creates a systematic revenue leak that compounds every week — the family law prospect who calls at 8pm on a Tuesday and gets voicemail will often call the next firm on their Google results list before 9am the next morning
A stronger intake system is the highest-ROI investment the firm can make right now — not because more leads are needed, but because the firm is already generating leads it is not fully converting, and every unconverted qualified prospect is revenue the system left behind.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact form is located below the fold on the homepage — the primary digital intake tool is not visible without scrolling, reducing form submission volume for visitors who arrive, do not see an obvious action, and leave
  • No live chat or chatbot present — family law prospects frequently research late in the evening during emotionally difficult moments; no real-time response option means those prospects leave without any contact information captured
  • No automated contact form acknowledgment or response protocol confirmed — prospects who submit a form may not receive a response until the next business day, creating a response gap that competitors with faster follow-up will close before the firm ever calls back

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

McNeil Law Firm has a team of four — but John McNeil is the sole attorney, and every dollar of revenue the firm generates requires his direct legal involvement. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • John McNeil is the only attorney — every client matter, every hearing, every brief, and every negotiation requires his direct involvement; case volume cannot increase without his hours increasing proportionally
  • A paralegal hire has been identified as necessary but has not yet been made — without this hire, John cannot delegate case preparation, client communication, or document management, meaning he remains the bottleneck on every matter
  • Julia spans operations and bookkeeping across two demanding functions — neither role is receiving dedicated focus, which creates gaps as the firm scales and both functions grow in complexity
  • No management layer exists — no defined org structure, no accountability framework, and no clear delineation between who handles what when John is focused on legal work
Blind Spot: The firm needs at least one additional attorney to hit its $1.5M revenue goal, but hiring attorneys before the marketing system generates consistent, predictable case volume is a significant risk — the right sequence is build the pipeline first, then hire into the demand that system creates.
The Positive
  • Julia Eckard has operational ownership of bookkeeping and firm administration — there is an existing structure that, with the right systems, can grow into a stronger operations function
  • The support team of three (Julia, Melissa, Shawntel) provides a foundation to build a defined intake and operations process without immediately adding headcount
The Gap
  • The $1.5M revenue goal requires approximately 30 new clients per month at a blended $4,100 case value — at current capacity with one attorney, that volume is not achievable without either a paralegal freeing John's time or an additional attorney sharing the caseload
  • Every time John McNeil takes time off or steps away from the firm — even for a week — revenue generation stops entirely; there is no production redundancy and no safety net if he is unavailable
  • Without a defined org structure and accountability framework, adding staff creates confusion rather than leverage — hiring before building the systems to manage new hires is a common reason scaling efforts stall
The path to financial predictability runs directly through building a team structure where John McNeil is not the only person who can produce — because a firm that requires the owner for every outcome can only grow as fast as the owner can work.

Profit Plan Assessment

McNeil Law Firm is running below prior-year revenue — down approximately 30% to $720,000 — with no financial projections, no formal budget, and a manual bookkeeping system that cannot provide the visibility needed to manage slow seasons proactively or make confident investment decisions. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Revenue has declined approximately 30% year-over-year — from just over $1M to approximately $720,000 — with no formal analysis of what drove the decline or a documented plan to reverse it
  • Julia handles all bookkeeping manually with software described as inadequate for projections — no budget, no monthly P&L review, no cost-per-acquisition tracking by lead source or practice area
  • No financial forecasting model exists to anticipate slow seasons — the 6-week holiday slow period John identified is a known, recurring problem with no proactive plan to build pipeline before it arrives
  • Marketing spend on Google PPC and LSAs has no attribution — the firm does not know whether its current ad budget is generating a positive ROI, a negative ROI, or breaking even
Blind Spot: Without knowing cost per acquisition by lead source, it is entirely possible that the firm is spending more to acquire certain case types via paid ads than those cases generate in revenue — a scenario where increasing the marketing budget accelerates the revenue decline rather than reversing it. Attribution is not a marketing tool; it is a financial control.
The Positive
  • Julia has identified the bookkeeping gap and is actively evaluating replacement software — awareness of the problem and a replacement project in progress are the first step toward financial visibility
The Gap
  • Without a monthly P&L review and cost-per-acquisition data, hiring decisions — including the paralegal and potential additional attorney John identified — are being made without knowing whether the firm's current cash flow can absorb them
  • The 6-week holiday slow season is a predictable problem that has no plan — knowing the slow season is coming is not the same as building the pipeline in October and November to carry the firm through it
  • No financial visibility means John McNeil cannot answer the question that determines every major business decision: if I invest $X in marketing or a new hire, what does this firm look like in 90 days?
Financial certainty — knowing the pipeline is full six months out, knowing payroll is covered, knowing the slow season has been planned for — is not possible without a financial visibility system; it is exactly what building one unlocks.
