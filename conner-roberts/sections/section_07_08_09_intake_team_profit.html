Intake Assessment

With 40 to 50 new cases per month coming in, Conner & Roberts is already generating significant client volume — but there is no defined intake process operating independently of partner involvement, and the website's contact form is buried below the fold where high-intent visitors have to work to find it. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • The homepage CTA links to a separate contact page where the form is positioned below the fold — high-intent visitors who arrive ready to act must navigate away from the homepage and scroll down before they can reach it, and many won't
  • No after-hours intake capability observed on the website — prospective clients in the middle of a custody or divorce crisis who visit evenings or weekends have no way to start a conversation, and the first firm to call them back wins the case
  • Partners personally handling billing escalations and complex administrative questions — each question that reaches Amelia or Lisa costs a partner-hour that should be spent on client work or firm building
  • No defined follow-up sequence described for prospects who do not sign immediately — in family law, emotional urgency means the attorney who responds first and follows up consistently gets the client, regardless of credentials
Blind Spot: Every hour a prospect waits for a callback is an hour they spend looking at competitors' websites and profiles. Family law decisions are emotionally driven — and the attorney who responds first often gets the case, regardless of firm size or reputation. After-hours inquiries that go unanswered are not just missed leads; they are cases that call another firm by morning.
The Positive
  • The firm is handling 40 to 50 new cases per month, which confirms that an intake function is operating and converting at meaningful volume — the foundation is there, it just needs structure and automation to scale without partner involvement
The Gap
  • Every after-hours website visitor who cannot reach a human or an automated response is a family law case that calls a competitor by morning — a direct and recurring revenue loss the firm cannot see because it never makes contact
  • Partner-level involvement in intake and billing questions means the firm's highest-cost resource is applied to tasks that trained staff or automated systems should handle — reducing the hours available for case work and firm building
  • Without a defined follow-up sequence, an unknown portion of leads who showed interest but did not sign immediately are never contacted again — and in a firm generating this volume, recoverable revenue is being walked away from every month
Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The homepage CTA "Request a Free Initial Consultation" links to /contact-us/ — requiring visitors to navigate away from the page they landed on before they can submit their information, adding unnecessary friction at the highest-intent moment
  • No automated intake flow or follow-up confirmation is visible — prospects who submit a form have no immediate response confirming their inquiry was received and setting expectations for next steps
  • The gap between a website visit and a signed retainer is where family law prospects most often abandon and call a competitor who makes it easier to start — reducing that gap is the fastest intake improvement available

Team Assessment

Conner & Roberts has 10 people — 5 attorneys and 5 support staff — but both founding partners are still doing the work of first-line management: personally handling collections, reviewing documents, and fielding questions that should be resolved well below the partner level. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Lisa Conner working 60+ hours per week and taking work home nightly — a sustained pace that confirms the firm has structurally outgrown its current operational model
  • Lisa manually reviewing all uncontested divorce documents — a repeatable, pattern-driven task consuming partner-level time and cost that should be handled by trained staff or automated systems
  • Lisa spending 10 to 20 hours per week on manual retainer collections — a billing process with no automation or delegation in place, applied to the firm's highest-cost resource
  • No operational leadership structure identified — both partners filling the gap between case responsibilities and management responsibilities with no manager, director, or COO in place
Blind Spot: The decision to defer a Fractional COO reflects confidence in the partners' leadership capacity — and that confidence is likely warranted. The risk is that "I can manage this myself" is exactly what keeps both attorneys personally involved in day-to-day operations for the next five years. The same dynamic that has made the firm impossible to step back from so far will continue until something structurally changes.
The Positive
  • Five support staff alongside five attorneys is a meaningful team structure — the people exist to support a delegation-first approach, they just need defined roles, clear accountability, and the right technology to reduce partner dependency
The Gap
  • Every partner-hour spent on collections, document review, or administrative escalations is an hour not spent growing the firm as an asset — and Amelia and Lisa cannot build something they can eventually sell if they cannot step back from it
  • Without a defined management layer, each new case adds to partner workload rather than distributing through a trained team — growth at this pace currently makes the problem worse, not better
  • A 10-year vision of a sellable firm asset requires demonstrating that the firm runs without its founders — no buyer values a firm whose key person dependencies include both founding partners
Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

Conner & Roberts grew from $2M+ in 2025 to a projected $3M+ in 2026, driven by a case volume that would be impressive for any family law firm of this size — but the revenue growth has not been matched by the financial infrastructure to manage, plan, and protect what the firm generates. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"Build a scalable, sellable firm asset over 10 years, allowing partners to transition from practicing attorneys to business owners."
The Problem
  • No CFO-level financial oversight or dedicated finance role identified — at $3M projected for 2026, the firm needs more than bookkeeping to manage cash flow, partner draws, growth investment, and profit planning
  • Lisa spending 10 to 20 hours per week on manual retainer collections — partner time being consumed by revenue recovery rather than revenue generation or firm building
  • Billing inquiry escalations reaching partner level — no staff-level resolution process or billing accountability structure in place, meaning complex questions consume partner hours that could be spent elsewhere
  • No described KPI tracking or financial dashboard for monitoring acquisition cost per channel, revenue per attorney, or margin trends — the firm is growing by feel rather than by plan
Blind Spot: At $7,500 to $10,000 per case and 40 to 50 new cases per month, the firm is potentially generating $300,000 to $500,000 in new monthly billings — but without tracking acquisition cost by channel or margin by case type, the partners cannot know which cases are profitable and which are being subsidized by partner time. Some of the highest-volume case types may be the least profitable per attorney hour.
The Positive
  • Revenue of $2M+ in 2025 and $1.6M already collected by June 30, 2026 — a trajectory toward $3M+ that reflects strong market demand and real case volume, not accounting tricks
  • An average case value of $7,500 to $10,000 is at the high end for family law — positioning the firm to generate meaningful margin per case once operations are systematized and partner time is no longer the bottleneck
The Gap
  • Without financial visibility by practice area or case type, the partners cannot make informed decisions about where to invest marketing dollars, which services to grow, or which case types to prioritize — the firm grows by feel rather than by data
  • Retainer collections absorbing 10 to 20 partner hours per week is both a cash flow drain and a partner time drain — money that is owed is not being recovered efficiently, and the cost of recovery is too high
  • No profit plan or target partner income described — at this revenue level, without deliberate profit planning, growth creates complexity faster than it creates wealth, and the partners' personal financial position does not keep pace with the firm's revenue
Building a sellable firm asset over 10 years requires more than growing revenue — it requires knowing your numbers, protecting your margin, and demonstrating to a future buyer that the firm's profit is predictable and planned.
