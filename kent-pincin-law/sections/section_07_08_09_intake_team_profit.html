Intake Assessment

Kent Pincin Law is turning away qualified referral volume today — not because the leads aren't there, but because Michael Kent is the entire intake team. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
"The firm cannot accept live transfers from a referral source due to lack of capacity."
The Problem
  • Michael Kent personally reviews 40-50 cases and handles roughly 90% of intake every month — with no delegation in place
  • Employment cases require attorney-led intake, which currently blocks any handoff to non-lawyer staff
  • The firm has had to turn away live transfers from a referral source due to lack of capacity — a direct, stated loss of qualified volume
  • No case-assignment workflow exists to route incoming referrals as the attorney team grows
Blind Spot: You may not realize that every referral you turn away is not neutral — it is volume a competitor with more intake capacity is now capturing instead, on a case that was pre-screened and ready to sign.
The Positive
  • A 26% close rate on the cases Michael does review shows the intake judgment itself is strong — the constraint is capacity, not quality
  • 90% of leads already arrive pre-screened through referral relationships, meaning the raw material for a scaled intake process already exists
The Gap
  • Turned-away live transfers represent lost signed cases and lost revenue every single month this continues
  • Without a defined intake workflow, growth in attorney headcount cannot translate into growth in case volume
  • Attorney-led intake caps how much volume the firm can ever process, regardless of how many leads exist
Fixing intake is the single fastest path to Michael and Emily becoming pure CEOs rather than the firm's de facto intake department — every case that gets processed without Michael personally touching it is a step toward that freedom.

Team Assessment

Kent Pincin Law has tripled its attorney headcount in a year, but the two founding partners are still the ones holding IT, case assignment, and intake together personally. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
"Pure CEOs, managing managers."
The Problem
  • Team grew from 2 to 6 attorneys in one year, with two more hires planned — but no manager layer was added alongside that growth
  • Michael Kent handles IT, case assignment, and intake personally rather than delegating to dedicated staff
  • Emily Pincin manages litigation and accounting directly, with no operational leader in between her and the case teams
  • No dedicated ops, marketing, or intake team member exists anywhere in the firm today
Blind Spot: The fact that the firm has scaled this far on founder effort alone is actually the risk — it means every future hire adds more for Michael and Emily to personally manage instead of less, unless a delegation layer goes in now.
The Positive
  • Tripling headcount in a single year shows real ability to attract and retain attorney talent
  • Clear division of labor already exists between the two partners (case acquisition/intake vs. litigation/accounting), which gives a natural foundation to build formal delegation on top of
The Gap
  • Owner dependency at this scale means neither partner can take meaningful time away from the firm without operations stalling
  • Continuing to hire attorneys without adding operational leadership will make the bottleneck worse, not better, as the firm approaches 12-15 attorneys
  • No accountability structure exists to catch problems before they reach the partners directly
Becoming "pure CEOs managing managers" starts with putting a dedicated operational leader between the partners and the day-to-day — without it, 12-15 attorneys means 12-15 attorneys' worth of problems landing on Michael and Emily's desks.

Profit Plan Assessment

Kent Pincin Law is generating strong monthly revenue, but rapid team growth has driven overhead up faster than partner take-home pay. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"High overhead from growth means partner compensation is lower than that of some attorneys."
The Problem
  • Monthly revenue ranges $200K-$900K, averaging $400K-$500K — but overhead from rapid hiring is compressing what partners actually take home
  • Partner compensation currently trails what some individual attorneys at the firm earn, by the partners' own account
  • No dedicated financial or operational oversight exists to manage costs as the firm scales toward 12-15 attorneys
  • No specific margin figures were shared, meaning the partners themselves may lack full visibility into where overhead is going
Blind Spot: Growing revenue while personal take-home shrinks is a warning sign that is easy to miss when the topline number keeps climbing — without a dedicated eye on overhead, adding more attorneys could keep making this worse even as the firm looks more successful from the outside.
The Positive
  • Revenue itself is substantial and growing, giving the firm real room to invest in fixing the underlying overhead problem
  • The partners are already aware of and naming the compensation compression, rather than being blindsided by it — that awareness is a strong starting point
The Gap
  • Without dedicated oversight, overhead growth could continue to outpace revenue growth as headcount doubles again
  • Partner compensation trailing associate attorneys directly threatens the $5M+/year personal distribution goal each partner has set for the next 3-5 years
  • No clear plan currently connects the firm's growth trajectory to the partners' personal income targets
Getting overhead under control is what turns firm growth into personal wealth for Michael and Emily — without it, scaling to 12-15 attorneys risks becoming more work for the same, or even less, personal payoff.
