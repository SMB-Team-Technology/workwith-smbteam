Intake Assessment

Grey Lex is generating real leads through PPC and SEO, but half of them are never contacted again after the initial call — the firm is paying to acquire leads it is not converting. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
"The other 50% get no follow-up. Vendor CRM exists but unused. Wants someone to qualify, schedule, and follow up."
The Problem
  • 50% of all inbound leads receive no follow-up contact after the initial call — half of every marketing dollar spent is generating a lead that is then abandoned
  • Clay personally handles all consultations; when he is in a courtroom or working a legal file, new clients cannot be closed — the two functions cannot happen simultaneously
  • No-show rescue does not exist — when a prospect misses a scheduled consultation, there is no automated re-engagement sequence to recover the appointment
  • The Town Square Media CRM is in place but largely unused; there is no drip sequence, no lead scoring, and no pipeline visibility to know which leads are still active
Blind Spot: Clay's close rate is approximately 50% when he personally engages — which is excellent. But that number masks how many leads never reach him at all. The real firm-wide close rate across all inbound leads (including the 50% that get no follow-up) is likely closer to 25% or less. The problem is not Clay's ability to close — it is how few leads make it to a conversation with him.
The Positive
  • Clay's personal close rate of ~50% when engaged is well above the industry average — there is a proven conversion engine here; it just needs to be supported with a team to feed it
The Gap
  • At an average Ch. 7 case value of $2,500 and a 50% close rate, each uncontacted lead represents approximately $1,250 in lost revenue — with no follow-up system, the firm is losing thousands of dollars per week to leads it already paid for
  • No after-hours or weekend coverage means prospects who search on Friday evening or Saturday morning have already called a competitor by Monday
  • Without a systematic intake process, Clay cannot hand off the consultation function even when the right person is hired — there is no defined process to train them on
Clay told us he wants to stop being the bottleneck between legal work and sales work — but that is exactly what broken intake makes him. Fixing intake is the first step to building a firm that runs without him.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The consultation request form is positioned above the fold on the homepage — a strong setup that removes friction for high-intent visitors ready to take action
  • No live chat widget is present on the website — prospects who arrive with questions outside business hours have no way to engage and are likely leaving without converting
  • There is no confirmation or follow-up automation connected to the web form — a prospect who submits a request at 9 PM may not hear from anyone until the next business day, by which time they may have already scheduled with a competitor

Team Assessment

Grey Lex has two dedicated staff members and a clear vision for where the firm needs to go — but Clay is still personally responsible for every critical decision, every client consultation, every legal filing, and every business development activity. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
"Clay is the closer and main lawyer; sales work prevents legal work and vice versa."
The Problem
  • Clay is simultaneously the firm's primary attorney and its only closer — neither function can scale without cloning him, and each one actively blocks the other
  • No management layer exists; Clay is managing a paralegal, a case manager, and all business functions with no operational infrastructure or accountability system
  • No hiring playbooks, no onboarding documentation, and no defined roles beyond informal task assignments — adding a VA or a Ch. 13 attorney requires Clay to design the entire system from scratch
  • Clay is also personally handling bookkeeping in QuickBooks — administrative work is consuming time that should be going toward high-value legal and business development activity
Blind Spot: Clay's strength as a salesperson and his deep involvement in every client relationship are genuine advantages — but they are also what is preventing him from building the firm he described. The same qualities that make him excellent at Stage 4 are the qualities he has to learn to delegate at Stage 5 and 6. Recognizing this is what separates owners who scale from those who stay stuck.
The Positive
  • Clay has already hired two staff members and built a functional case workflow using Jubilee for bankruptcy — there is a real foundation here, not a blank slate
  • His MBA background and self-described management ambition mean he understands business systems conceptually — what is missing is the operational infrastructure, not the mindset
The Gap
  • As long as Clay is the only person who can close a client, every hour he spends in court or on a legal file is a lost sale — the firm's revenue ceiling is physically limited by one person's calendar
  • Without a documented intake and management process, adding a VA or a new attorney will not solve the problem — they will need Clay to train them, supervise them, and make every decision for them
  • No accountability system means Clay cannot step back even if he wanted to — there is no way to know if things are running without him checking
The firm Clay described — statewide, staffed, systematized — requires Clay to spend his time running the business, not doing the work of the business. Building that team structure is not optional; it is the entire path to $1M.

Profit Plan Assessment

Grey Lex is generating real revenue with strong unit economics — Ch. 7 cases at $2,500 average and property damage cases at $10,000–$20,000 each — but Clay is personally doing the bookkeeping, has no dashboards or KPIs, and is turning away entire case categories worth thousands of dollars each. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"One extra case covers it."
The Problem
  • Clay is doing his own bookkeeping in QuickBooks — no financial analyst, no dashboard, and no way to know in real time what the firm costs to run, what it costs to acquire a client, or what each practice area actually contributes to profit
  • No KPIs or financial metrics in place; revenue growth is being pursued without clarity on margins, overhead ratios, or profitability targets
  • All Chapter 13 cases are being declined — court-set fees of approximately $5,000 per case represent revenue that is walking out the door simply because the capacity to handle it has not been built
  • No recurring revenue or retainer model exists; every month starts from zero, with no predictable base of income to forecast from
Blind Spot: Clay's instinct that "one extra case covers" a new cost is a natural and intuitive way to think about overhead — but it is not how a $1M firm makes financial decisions. Without knowing the actual cost per acquired client, the margin per case type, and the contribution of each practice area, it is impossible to know whether growing faster is making the firm more profitable or just more busy.
The Positive
  • Clay's unit economics are strong: Ch. 7 cases average $2,500 with a range up to $8,500, and property damage cases yield $10,000–$20,000 in contingency fees — these are high-margin practice areas with real upside
  • Overhead is exceptionally low — both office locations secured at approximately $100/month total through creative arrangements, giving Clay unusually strong margins at his current revenue level
The Gap
  • Without tracking cost per lead and cost per case by practice area, Clay cannot know whether his PPC spend on bankruptcy is producing a better return than property damage, or whether he should shift budget between channels
  • Declining all Ch. 13 cases at ~$5,000 per case is a structural revenue gap — even adding a part-time or contract Ch. 13 attorney to handle those referrals could add $5,000–$15,000 in monthly revenue with minimal incremental overhead
  • No financial model exists for the $500k–$1M growth target — without knowing exactly how many cases at what value are needed, and what it will cost to generate them, the goal is a wish rather than a plan
Clay wants to build something worth owning and worth scaling — and that requires knowing the numbers behind the business, not just the revenue on the surface.
