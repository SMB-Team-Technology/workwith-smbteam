Intake Assessment

Roughly 20% of the firm's 260–300 weekly inbound calls go unanswered — meaning one in five potential clients gets no response at all. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • About 20% of 260–300 weekly calls go unanswered — hundreds of potential clients a month get no response
  • The website's own contact form is buried at the bottom of the homepage, adding friction for the leads who do try to reach you online
  • No after-hours coverage was described — calls outside business hours have no clear path to a live response
  • With referrals as the near-exclusive client source, every unanswered call is a paid-for lead going to waste
Blind Spot: Because most current clients arrive through the Shannara referral relationship, an unanswered call may feel like a smaller loss than it is — but every referral costs 50% of the fee, which means a missed call is a fully paid-for lead walking away for nothing.
The Gap
  • At 20% of 260–300 weekly calls going unanswered, roughly 52–60 potential client conversations are lost every week — before any new marketing spend is even considered
  • A buried contact form compounds the call-answer problem by adding a second point of drop-off for prospects who try to reach the firm online instead
  • With case economics already thin under the referral model, losing calls the firm didn't pay a referral fee for is the most direct path to recovering margin
Every unanswered call is revenue that never had a chance to become a case — and fixing that costs nothing in new marketing spend, only better process.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • The "Get a Free Case Evaluation" form sits at the bottom of the homepage, not above the fold, where most visitors will see it first
  • No live chat was observed on the site — prospects browsing after hours have no immediate way to engage
  • The CTA phone number appears twice in the header navigation, which is a strong signal — but it only helps if the calls that come in are consistently answered

Team Assessment

David Grimmett leads a lean team of one associate, one EA, one paralegal, and one VA — a support structure built around him rather than one that lets him step back. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • The current team — one associate, one EA, one paralegal, one VA — is sized for today's caseload, not the 10-lawyer firm the goal describes
  • No case managers or department leads were described who could absorb day-to-day decisions without David
  • Growth to 10 lawyers within 3–5 years requires a leadership layer that does not yet exist
  • With 80% of the 156-case load being low-value work, much of the team's time may be consumed by cases that do not move the firm toward its goals
Blind Spot: Because the team is small and each person's role is clear today, it may not be obvious how much further delegation has to go before David can add lawyers five, six, and seven without personally training and overseeing each one.
The Positive
  • A support team already exists across intake, case management, and administrative work — Grimmett Law Firm is not starting from zero on team-building
The Gap
  • Going from a 5-person team to a 10-lawyer firm requires a management structure that has not yet been built
  • Without delegated leadership, adding lawyers means adding to David's personal oversight load rather than reducing it
  • The gap between today's team and the stated 10-lawyer goal is a structural one, not just a hiring one
Scaling to 10 lawyers and $10M means building a team that can run cases and make decisions without David in every room — that is what makes dominating this market sustainable rather than exhausting.

Profit Plan Assessment

Grimmett Law Firm's annual revenue is roughly $900,000 and described as "on track" toward a $10M goal — but with attorney's fees of $2,500–$5,000 running against referral costs of $3,000–$4,000 per case, growth under the current model may not translate into more profit. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Case economics are slim to negative per case: $2,500–$5,000 attorney's fee against a $3,000–$4,000 referral cost paid to Shannara
  • 156 active cases, 80% of them low-value — a caseload weighted toward the least profitable work
  • The stated goal of a 15% profit margin has not yet been mapped against a concrete financial plan
  • Revenue growth alone ($900K today toward a $10M goal) does not address whether each additional dollar of revenue converts to profit under the current referral cost structure
Blind Spot: A firm can be "on track" on revenue and still be moving away from its profit goal — if the mix of cases stays 80% low-value under a referral model that costs more than some cases are worth, more revenue can mean more work without more take-home profit.
The Gap
  • Without a plan to reduce dependence on the referral relationship, growing revenue may simply grow the total dollar amount paid out in referral fees
  • No stated visibility into cost per case type makes it difficult to know which of the three practice areas (Personal Injury, Bad Faith, Child Custody) is actually the most profitable
  • The 15% profit margin goal will remain aspirational until case mix and referral dependency are directly addressed
Hitting $10M with a real 15% margin means fixing what each case actually nets the firm — not just growing the top line under the same referral-cost structure.
