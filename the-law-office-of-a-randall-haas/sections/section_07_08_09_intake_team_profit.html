Intake Assessment

The Law Office of A. Randall Haas has no confirmed intake system beyond a phone number and a contact form buried on a separate page — in a practice area where clients are calling under extreme stress, often after hours, and ready to hire the first attorney who responds with confidence. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Contact form is only accessible on the /contact/ page — not on the homepage, not on practice area pages, and not above the fold where first-time visitors decide whether to stay or leave
  • Phone is the only real-time intake channel — there is no callback widget, no text/SMS opt-in, and no mechanism to capture a lead who is unwilling to call at that moment
  • No confirmed after-hours coverage — criminal defense clients frequently need help urgently outside of business hours, and a call that goes to voicemail at 11 PM is a case that will be hired by whoever picks up first
  • No defined follow-up sequence for leads who submit the contact form or leave a voicemail — without a system, follow-up is inconsistent and cases are lost before they are ever worked
Blind Spot: Criminal defense clients are often calling on behalf of a family member who has just been arrested — they are emotional, urgent, and comparing multiple firms simultaneously. The firm that responds first with empathy and confidence wins the case. Without an after-hours intake solution, every call that comes in outside of business hours is a direct gift to a competitor.
The Positive
  • Gladys (the attorney's assistant, named positively in multiple client reviews) represents the beginning of an intake presence — clients have noted her helpfulness, indicating she is capable of playing a more structured intake role
The Gap
  • In criminal defense, the average case value ranges from $3,500 for a DUI to $50,000+ for serious felony or federal work — a single missed intake due to slow follow-up or no after-hours coverage can represent a five-figure loss from one unanswered call
  • Without a structured intake process, it is impossible to know how many leads the firm is already generating and losing — the leak is invisible, which means it cannot be fixed
  • As marketing investment increases and lead volume grows, a broken intake system will amplify losses rather than capture gains — every dollar spent on ads is partially wasted without a reliable process to convert those leads
A systematic intake process is what transforms Randall's reputation into consistent, predictable revenue — and it is the difference between a practice that works for him versus one that keeps him working.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact form exists on the /contact/ page only — fields include Full Name, Email, Phone, Legal Matter (dropdown), How Did You Hear About Us, and CAPTCHA; the form is functional but inaccessible to the majority of site visitors who do not navigate to that page
  • No chat widget, callback tool, or text/SMS opt-in is present on any page — the firm offers no way for a visitor to initiate contact other than calling the phone number or navigating to the contact page
  • Homepage has a prominent phone number and a "Contact A. Randall Haas today for a consultation" CTA — this is a strength, but without a backup capture mechanism, any visitor who is not ready to call immediately is effectively lost

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

The Law Office of A. Randall Haas is a two-person operation — the attorney and one support staff member — which means every case, every client call, and every operational decision runs through Randall personally. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • The firm cannot operate without the attorney present — there is no structure in place that would allow cases to be managed, clients to be served, or intake to run independently
  • One support staff member (Gladys) provides administrative support, but there is no evidence of documented processes, role clarity, or delegated authority that would allow her to manage the firm in Randall's absence
  • The firm's revenue capacity is hard-capped at what one attorney can personally handle — adding clients beyond that threshold means longer hours, not more income
  • No accountability structure, no performance tracking, and no scalable operations exist — the firm is operationally identical to a solo practice from day one, regardless of how long it has been running
Blind Spot: After 27 years of building and running the practice solo, the current structure likely feels natural and functional — because it has been working. But "working" means Randall is the firm. The moment he wants to step back, travel, or reduce his hours, revenue stops. The blind spot is that what feels like independence is actually the deepest form of owner-dependency: the firm cannot exist without him, and that will never change without intentionally building the team and systems to replace his role.
The Positive
  • Gladys is named positively in multiple client reviews — she has built rapport with clients and has the relational skills to grow into a more structured intake and operations role with the right coaching
The Gap
  • Every week Randall spends on intake calls, administrative tasks, and operational decisions is time taken away from high-value legal work — the gap between billable hours and operational hours is a direct cost to the firm's profitability
  • Without a self-managing team, any revenue growth from marketing investment creates more work for one person — the ceiling on the firm's income is the ceiling on Randall's availability
  • Rossen Law Firm went from small practice to 9 offices and 5 attorneys by building a team and systems — the gap between their operation and this one is not reputation, it is infrastructure
Building a team that runs the firm without Randall is not about stepping back from what he loves — it is about creating the freedom to choose how he spends every hour, and the security of knowing the practice will thrive whether he is there or not.

Profit Plan Assessment

Financial visibility was not discussed during this audit, and no data on revenue, margins, or take-home pay is available from the discovery process — which itself is a signal that profit planning is not a structured part of how the firm operates today. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No financial visibility data was available from this audit — revenue, margins, cost per case, and take-home pay are unknown without a structured financial tracking system
  • Solo practice economics often create a disconnect between top-line revenue and actual take-home: overhead, case costs, and time allocation can erode margins significantly without visibility into where money is going
  • Without knowing cost per case type, it is impossible to identify which practice areas are most profitable — and which are consuming the most time for the least return
  • No financial forecast or profit plan means growth decisions (adding staff, increasing ad spend, expanding service areas) are made without understanding their impact on net income
Blind Spot: In solo and small firm criminal defense practices, federal and felony cases often carry the highest fees but also the highest time cost. Without tracking profitability by case type, the firm could be taking on lower-fee, high-volume cases that consume the majority of billable time while leaving high-value specialty work under-served. Knowing which cases are worth taking — and which should be referred — is a profit decision, not just a practice preference.
The Gap
  • Working harder without a profit plan is the most common way solo practitioners increase revenue without increasing wealth — the absence of financial infrastructure means Randall cannot know whether this year's effort is creating a better life or just more activity
  • As marketing investment increases and client volume grows, the absence of a profit plan will mean more revenue flows in without a clear picture of what stays — scaling without financial clarity accelerates costs faster than it accelerates wealth
  • A 39-year practitioner approaching the stage of his career where legacy and financial security matter most needs a profit plan that transforms current revenue into long-term wealth — not just a practice that pays the bills
Knowing exactly what the firm earns, what it keeps, and how to grow both is the foundation of working smarter — and the difference between a practice that funds a life and one that consumes it.
