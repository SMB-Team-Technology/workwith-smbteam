Intake Assessment

The Spagnola Law Firm is generating leads through LSA and SEO, but nearly all of them are being lost before they become paying clients — because there is no intake system, no intake team, and no process beyond Samuel answering when he can. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED — Critical Priority
"LSA: ~21 leads/2 weeks with 1 consult booked"
The Problem
  • Samuel personally handles every intake inquiry — there is no intake coordinator, no delegation, no process running independently of him
  • 1 consult booked from 21 LSA leads in two weeks represents approximately a 5% LSA-to-consult conversion rate; an optimized intake process with professional follow-up should convert at 20–30%
  • Answering Legal handles overflow calls but cannot close paid consultation bookings — it takes messages, nothing more; leads who call after hours and reach the answering service are not being converted
  • No contact form on the website means there is no intake pathway for the majority of website visitors who are not ready to call — a critical silent loss of leads that never even enter the funnel
Blind Spot: The 5% conversion rate feels like a lead quality problem, but it is almost certainly a process problem. Family law leads from LSA are high-intent — they searched for an attorney, clicked the Google Screened listing, and called. When they do not convert to a consult, it is usually because the follow-up was slow, the booking process was unclear, or the value of a paid consultation was not communicated. None of those are fixed by better ads — they are fixed by a better intake process.
The Positive
  • Samuel is already aware of the intake gap and is actively researching solutions — calls with Smith.ai and the SMB Attorney Assistant VA are scheduled for June 25, which shows he understands this is the next lever to pull
The Gap
  • At a 5% conversion rate on 42 LSA leads per month, the firm is booking approximately 2 consults/month from $3,000 in LSA spend — that is a $1,500 cost per consult; if conversion improves to 20%, that same $3,000 generates 8 consults with an identical budget
  • Every missed consult is a potential $8,000 contested case that went to Kreider, Robertson, or Woodruff instead
  • Without a trained intake coordinator, adding more lead volume through Google PPC or Meta ads will not produce proportional results — the existing conversion gap will simply apply to a larger pool of wasted leads
Intake is the fastest path to more revenue from existing spend — and the first thing that must be in place before Samuel can step back from day-to-day operations and let someone else run the front door of his firm.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • No contact form exists anywhere on the triadlaw.com website — the only call to action is a phone number; this is confirmed in the research and means any after-hours or hesitant visitor has no way to engage
  • No live chat or chat widget is present on the site — visitors who have questions but are not ready to call leave without any engagement and are unlikely to return
  • The absence of a web form means no leads are being captured passively — all lead generation is dependent on a prospect taking the high-friction action of calling during business hours

These findings are also referenced in the Website Audit section.

Team Assessment

The Spagnola Law Firm is one person deep — Samuel is the attorney, the intake coordinator, the relationship manager, and the business owner, all at once — and there is no redundancy, no delegation, and no way for the firm to function in his absence. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED — Critical Priority
The Problem
  • Samuel is the only attorney — if he gets sick, takes a vacation, or has a family emergency, every active client matter is affected and no new cases can be opened
  • One legal assistant (Heather Elungon) is the entire support staff; there is no intake coordinator, no operations manager, and no one with authority to handle decisions in Samuel's absence
  • The current structure creates a hard ceiling on revenue — even with better marketing and a fixed intake process, there is only so much caseload one attorney can carry
  • Samuel is aware of this constraint and considering hiring an attorney and an intake VA, but no hire has been made — the firm is still fully dependent on him today
Blind Spot: The most dangerous form of team dependency is the kind that has worked so far. Because Samuel has successfully operated as a solo practitioner for over 35 years, the risk of that model has never been forced into plain view. But one prolonged illness, one difficult personal period, or one sudden surge in caseload demand exposes exactly how fragile this structure is — and there is no recovery plan.
The Positive
  • Samuel has already identified the two hires he needs — an additional attorney and an intake coordinator VA — and has calls scheduled with vendors to move on this; the awareness is there, and the direction is right
The Gap
  • Until an intake coordinator is in place, every new lead generated by better marketing must still be handled by Samuel — which means scaling marketing without scaling the team creates overload, not growth
  • Without a second attorney, the firm cannot absorb the caseload required to hit the 12-cases-per-month goal without Samuel working unsustainable hours
  • The $1 million target requires approximately three times the current case volume — that is not achievable by one attorney operating at maximum capacity; it requires a team
The firm Samuel is trying to build — one that runs and earns without requiring his constant presence — begins with the hires he has already identified. Coaching accelerates that process by giving him a proven framework for hiring, onboarding, and holding people accountable.

Profit Plan Assessment

Samuel knows his revenue ceiling ($400,000), his average case value ($8,000), and his minimum fee ($5,000 non-refundable) — but he does not have a clear picture of what each case costs to acquire, what his margins actually are, or a structured plan to break through the ceiling he has identified. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER — In Progress
"Currently ~400K annual ceiling"
The Problem
  • Revenue has hit a ceiling at $400,000 — but there is no financial analysis identifying exactly why the ceiling exists or which levers would most efficiently break through it
  • Spending $6,000–$7,000/month on Matador and LSA combined with an LSA conversion rate of approximately 5% — the actual cost per acquired case is unknown, which makes it impossible to evaluate whether current marketing spend is producing an acceptable return
  • No mention of a profit plan, financial tracking system, or margin analysis — revenue is known, but what remains after expenses and overhead is not being actively monitored against goals
  • The firm is at a stage where adding costs (new attorney hire, intake coordinator) will be necessary to grow — but without financial visibility, those investments can be harder to make with confidence
Blind Spot: At $400K in revenue with $6–7K/month in marketing spend, the firm is allocating approximately 18–21% of revenue to marketing alone — before accounting for staff, office overhead, and professional costs. Without a profit plan, it is difficult to know whether growing to $700K means more profit or just more expenses scaled proportionally. A clear financial model is what makes the difference between growing wealth and growing workload.
The Positive
  • Samuel has a well-defined case value model — $8,000 average, $5,000 minimum non-refundable on contested matters — which provides a clear basis for ROI calculations and financial planning
  • Revenue goals are specific and stated ($650–700K near term, $1M long term), which means the financial gap is quantifiable and a path to close it is buildable
The Gap
  • Without tracking cost per acquired case, it is impossible to know whether the $3,000/month LSA spend is a good investment or a money pit — that number needs to be calculated before any marketing budget decisions are made
  • The path from $400K to $700K requires approximately 37 additional cases per year (at $8K average) — that is a clear, achievable number, but only if the intake, team, and marketing systems are aligned to generate and close them
  • A profit plan that sets targets for revenue, overhead percentage, and owner compensation would give Samuel a framework for making the hiring and investment decisions he already knows he needs to make
Samuel's $1 million goal is not just a revenue target — it is the threshold at which the firm can afford the team and infrastructure that gives him back his time. Getting there requires knowing the numbers, not just the revenue.
