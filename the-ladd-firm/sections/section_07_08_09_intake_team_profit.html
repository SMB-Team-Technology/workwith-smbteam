Intake Assessment

The Ladd Firm is booking roughly 5 design meetings a week against a stated goal of 10, with no defined intake process described on the call. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No defined intake or follow-up process was described for leads coming from LSA, Google PPC, or the website
  • Website contact form sits below the fold — not visible without scrolling on the homepage
  • No live chat to catch visitors who are not ready to pick up the phone
  • Design meeting volume (~5/week) is running at roughly half the firm's stated goal of 10/week, despite active spend on lead generation
Blind Spot: Every design meeting the firm is not booking is a lead it already paid for through its $6,000/month ad spend — the gap between 5 and 10 meetings a week may be an intake problem wearing a lead generation costume.
The Gap
  • At $4,000 per average case, each design meeting that never gets booked or followed up on is real revenue walking to a competitor
  • Doubling meeting volume through better lead generation alone will not work if intake cannot convert the leads already coming in today
  • Without a defined process, growth depends on Banks personally staying on top of every lead — the opposite of the freedom he is working toward
Fixing intake means the firm captures more of the meetings it is already paying for — without Banks needing to personally chase a single one of them.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Contact form is located below the fold, with name, phone, email, and message fields
  • No live chat present to engage visitors who land after hours or are not ready to call
  • Strong above-the-fold phone CTA exists, but visitors who prefer not to call have no lower-commitment path to convert

Team Assessment

Banks has made real delegation moves — hiring a probate attorney in October and building out an operations and marketing support structure — but still needs roughly five hours a day for production work himself. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Banks personally needs approximately five hours a day for production work, on top of running the business
  • Marketing execution runs through a single outside contractor (Colin Bulger) covering website, SEO, PPC, and LSA — a lot of responsibility concentrated in one person with no internal backup
  • Has expressed concern about being overloaded by new initiatives, which has made him hesitant about adding more moving pieces even when they would help
  • Mary Carol Ladd (co-attorney) and the recently hired probate attorney give the firm case capacity, but no one has been described as owning marketing or growth strategy internally
Blind Spot: The hesitation about taking on more — the fear of being overloaded — is itself a sign the firm is not yet self-managing. A firm that runs itself would let Banks add initiatives without personally absorbing the extra load.
The Positive
  • Banks has already taken the hardest step for many owners — hiring a dedicated probate attorney to build out a second practice line rather than trying to handle it all himself
  • Summer Palmer and Leo Baker give the firm a starting operations and marketing structure most solo-built firms never establish
The Gap
  • Every hour Banks spends on production is an hour he is not spending on the strategic decisions that would let the firm run without him
  • A single external contractor managing four channels at once is a capacity ceiling on how fast lead generation can improve
  • Doubling design meeting volume without adding self-managing structure means doubling the load on an already-stretched owner
Until the firm can absorb new initiatives without Banks personally carrying the extra weight, more revenue will only mean more hours — not more freedom.

Profit Plan Assessment

The Ladd Firm is growing revenue and tracks its ad-cost-to-revenue ratio at a basic level, but has no stated profit plan beyond that. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
"~10% ad cost to revenue last month, approx $60K revenue on $6K spend"
The Problem
  • Revenue pace is growing (~$600K last year to ~$800K this year), but no profit margin or take-home figure was stated on the call
  • Average case value of $4,000 sits below the local market rate of roughly $5,000 — every case closed leaves money on the table relative to the market
  • Financial visibility appears limited to top-line revenue pace and a single ad-cost-to-revenue ratio, not a full profit plan
  • Doubling revenue toward the ~$1.6M working goal without addressing case value or cost structure could mean doubling the work without doubling what Banks actually keeps
Blind Spot: Knowing the ad-cost-to-revenue ratio is a good habit, but it is not the same as knowing overall profit margin — the two can move in opposite directions if the cost of running the practice grows as fast as revenue does.
The Positive
  • Banks already tracks at least one key financial ratio (ad cost to revenue) — most firms at this stage track nothing at all
The Gap
  • Without a full profit plan, growth from $800K toward the ~$1.6M goal could arrive without a proportional increase in what Banks personally takes home
  • A below-market average case value compounds across every new client the firm signs
  • No visibility into per-practice-area profitability (estate planning vs. probate) to guide where to invest further
A real profit plan is what turns a bigger firm into a more valuable one for Banks personally — not just a busier one.
