Intake Assessment

Kemp Injury Law already has real intake coverage in place — but with no way to measure what it's actually catching or losing. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: AMBER
"No formal system tracks total monthly lead volume."
The Problem
  • No formal system tracks total monthly lead volume — nobody can say how many leads are coming in or how many are being lost
  • Adam acknowledges some lead leakage each month, treated as secondary to the bigger problem of insufficient lead volume overall
  • No CRM was mentioned to sit behind the phone/answering-service setup and give visibility into what happens to each lead
  • With Adam treating the firm as a side hustle relative to his other businesses, intake oversight likely competes for his attention with casework and those primary businesses
Blind Spot: Without lead-volume tracking, Adam has no way to know whether the intake system he already built is working well or quietly losing prospects — the marketing rebuild will only pay off if the leads it generates are actually being counted and converted.
The Positive
  • A dedicated staff member carries a phone 24/7, backed by a lead case manager and a call answering service — real after-hours coverage most firms this size do not have
The Gap
  • Every unconverted lead right now is scarcer and more valuable, since overall volume has collapsed to zero — unmeasured leakage costs more than it used to
  • Without lead-volume tracking, Adam cannot tell whether the marketing rebuild is actually increasing signed cases or just increasing calls that still leak out
  • Manual, untracked handoffs between the answering service, the case manager, and casework staff are where leads are most likely lost
Fixing lead generation only matters if the leads it produces are actually converted — otherwise Adam is paying to generate cases he still has to chase personally.

Team Assessment

At 3 staff and 1.5 attorneys, Kemp Injury Law does not yet have the leadership layer Adam says he needs to reach 3 lawyers, 10 staff, and 30 cases a month. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
"Side hustle" — how Adam describes Kemp Injury Law relative to his primary development and construction businesses.
The Problem
  • Team is 3 staff and 1.5 attorneys — Adam plus one part-time attorney
  • No leadership layer (manager, ops lead) was mentioned to run the firm day-to-day
  • Adam's stated goal is 3 lawyers, 10 staff, and 30 cases/month within 3-5 years — roughly triple the current attorney count and more than triple the staff
  • Adam treats the firm as secondary to his primary development and construction businesses, yet remains its central operator
Blind Spot: Treating the firm as a side hustle does not make the day-to-day dependency any less real — until someone else can own intake, marketing oversight, and casework decisions, Adam's attention gets pulled back in no matter how he prioritizes it.
The Positive
  • A part-time attorney is already in place — a starting foundation toward the 3-lawyer team Adam is targeting
The Gap
  • Reaching 30 cases/month with only 1.5 attorneys and 3 staff is not operationally realistic — the team has to grow alongside lead generation
  • No accountability structure means growth adds more to Adam's plate, not less
  • Without a leadership layer, tripling case volume would triple Adam's day-to-day load instead of freeing him from it
Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

No revenue goal, average case value, or margin figures were shared on the call — Kemp Injury Law's growth target is defined in cases and headcount, not dollars. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No average case value was stated — a critical input for measuring return on any marketing spend
  • No revenue goal in dollar terms was given — growth is framed only as "3 lawyers, 10 staff, 30 cases/month"
  • No mention of current profit margins, cost per case, or cost per acquisition
  • An estimated ~$1M in annual revenue with no stated visibility into what portion converts to take-home profit
Blind Spot: Without a case value or cost-per-acquisition figure, Adam has no way to know whether tripling case volume actually triples his profit — or just triples his workload.
The Gap
  • Without a profit plan, hitting the 30 cases/month target says nothing about whether Adam is actually closer to stepping back
  • No visibility into which case types (MVA, motorcycle, rideshare, premises liability) are most profitable to prioritize as volume grows
  • Scaling the team to 10 staff without a financial plan risks growing overhead faster than profit
Stepping back only works if the firm is profitable enough to run without him — that requires a plan, not just a case count.
