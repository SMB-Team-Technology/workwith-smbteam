Intake Assessment

As a solo practitioner with no confirmed intake staff, all new client contact at this firm flows through Attorney Perkins-Mouton personally — meaning every call that comes in while she is in court, with a client, or unavailable after hours is a lead that has nowhere to go. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No dedicated intake staff observed — attorney personally handles all initial client inquiries, meaning intake capacity is directly limited by the attorney's available hours
  • No CRM or intake tracking system confirmed — without a system to log, follow up, and track prospects, leads that do not sign immediately are almost certainly being lost
  • No after-hours coverage visible — potential clients who call in the evening or on weekends reach a voicemail and are likely to call a competitor before the next business day
  • No defined intake process or follow-up sequence — if a prospect needs time to think or to gather funds, there is no system to stay in contact and convert them over days or weeks
Blind Spot: Studies consistently show that law firms lose 35–50% of inbound leads simply due to slow or missed follow-up. At a 15% default close rate, even a modest improvement in intake speed and follow-up consistency can add significantly more retained clients from the same number of leads — before any new marketing spend is added. The leads you are already receiving are being underconverted right now.
The Gap
  • Every missed call or slow response is a lost client — in personal injury, where a single contingency case can be worth $5,000–$25,000, even one missed inquiry per week represents substantial annual revenue walking out the door to a competitor who answered faster
  • Without a CRM, there is no ability to measure conversion rate, identify which lead sources are working, or follow up systematically — the firm is flying blind on one of its most important revenue levers
  • As paid advertising comes online and lead volume increases, an unstructured intake process will become an increasingly costly bottleneck — the time to build intake infrastructure is before the leads start coming, not after
A structured intake system means that when the firm's marketing starts working, every lead that comes in has a real chance of becoming a client — and the attorney's time is protected for the work that only she can do.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • Website is currently inaccessible to visitors due to the expired SSL certificate — contact forms, if present, cannot be reached by prospective clients; this is a complete barrier to web-generated lead capture
  • No live chat tool or chatbot was observed in any directory listing or cached site reference — after-hours web leads have no path to the firm
  • Once the website is rebuilt and secured, adding a prominent contact form above the fold and a clear call-to-action on every practice area page is essential to converting the traffic that paid advertising will generate

Team Assessment

Attorney Perkins-Mouton has built and sustained a solo practice for 29 years — an extraordinary accomplishment that speaks to her skill and determination — but the solo structure means the firm's capacity, income, and community reach are entirely bounded by one person's available hours. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • The firm operates entirely through the attorney — there is no team member who can handle intake, manage cases, or make operational decisions independently
  • No management layer exists — every client interaction, every court filing, every business decision flows through one person with no delegation possible
  • There is no accountability structure or documented process that would allow a new hire to step into a role without the attorney training and supervising them personally
  • The firm cannot take on meaningfully more cases without the attorney working more hours — there is no leverage, and growth directly increases personal workload
Blind Spot: A solo practice that grows without a team does not create freedom — it creates a higher-revenue version of the same constraint. Every new client added without support staff means less time for the existing clients, less time for the community work that drives this practice's identity, and no path to the kind of stability that lets the attorney take a vacation, be sick, or step back without the firm grinding to a halt.
The Positive
  • Twenty-nine years of practice means the attorney has deep institutional knowledge that can be systematized — documented processes, training materials, and intake scripts built from her experience become the foundation of a scalable team
The Gap
  • Without a team, the attorney is the bottleneck for every function in the firm — lead generation improvements, intake investments, and marketing spend all have their ROI limited by how much the attorney can personally handle
  • The personal brand ("The Queen of Justice") is a marketing asset, but a brand built entirely around one person creates a firm that is unsaleable, unfundable, and unresilient — it cannot outlast her involvement without a team structure behind it
  • A competitor who adds a trained intake coordinator and a paralegal this year will be able to handle 2–3x more cases with the same attorney hours — the compounding advantage of team-building starts now, not later
Building a team that handles intake, case management, and operations is the structural change that turns a 29-year solo practice into a firm with real capacity — and gives the attorney the freedom to focus on the work, and the community, she actually cares about.

Profit Plan Assessment

A multi-practice firm spanning personal injury contingency work, flat-fee criminal defense, estate planning, and real estate transactions carries very different revenue timing, overhead structures, and profit margins across each service line — without a clear profit plan, it is impossible to know which practice areas are driving income and which are consuming the attorney's most valuable hours. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • No financial visibility data was available for this engagement — profit planning at the solo practitioner stage is often informal and reactive rather than structured and forward-looking
  • Revenue is entirely passive and referral-dependent — without a marketing engine, income is unpredictable month to month, making budgeting, planning, and investment decisions difficult
  • The mix of contingency (PI) and flat-fee matters creates income volatility — a month heavy on criminal and estate planning cases may have very different cash flow than one with PI settlements coming in
  • No confirmed tracking of cost per case type, time invested per matter, or profitability by practice area — without this data, the attorney may be her own most expensive employee on her least profitable cases
Blind Spot: Personal injury contingency cases look profitable in settlement months and invisible in the months between. If flat-fee matters are subsidizing the time invested in building PI cases, the real profit margin on PI may be lower than it appears. And if estate planning or real estate work is consuming attorney hours at rates below what criminal defense or PI would generate, a profit-by-practice-area breakdown could reveal a significant reallocation opportunity — without adding a single new case.
The Positive
  • Multiple practice areas means the firm has natural revenue diversification — if one area slows, others can carry the load; this is a structural resilience that single-practice firms do not have
The Gap
  • Without a profit plan, there is no way to know whether growing revenue is actually growing take-home income — adding more cases without understanding margins can mean more work with the same or lower personal earnings
  • No financial forecasting means the firm cannot plan investments (team hires, marketing spend, technology) against projected returns — every growth decision is made on intuition rather than numbers
  • As marketing comes online and lead volume increases, the absence of financial tracking will make it impossible to measure ROI and optimize spending — the firms that scale profitably do so because they know their numbers at every stage
A clear profit plan is what converts 29 years of hard work into financial security and community impact that is sustainable — knowing exactly what the firm earns, keeps, and is on track to produce is the foundation of the freedom and legacy Attorney Perkins-Mouton has earned.
