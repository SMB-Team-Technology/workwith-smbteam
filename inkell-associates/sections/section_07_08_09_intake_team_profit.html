Intake Assessment

The Inkell Firm has a real intake structure — overseas intake specialists, pre-litigation paralegals, and a demand/negotiation department — but the firm's own close rate sits near 50% against its stated 80% target. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating. This is where the biggest immediate ROI lives.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Current close rate is roughly 50%, well short of the firm's own 80% target
  • Sales scripts exist but are not consistently followed across the intake pipeline
  • Follow-up is inconsistent — an estimated one-third of qualified opportunities are mishandled somewhere between first contact and signed case
  • No independent accountability structure catches these gaps before they cost the firm a signed case — they surface only when Griffin personally notices them
Blind Spot: High lead volume can mask a conversion problem. With PPC and LSA both actively generating leads, it is easy to read "more leads" as the fix — when the real leak is that roughly one in three qualified opportunities never makes it to a signed case.
The Positive
  • A real intake structure already exists — three overseas intake specialists feeding three pre-litigation paralegal teams — so this is a coaching and accountability fix, not a build-from-scratch problem
The Gap
  • Closing the gap from 50% to even 65-70% would convert meaningfully more of the leads already being paid for — without spending another marketing dollar
  • Every mishandled opportunity is revenue the firm already generated and then lost to inconsistent follow-up
  • Without sales-script adherence and accountability tracking, intake performance will keep depending on how closely Griffin is watching in any given week
Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Team Assessment

Inkell & Associates has built a real team of 24, plus 2-3 outsourced staff, spanning intake, pre-litigation, negotiation, and litigation — but every one of those functions still routes back through Yuri Griffin as the single point of failure. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
"An autonomous firm where systems drive performance, allowing Griffin to focus on strategic work."
The Problem
  • All operational knowledge and problem-solving runs through Griffin — the firm cannot function independently for an extended period without him
  • There is no independent management layer holding the team accountable to its own targets
  • The litigation team is missing its monthly complaint-filing metric (target: 6/month) and is described as resistant to AI adoption
  • Pre-litigation paralegals are pushing back on 70-80 case caseloads despite a structured process designed to reduce that burden — a sign the process is not being reinforced by anyone besides Griffin
Blind Spot: Having built real delegation — intake specialists, paralegals, a negotiation department, a litigation team — can make it feel like the team problem is solved. It is not: delegation of tasks is not the same as an accountability structure that runs without Griffin personally overseeing it.
The Positive
  • The org structure itself is more mature than most firms this size — three intake specialists feeding three paralegal teams, a dedicated demand/negotiation department, and a separate litigation team are all already in place
The Gap
  • Every escalation, missed metric, and accountability gap still lands on Griffin personally, which is what keeps him from stepping back regardless of how much the team grows
  • Missed metrics (the 6/month litigation filing target) are surfacing without a management layer positioned to catch and correct them proactively
  • Team resistance to new processes (AI adoption, caseload structure) has nowhere to be resolved except through Griffin directly
Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Profit Plan Assessment

Inkell & Associates is growing revenue quickly — from $2.2M in 2025 to a projected $5M+ in 2026 — but that visibility currently lives entirely in a local dashboard that only Griffin maintains and reads. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: AMBER
The Problem
  • Financial visibility exists but is not shared — it lives in a local RStudio dashboard that Griffin builds and reads himself, inaccessible to the broader team
  • No firm-wide profit plan appears to track the strategic shift toward litigation cases, which the firm believes generate roughly double the revenue per case-day of pre-litigation work ($40.22 vs. $34.33)
  • Revenue is scaling faster than the operational systems supporting it, which typically compresses margin even as top-line numbers look strong
  • No profitability claims beyond the revenue-per-case-day comparison were made on the call — actual margin and per-case profitability were not discussed
Blind Spot: A dashboard that only the owner can read is not financial visibility for the firm — it is financial visibility for one person. If Griffin is out for even a week, no one else can answer "how are we actually doing?"
The Positive
  • Griffin is already tracking data at a granular level (revenue per case-day by practice type) — the instinct toward data-driven decisions is already present, it just is not yet shared or systemized
The Gap
  • Without shared financial visibility, no one besides Griffin can catch a margin problem before it becomes a revenue problem
  • The litigation-focused growth strategy is a real financial bet, and right now only one person in the firm can evaluate whether it is paying off
  • Revenue projected to more than double in a year (2025 to 2026) without a profit plan means the firm may be scaling costs just as fast as it scales revenue
A profit plan the whole leadership team can see — not just Griffin's personal dashboard — is what turns fast revenue growth into the kind of firm that can actually run without him.
