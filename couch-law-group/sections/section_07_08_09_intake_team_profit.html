Intake Assessment

Couch Law Group's intake process is entirely manual and entirely dependent on Jordan — a structure that works at three weeks of age but will become a hard ceiling on growth the moment lead volume increases. Fixing intake means converting more of the leads the firm is already generating — without spending another dollar on marketing.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Paralegal Vanessa collects initial information via a Microsoft Form, but Jordan personally reviews every intake and makes the final call on whether to take the case — he is the bottleneck at every step
  • Follow-up after initial contact is manual phone calls only — there are no automated email or SMS sequences in Clio to re-engage leads who do not respond immediately
  • No defined response time standard exists — leads who contact the firm outside business hours have no after-hours coverage and no automated acknowledgment
  • Vanessa's intake role has no scripts, no qualification criteria, and no KPIs — she is doing administrative work without a defined process to turn inquiries into signed clients
Blind Spot: Jordan's 80% close rate on qualified leads is exceptional — but the bottleneck is not conversion, it is qualification speed and lead volume. Every hour Jordan spends reviewing an intake form is an hour he is not billing. If paid ads bring in 10–15 leads per month, the current system will collapse under the volume unless Vanessa's role is defined and automated before advertising begins.
The Positive
  • Jordan's close rate of approximately 80% for qualified leads is significantly above the industry average — the conversion skill is there; the system around it is not
  • The firm already uses Clio as a CRM, which has native intake automation, workflow triggers, and follow-up tools that can be configured without purchasing additional software
The Gap
  • Without a defined intake workflow, every new lead that comes in from a paid ad or a Google search requires Jordan's personal time to process — at 15 leads per month, that is a second job on top of casework
  • Leads who do not receive a response within the first five minutes of contacting a firm are 21 times less likely to convert — a manual phone callback system cannot compete with that window
  • Jordan's goal of taking home $250k–$300k depends on the firm running cases, not Jordan running the phone — without intake delegation, revenue scales only as fast as Jordan does
A firm where Jordan can step back starts with an intake system that qualifies, follows up, and converts without Jordan being involved in every step — and that system can be built in Clio, right now, before ads even launch.

Website Contact Forms & Live Chat

  • No contact form or intake path was observable on the website during research — the site returned a 403 error; however, a conversion-optimized form above the fold is a critical missing component for any site receiving paid or organic traffic
  • Without a high-converting contact form, every visitor who does not pick up the phone is a lead lost permanently — and mobile users in particular will not scroll to find a buried contact page
  • Website intake and phone intake must connect to the same Clio workflow — right now there is no evidence that the website is feeding directly into any intake sequence

Team Assessment

Couch Law Group has three people — Jordan, Annie, and Vanessa — but no defined roles, no accountability structure, and no system that allows any of them to operate independently without Jordan's direct involvement. Until this changes, the owner cannot have more free time — no matter how much revenue grows.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • Vanessa is full-time but her role has no defined KPIs, no scorecard, and no documented responsibilities beyond using the Microsoft Form — she is a resource without a framework
  • Annie's role as co-founder is entirely undefined — there is no formal operational or administrative function assigned to her, which means significant leverage is sitting idle
  • No hiring plan exists — Jordan acknowledged that recruiting attorneys to the rural Western NC area is a known challenge, but there is no structured strategy for when to hire or what role to hire next
  • Jordan is simultaneously the lead attorney, intake reviewer, business developer, and operational decision-maker — every function of the firm runs through him personally
Blind Spot: The fact that the firm is only three weeks old makes the current team situation feel normal — but the habits built in the first 90 days become the operating model for years. Every week Jordan spends as the bottleneck for intake, admin, and casework is a week those patterns get harder to change. Building team structure now, before volume increases, is dramatically easier than rebuilding it after growth forces the issue.
The Positive
  • The firm has both a paralegal and a co-founder available — the raw materials for delegation are there; they just need defined roles and systems to activate them
  • Jordan's background in a structured Nashville firm environment means he understands what a functioning team looks like — the challenge is building that structure here, not learning what it is
The Gap
  • Without scorecards and KPIs for Vanessa, Jordan cannot delegate confidently — he will always second-guess or personally verify work that should be handled without him
  • Annie's undefined role means Jordan carries operational weight that another capable person is present and available to shoulder — that is leverage being left on the table every day
  • Every hiring decision made without a framework will slow the firm down — recruiting the wrong role or at the wrong time is expensive in both money and disruption in a three-person practice
Jordan came back to Western NC to build something in his community — not to become the full-time administrator of a firm that cannot run without him. The team structure that changes that is built with scorecards, defined roles, and a clear next hire — and it starts before the volume arrives, not after.

Profit Plan Assessment

Couch Law Group has no bookkeeper, no accountant, and — by Jordan's own description — no clear financial visibility of any kind. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Until there is a clear profit plan, growth may just mean more work without more wealth.

Status: RED
The Problem
  • No bookkeeper and no accountant — financial management does not exist in any structured form at this stage of the firm
  • The firm's previous bookkeeping relationship was terminated when the partner became the subject of an SEC fraud investigation — the transition left a gap in financial records and confidence
  • Jordan described financial visibility as "not clear at all" — there is no monthly P&L, no expense tracking, and no data on what the firm keeps versus what it spends
  • No profitability tracking by case type — Jordan does not currently know which cases generate the best margins, which practice areas are most efficient, or how to allocate his time toward highest-value work
Blind Spot: A take-home target of $250k–$300k per year is a specific and achievable goal — but only if the financial architecture is built to route money there deliberately. Without a profit plan, revenue growth often produces lifestyle inflation and higher expenses rather than higher take-home pay. The firms that hit their personal income targets are the ones that plan for profit first, not last.
The Gap
  • Without monthly financial reporting, Jordan cannot know whether the firm is on pace for its $300k Year 1 goal — or whether a course correction is needed before the end of the year
  • Decisions about hiring, ad spend, and equipment are being made without knowing the firm's true cost structure — that is financial risk in a firm that has been operating for three weeks
  • Investors and lenders — if ever needed — require clean financials. Building that infrastructure now, while the books are simple, is dramatically easier than reconstructing it after years of informality
The $250k–$300k personal take-home Jordan and Annie are targeting is a real number — but it is only achievable with a real financial system, clean books, and monthly visibility into what the firm earns, spends, and keeps.
